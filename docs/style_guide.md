# Schools of the Republic — Style Guide v1

This guide governs the editorial register and conventions of *Schools of the Republic* (the Library's historical synthesis), and by extension the per-state dossiers and the *Sacred Compact* white paper. Its purpose is to produce a book that reads the same whether one chapter is rewritten by Margaret on Tuesday morning, three chapters are rewritten by parallel AI sub-agents on Thursday afternoon, and a fourth is touched by a Library editor in 2031. Consistency is the deliverable. Without this guide, parallel work drifts; with it, the seams disappear.

## 1. Audience and register

The book is written for **the educated general reader** — a register Margaret characterized as "advanced high school or freshman college." Concretely, the book should be readable by:

- A school-board member preparing for a vote.
- A legislative aide drafting a one-pager for a state senator.
- A retired teacher with no legal training but an interest in school finance.
- A journalist on the education beat with no specialty in trust law.
- A trust-law scholar who needs the citations to be sound.

The challenge is the last constituency. The book has to be *readable* by the first four and *citable* by the fifth. This means short sentences carrying primary-source citations, not legalistic prose carrying easier ideas.

Margaret's working test: *"Whenever I present to the legislature, it has to be on the second-grade level. Anything past that was right over the head."* That is hyperbole about the audience but precise about the discipline. Aim for sentences a legislative aide could lift verbatim into a one-pager.

## 2. Reading-level metrics

Each chapter should hit, on average:

- **Sentence length: 18–22 words.** Current text frequently runs 25–35.
- **Maximum sentence length: 35 words.** Anything longer should be split unless the legal precision is genuinely lost in the split. Most can be split.
- **Flesch Reading Ease: 40–60** (roughly 12th-grade through freshman-college reading level). The median should land near 50. Current text often scores 30–45 (college-graduate level). Pandoc and most editors can compute Flesch on the fly; check during the rewrite, not after.
- **No sentence with three or more subordinate clauses** unless the third is a citation.

These are *averages and ceilings*, not hard rules per sentence. The point is the discipline, not the arithmetic.

## 3. Vocabulary precision

Three rules carry most of the weight, two of them from Margaret's May 5 review.

**3.1. Beneficiary = schools, not kids.** The legal beneficiary of the trust is the schools (the institutional recipient, then the schoolchildren the schools serve). The book has been sloppy in conflating the two. **In trust-law-precise contexts, the only correct word is "schools."** Children benefit from the policy; schools are the legal beneficiary. Where the prose wants to talk about the human stakes — current and future schoolchildren — say so explicitly and at the right point in the argument, but do not let "kids" or "children" stand in for "schools" as the legal-beneficiary word.

Examples:

- *Wrong:* "The trust exists to benefit kids."
- *Right:* "The trust exists to benefit the schools, which in turn educate the children of the state."
- *Wrong:* "Children are the beneficiaries of the Common School Fund."
- *Right:* "Schools are the legal beneficiaries of the Common School Fund. The fund's distributions reach children through the schools that teach them."

**3.2. The plotting decision was 1785, not 1775.** The Land Ordinance establishing the township grid system was 1785 (May 20, 1785). Earlier dates are wrong. The 1775 date appears in at least one place in the current text and must be corrected wherever it appears.

**3.3. Use full names on first use, short forms thereafter.** "Common School Fund" then "the Fund" (or "the CSF" only when context makes the abbreviation unambiguous). "Public Land Survey System" then "the PLSS." "Section 16" needs no abbreviation but its meaning ("one square mile out of each thirty-six-square-mile township, reserved for school support") needs to be made explicit on first use. "Bureau of Land Management" then "the BLM" or "the Bureau."

**3.4. Plain words over fancy ones; positive constructions over negative.** Two related rules carry most of the weight here.

*Plain over fancy.* Use the shorter, more common word when it does the job. *"Use"* not *"utilize."* *"Help"* not *"facilitate."* *"Start"* not *"commence."* *"End"* not *"terminate."* *"Before"* not *"prior to."* *"About"* not *"regarding"* or *"with respect to."* *"Show"* not *"demonstrate"* or *"illustrate."* *"Use"* not *"employ."* *"Get"* not *"obtain."* *"Buy"* not *"purchase."* *"Send"* not *"transmit."* The shorter word is almost always clearer; the longer word usually arrives because the writer wants to sound more formal. The book sounds formal because the substance is serious, not because the vocabulary is.

*Positive over negative.* When you can name what you want directly, do that — don't name what you want to avoid. *"Make things clear"* not *"avoid obfuscation."* *"Cite the source"* not *"avoid unsourced claims."* *"Keep it short"* not *"avoid verbosity."* *"Show the reasoning"* not *"don't hide the reasoning."* Negative constructions add a syllable, soften the verb, and force the reader to invert the meaning. The positive construction is shorter, sharper, and lands the instruction directly. Reserve negative constructions for cases where the negative is the point — when something is genuinely about avoidance, prohibition, or omission.

## 4. Voice

**Third person, sourced, evidentiary.** The book is a *library work*, not an advocacy piece. Sharper takes belong in the Voices section of the live site. The book reports, frames, and cites; it does not argue from the rostrum.

Specifically:

- **No first-person plural in the chapter prose.** "We will see" / "Let us consider" / "Our investigation" — strike them. The author's authority comes from the substance and the citations, not from a guiding voice.
- **No editorial verbs.** "Outrageous," "scandalous," "shocking," "appalling" — these belong in Voices, not in the book. Where the State has been credibly criticized, attribute the criticism to the source making it: *"State auditors found in 1894 that…"* not *"The State outrageously…"*
- **Modal restraint.** "Must" and "should" are advocacy verbs. The book uses them only in summarizing what a legal authority says: *"The Restatement of Trusts states that the trustee 'must…'"* — not *"The State must do X."*
- **No second person.** No "you" addressing the reader. The book is read by many; it speaks to none of them directly.

The exception is the litigation-impossibility chapter (BOOK-002), which is allowed a slightly more direct register because it has to make the structural argument the rest of the book rests on. Even there, advocacy verbs stay out.

## 5. Banned phrases — the AI-tells list

Every AI sub-agent that touches this book will be tempted by the following phrases. They are banned. Their presence in a chapter is grounds for revision regardless of what else the chapter does.

- *"It is important to note that…"*
- *"It is worth mentioning that…"*
- *"It should be noted that…"*
- *"Furthermore,"* / *"Moreover,"* / *"Additionally,"* as paragraph- or sentence-openers.
- *"In the realm of…"*
- *"Navigating the complexities of…"*
- *"Delve into…"*
- *"Plays a pivotal role in…"*
- *"Robust framework"* / *"comprehensive framework"* / *"holistic framework."*
- *"Multifaceted"*
- *"Tapestry of…"*
- *"Underscores"* and *"highlights"* used as filler-verbs (they're fine when they carry actual weight).
- *"Not merely X but Y"* — usually slack; cut "merely" or rewrite.
- *"In today's world,"* / *"in our modern era,"* / *"in the 21st century"* — write the actual time period.
- *"Crucial"* / *"vital"* / *"essential"* used as intensifiers. Use them only when something genuinely depends on the thing being crucial — and then prove it depends.
- *"Throughout history"* / *"since time immemorial"* — name the period.

Closing-platitude banned forms:
- *"Only time will tell."*
- *"The road ahead is long…"*
- *"The stakes could not be higher."*
- Any sentence whose function is to signal "we have arrived at the end of the section."

## 6. Sentence and paragraph structure

**Sentences.** Lead with the subject. State the verb early. Cite at the end. Most sentences should be readable aloud in a single breath without dropping the thread.

**Paragraphs.** One main idea per paragraph, ~4–8 sentences. The first sentence introduces the idea; the middle sentences develop it; the last sentence either lands a citation or sets up the next paragraph. Paragraphs longer than 8 sentences usually contain two ideas and should be split.

**Lists.** Use sparingly. Prose is the default. A list earns its place when (a) the items are genuinely parallel, (b) the count matters, or (c) the reader will want to scan rather than read. Most "lists of three" in current AI-drafted prose are ritual rhythm; rewrite them as prose.

**Em-dashes.** Use sparingly. They are useful for parenthetical asides that disrupt the sentence rhythm. Two per paragraph is too many; one per paragraph is often too many.

**Connective tissue.** Use *Then* and *But* and *And* and *So* and *Yet* as sentence-openers freely; English permits this and AI prose often forbids it. *However* as a sentence-opener is allowed but adds three syllables for no gain — *But* is usually better.

## 7. Citations and sources

The book's authority depends on its citations. The citations have to be sound. Margaret's central observation from the May 5 call: state-supreme-court trust decisions over the past 25 years quote derivative law-review summaries that often misframe the 1785 origins. The book cites primary sources directly so future opinions citing *Schools of the Republic* will be quoting sound primary authority.

**Hierarchy of preferred sources, in order:**

1. Primary legal source: the Land Ordinance of 1785; the Northwest Ordinance; specific Enabling Acts; the U.S. Constitution; state constitutions; the *Restatement (Third) of Trusts*; Bogert's *The Law of Trusts and Trustees*; the *Uniform Trust Code* (where adopted).
2. Primary case law: cited as *Wood v. Honeyman*, 178 Or. 484, 173 P.2d 56 (1946) — full first time, short form thereafter.
3. Primary historical record: agency annual reports, legislative committee reports, official correspondence of named figures (Jefferson's 1783–84 committee work, etc.).
4. Period newspapers (with image plates where possible).
5. Modern scholarly secondary sources (Souder & Fairfax 1996; Lincoln Institute / Sonoran Institute studies).
6. Law-review summaries — only when no direct primary or secondary source covers the point, and only with the underlying source flagged for follow-up.

**Citation-pending flags.** Where a claim is unsourced during drafting, mark it `[CITE PENDING]` in the prose. The pre-publication pass closes all `[CITE PENDING]` flags or removes the claim. Never let an unsourced claim ship.

**Quotation conventions.** Block-quote primary sources of substantial length (40+ words). Inline-quote shorter passages. Always identify the speaker or source in the lead-in; do not let a block quote float without attribution.

## 8. Headings and visual structure

- **Chapter titles:** sentence case (`Chapter 4: How the Section 16 reservation became a trust`), not Title Case (`Chapter 4: How The Section 16 Reservation Became A Trust`).
- **Section headings:** sentence case, no terminal punctuation.
- **Sub-section headings:** italics, sentence case, no terminal punctuation.
- **No emoji.** Anywhere. The book is a library work.
- **Image plates** (period newspapers, historical maps, signed declarations) carry captions in italics with full source attribution.

## 9. Date and number formatting

- **Dates:** "May 20, 1785" on first use in a chapter; thereafter "1785" or "May 1785" depending on what the prose needs.
- **Years used as adjectives:** "the 1859 Admission Act" — no comma, no apostrophe.
- **Numbers under 100:** spelled out unless they're acreages, dollar amounts, statutory section numbers, or specific reference points. *"thirty-two states"* not *"32 states"*. *"640 acres"* not *"six hundred forty acres"*.
- **Numbers 100 and above:** numerals. *"3,400,000 acres"* or *"3.4 million acres"* — pick one form per chapter and stick with it.
- **Money:** *"$76.8 million"* (dollar sign, no comma in the number when expressed in shortened form). *"$2,500,000,000"* or *"$2.5 billion"* — pick one form per chapter.
- **Percentages:** numerals plus the % symbol. *"3.5%"* not *"3.5 percent"*.

## 10. Eighth Anchor reframing — terminology consistency

Margaret reframed the Eighth Anchor on May 5: the *Library itself* is the Eighth Anchor, not the parcel-level public ledger Vision v2 imagined. The book uses Margaret's framing throughout. Earlier references to the Eighth Anchor as "the parcel ledger" or "the public ledger" are stale and must be revised. The Eighth Anchor in the book is the institutional infrastructure of watchfulness — the Library and its co-librarians and its constituency — not a future deliverable.

## 11. AI-assistance disclosure

The book carries a standing methodology disclosure in the front matter and at the end of the introduction: *"This book was drafted with substantial AI assistance under the editorial supervision of Dave Sullivan and Margaret Bird. Every claim has been reviewed by a named human editor before publication. The Library's complete editorial methodology is described at schooltrusts.net/about."* Sub-agent rewrites do not require their own disclosure; the book's standing disclosure covers them.

## 12. Before-and-after examples

The following pairs illustrate the register shift the rewrite pass aims to achieve.

**Example 1 — opening of a section.**

*Before:* *"It is important to note that the 1785 Land Ordinance, which has been described by historians as a watershed moment in American legal history, established a multifaceted framework for the disposition of public lands that, in its many dimensions, fundamentally shaped how the new nation would think about education funding for generations to come."* (47 words; one sentence; AI-tells: "It is important to note," "watershed moment," "multifaceted," "fundamentally shaped.")

*After:* *"The 1785 Land Ordinance set the structure that would shape American school funding for two centuries. Its central move was simple. Section 16 of every township — one square mile out of every thirty-six — was reserved 'for the maintenance of public schools.'"* (Three sentences; 41 words; concrete; quotes the source.)

**Example 2 — naming the beneficiary.**

*Before:* *"The Common School Fund exists to benefit the kids of Oregon, ensuring that future generations of children will have access to quality education."*

*After:* *"The Common School Fund's beneficiary is the Oregon public school system. The Fund supports schools; the schools educate the children of the state."*

**Example 3 — modal restraint.**

*Before:* *"The State must immediately disclose all relevant trust accounting records, as the schoolchildren of Oregon deserve nothing less than full transparency from their fiduciary."*

*After:* *"The Restatement of Trusts requires a trustee to provide 'full and accurate accounts' of trust administration. Plaintiffs in 24CV38372 contend the State has not yet met that standard, and the Motion to Compel filed May 2, 2026 puts the question to Judge Stone."*

**Example 4 — connective tissue and AI-tells.**

*Before:* *"Furthermore, it is worth noting that the doctrine of fiduciary loyalty plays a pivotal role in trust administration. Moreover, the Restatement underscores that trustees must navigate the complexities of multiple beneficiary interests with utmost care."*

*After:* *"The duty of loyalty is the spine of trust administration. The Restatement requires the trustee to act 'solely in the interest of the beneficiaries' — and where there are multiple beneficiaries, to deal with each impartially."*

## 13. Application discipline for parallel rewrite work

When parallel sub-agents apply this guide:

1. Each sub-agent reads this guide as its first action, before opening any chapter file.
2. Each sub-agent is given a specific file or chunk of files. No sub-agent edits a file outside its assignment.
3. Each sub-agent's output is committed to its own git branch and reviewed before merge. Cross-chapter consistency is verified at merge time, not before.
4. The orchestrator (Cowork-Claude or a Claude Code orchestrator session) runs the Flesch readability check on each chapter post-rewrite. Chapters scoring below 50 or above 60 trigger a re-pass.
5. Banned-phrase grep across the rewritten output: any banned phrase from §5 is grounds for re-pass.
6. Citation-pending flags are accumulated into a single closing-pass document. The pre-publication pass works through the flag list.

## Resolutions of v1-draft open questions (recorded for the change log)

The four open questions in the v1 draft were resolved by Dave on 2026-05-05 evening:

**Q1 (reading-level target).** Resolved at Flesch 40–60 — 12th grade through freshman college, median around 50. Slightly more sophisticated than the v1-draft's 50–60 band, which would have skewed below freshman-college. Reflected in §2.

**Q2 (modal restraint).** Resolved strict. Modal verbs ("must," "should") appear in chapter prose only when summarizing what a legal authority says ("the Restatement of Trusts requires…"). Advocacy modal verbs ("the State must…") stay out of the chapter prose; they belong in Voices. Reflected in §4.

**Q3 (first-person plural).** Resolved strict ban. No "we will see," "let us consider," "our investigation." The author's authority comes from substance and citation, not from a guiding voice. Reflected in §4.

**Q4 (ship v1 or wait for Margaret's pet peeves).** Resolved: ship v1.0 now. Pet peeves from Margaret and Dave will land in v1.1, v1.2, etc. as they emerge during the rewrite pass. The banned-phrase list (§5) is treated as living — append to it as needed.

**Additional rule from Dave** captured as §3.4: plain words over fancy ones (*"use"* not *"utilize"*); positive constructions over negative ones (*"make things clear"* not *"avoid obfuscation"*).

## Versioning protocol for this guide

This guide is itself a living document. As the rewrite pass surfaces new patterns to forbid or new patterns to encourage, the guide gets revised. Versioning convention:

- **v1.0** — current, approved 2026-05-05 evening.
- **v1.1, v1.2, etc.** — minor revisions (banned-phrase additions, vocabulary clarifications, example additions) without restructuring.
- **v2.0** — major restructuring or substantive policy change (e.g., changing the Flesch target, changing the modal-restraint policy).

Each version is snapshotted to `_archive/` before edits so the change history is recoverable. Sub-agents always read the current version at `docs/style_guide.md` in the live repo (the canonical location once promoted).
