# VI. The Coming Trusts: Designing for the AI Age

### The mapping is exact

The argument the school-trust evidence makes is general. Hyperbolic discounting is a property of human cognition, not of any specific institution. Long-running bureaucracies acquire their own continuity interest regardless of what they were chartered to do. Beneficiaries who cannot vote do not generate the political feedback that would otherwise correct drift, regardless of whether the beneficiaries are children in a Coos County township in 1885 or future-generation populations in a Stockholm climate compact in 2055.

The structures the AI moment is now constructing share, with the school trust, every one of these properties. They are designed to bind future generations to commitments made today. Their beneficiary populations are diffuse, unborn, or otherwise unable to defend the trust politically. They will be administered by bureaucracies that will, on the historical evidence, optimize for their own continuity over generational time. The cognitive bias of the people running them will, on the historical evidence, favor near-term institutional comfort over long-term beneficiary welfare.

What follows is, in operational terms, a translation exercise. The seven pillars of Section V apply to each of these structures with adjusted vocabulary. The standing-constituency requirement applies without adjustment.[^VI-1]

The mapping is also four-axis. Each AI-age structure has a characteristic primary vulnerability among the four faces of drift named at the close of Section IV. An AI Targeting Authority is most vulnerable to **purpose drift**: today's elite consensus on what counts as humanity's most pressing problems will shift, and the words "most pressing" will be filled with new content under the same nominal mandate. A perpetual climate trust is most vulnerable to **corpus drift**: carbon as the trust unit, atmospheric stability as the asset, may not survive the century as economically and scientifically meaningful frames; a trust whose corpus is locked to today's units of climate accounting will become obsolete when the units shift. A data trust is most vulnerable to **beneficiary drift**: the data subject's identity is itself unstable, given changes in privacy law, model architecture, and the rise of synthetic data — and the perimeter of "whose data is in the trust" can be redrawn faster than the trust's enforcement community can react. A sovereign wealth fund is most vulnerable to **legitimacy drift**: citizens-of-the-polity is a moving definition, and a fund's purpose is hostage to whichever generation's politics happens to define citizenship at any given moment. The pillars apply to all four structures. The architectural extensions named at the close of IV.F — adversarial compact review, asset-translation authority, beneficiary-class boundary provisions — apply differentially, axis by axis, to the structure's specific vulnerability.

### AI Targeting Authorities

Peter Diamandis and Alex Wissner-Gross, in *Solve Everything*, propose an AI Targeting Authority — a perpetual, autonomous body empowered to direct compute, capital, and human attention toward humanity's most pressing problems. The Authority would have its own resources, its own decision rules, its own legal personhood, and its own capacity to act across generations. Its operational concept, "Programmatic Down-Shifting," provides a method by which the Authority can reduce its own scope as conditions evolve.[^VI-2]

The proposal is, on the school-trust evidence, recognizably an aspirational structure with intergenerational beneficiaries. The pathology applies. The pillars apply.

Codified fiduciary duties (Pillar 1): the Authority's charter would have to declare, in terms equivalent to the 1910 New Mexico-Arizona "breach of trust" language, that diversion of the Authority's resources from beneficiary-mission purposes is a breach.[^VI-3] The duty must run to the beneficiary class — defined, presumably, as the future humans whose welfare the Authority's grants are intended to advance — not to any of the Authority's other stakeholders.

Direct distribution (Pillar 4): the Authority's grant decisions must be visible to the Authority's stakeholders in a form analogous to Utah's classroom-by-classroom transparency.[^VI-4] If a grant of compute to a longevity-research consortium is the Authority's equivalent of a section-16 disbursement, the population of practitioners and researchers and their broader civic communities must be able to see it. Visibility is the prerequisite for the distributed defensive perimeter.

Independent advocacy (Pillar 5): an office whose only mission is the future-beneficiary class — analogous to Utah's Land Trusts Protection and Advocacy Office — must be statutorily separate from the Authority's management.[^VI-4] The 2055 humans whose welfare is in the Authority's portfolio cannot show up in 2030 to argue. Someone must show up for them.

Specialized legal defense (Pillar 6): when the Authority's interests conflict with the interests of the entities that fund it, host its compute, or supply its political legitimacy — and they will, on the historical evidence — the Authority must have its own counsel.

Mandatory fiduciary education (Pillar 7): the Authority's governance committee, like the State Land Boards before it, will be composed of officials whose primary professional formation will be technical or political rather than fiduciary. Annual training is the cognitive recalibration the role requires.

The standing constituency: this is where the design problem is hardest. The school trust acquired its enforcement community late and partially, after two centuries of drift. The Authority would need its enforcement community in place before the first grant cycle. Diamandis and Wissner-Gross's concept of "Programmatic Down-Shifting" implicitly recognizes the problem; what it does not yet specify is who has the authority to do the down-shifting, on whose behalf, with what enforcement mechanism. The school-trust evidence says the answer cannot be the Authority's own staff. The answer has to be a community external to the Authority that is structurally invested in the Authority's compliance.

### Climate trusts and intergenerational equity

The international-law literature on intergenerational equity has been working the same problem the school trust embodies, in a different domain, for more than thirty-five years. Edith Brown Weiss's 1989 *In Fairness to Future Generations* is the canonical text.[^VI-5] The legal-philosophical question Weiss poses — whether and how a polity can incur enforceable obligations to a beneficiary class not yet alive — is the same question the 1785 framers answered in their own period vocabulary by reaching for the charitable-trust form. Weiss's answer is similar in shape: a trust-like fiduciary obligation, supplemented by representational mechanisms (a future-generations ombudsman; standing for future-generation advocates), all operating under explicit principles of intergenerational equity.

Climate trusts under construction in 2026 — the New Zealand Climate Change Commission, the proposed UN climate trust funds, the various national sovereign carbon funds — face the structural problem the school trust faced. The beneficiary class is the silent class. The institution administering the trust will, over a century, optimize for its own continuity. The cognitive bias of the people running it will favor the present.

The pillars apply. Codified duties (the 1910 NM-AZ "breach of trust" language scaled up to international compact). Asset restoration (where carbon budgets are exceeded, recovery mechanisms must exist). Individual accountability (board members of climate trusts personally liable for breach in the same way charitable-trust trustees are at common law). Direct distribution (the climate trust's outputs visible to local stakeholders, in the form of climate-resilience disbursements analogous to Utah's school-by-school distribution). Independent advocacy (a future-generations office, structurally separate from the trust's management). Specialized legal defense (the trust's own counsel, independent of the political branches of the parent state). Mandatory fiduciary education (annual training for the trust's governing officials).

The Pope's 2015 encyclical *Laudato Si'* and the international intergenerational-equity literature give the climate trust a moral vocabulary the school trust acquired only late in its history.[^VI-6] What both lack, still, is the operational architecture the school trust did acquire — and what that architecture's performance shows about the limits of formal language alone.

### Sovereign wealth funds

The Norwegian Government Pension Fund Global is the resilient case among contemporary sovereign wealth funds.[^VI-7] Its design is consistent with the seven-pillar formulation: a transparent management entity statutorily separated from the political branches; explicit ethical guidelines; published decision rules; an external advisory council with independent standing; codified duties to the beneficiary population (the Norwegian people, present and future); and a culture of professional fiduciary identity at the management entity that has, over the fund's three decades of existence, resisted political diversion.

Other sovereign wealth funds — the various Gulf-state funds, the more recently chartered Asian funds, several proposed funds in Africa and Latin America — vary in the strength of their architectures, and the school-trust evidence predicts that the variation will track the variation in their long-run performance. Funds with strong codified duties, independent advocacy, and active enforcement constituencies will hold their ground. Funds without will, on the predicted timeline, drift.

The point is not to advocate for Norway's specific architecture. The point is that the architecture's elements are recognizable. They are the same elements the school trust either had or lacked. The natural-experiment data is in.

### Compute escrows, longevity escrows, and data trusts

The smaller-scale aspirational structures emerging in the AI economy share the same pattern at smaller stakes. Compute escrows — proposals to hold AI infrastructure for public-benefit purposes on perpetual horizons — face the same drift pathology. Longevity escrows — funds dedicated to research and treatment access for beneficiaries on fifty- and hundred-year horizons — have the perfect-host beneficiary problem in clinically pure form. Data trusts — entities holding ownership of consumer datasets across changes in corporate ownership — have the eusocial-drift problem in compressed timeline.

The compounding feature of these structures is that they are not sequenced: they are emerging in parallel, in the same five-year window, with the same underlying technological substrate. The aggregate institutional inheritance of the AI moment will, by 2030, include several dozen aspirational structures with intergenerational beneficiaries, varying widely in the strength of their formal architectures and the presence of their enforcement constituencies. The school-trust evidence predicts that the variation in their thirty-year performance will track the variation in their starting conditions.

### The live cases: PERS and Social Security

Two structures already in operation in the United States are domestic exhibits of the same pathology under way in real time.

The Oregon Public Employees Retirement System (PERS) is, in the technical sense, a defined-benefit pension fund. In the structural sense, it is an aspirational structure with intergenerational beneficiaries — current public employees whose retirements depend on contributions and investment returns extending over a sixty- or seventy-year working-life-plus-retirement horizon. PERS's funded ratio has drifted in ways that match the school-trust pattern: near-term political pressure (legislative reluctance to fund the actuarially required contribution) compounded by hyperbolic discounting (the future obligation is steeply discounted by the legislators making the decision) and beneficiary-side political weakness (public employees, while not voiceless, do not generate the institutional feedback that would correct the drift in real time). The result is the predicted trajectory.[^VI-8]

Social Security operates on the largest stakes the country has. It is the most consequential aspirational structure the United States has built since the school trust, by orders of magnitude, and it faces the same pathology. The actuarial reports document the drift.[^VI-9] The political response — periodic "reform" packages that paper over the drift without correcting its causes — is the eusocial-drift signature in classical form. The bureaucracy administering Social Security has not failed; its operational competence is high. What is failing is the political infrastructure around it: the cognitive bias against valuing future beneficiaries at par; the absence of a structural advocacy office for the future-beneficiary class; the absence of personal accountability for the political actors whose decisions consume the fund's actuarial soundness.

These domestic cases are not anecdotal asides. They are the closest contemporary American examples of the school-trust pathology in operation, and they show that the diagnosis is not specific to nineteenth-century land grants. It is a diagnosis of how aspirational structures behave under intergenerational pressure, full stop.

### The mapping closes

The closing point is the simple one. The school-trust evidence and the AI-age design problem are not separate. They are the same problem at different stages of its institutional development.

The school trust has 240 years of running data, partial recoveries, and a slowly arriving enforcement constituency.[^VI-10] The AI-age structures have a charter window of perhaps five to ten years before they harden into the form they will keep. The window is when the architecture can still be specified.

What the school-trust evidence offers is a working manual: the pathology, the seven pillars, the eighth element. The architecture worked where it was installed; it failed where it was not. The next section names what to do about that, for the people now in the position to install it.
