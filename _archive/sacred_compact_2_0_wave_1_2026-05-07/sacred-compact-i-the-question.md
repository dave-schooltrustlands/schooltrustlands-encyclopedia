# I. The Question

### I.A — The trusts we are about to build

In 2026, Peter Diamandis and Alex Wissner-Gross published *Solve Everything*, a book that proposes a new governance institution they call an AI Targeting Authority — a perpetual, autonomous body empowered to direct compute, capital, and human attention toward humanity's most pressing problems. The proposal is serious. The authors argue that the AI moment requires institutions designed to outlast the political cycles that would otherwise consume them: structures with their own resources, their own decision rules, their own legal personhood, their own capacity to act across generations.[^I-1]

They are not alone. The same impulse is producing a thicket of new aspirational structures — climate trusts designed to bind tomorrow's legislatures to today's emissions commitments, sovereign wealth funds chartered for the benefit of citizens not yet born, longevity-research escrows committed to fifty- or hundred-year horizons, data trusts intended to hold ownership of consumer datasets across changes in corporate ownership, compute reserves designed to hold AI infrastructure for public benefit on a perpetual basis. The vocabulary varies. The architectural impulse does not. Each of these is a structure designed to bind future generations to commitments made today, on the theory that today's actors can see something the future will need but the future cannot yet defend on its own behalf.

These are the trusts we are about to build. None of them has been pressure-tested across generational time. We do not know how any of them will behave in their fortieth year, their seventieth year, their hundred-and-twentieth year — when the people who designed them are gone and the political world that surrounded them is unrecognizable.

We do not know, that is, except for one example.

### I.B — The trust we already built

In May 1785, the Confederation Congress put one sentence into a long surveying ordinance. The sentence reserved a parcel — section sixteen — in every six-mile-square township of the Northwest Territory "for the maintenance of public schools."[^I-2]

The Congress that wrote that sentence was operating under conditions that should be familiar to anyone designing the new aspirational structures of 2026: catastrophic public debt, a barely-functional capital market, an extractive geopolitical environment, an asset that was abundant in the moment but visibly finite over time (in their case, land; in ours, attention and compute), and a beneficiary class — children — with no political power to defend its share. They reached for an institutional form, the charitable trust, that English equity courts had spent two centuries hardening against precisely the failure modes their successors would face. They locked the asset into the geometry of national settlement. They stated the principle in compact articles declared to be perpetual. They made acceptance of the compact a condition of new states' admission to the Union.[^I-3]

It was, in its design, an impressive piece of work. It was the longest-time-horizon institution that the eighteenth century could engineer with the tools available, and the framers built it with care. The compact they built has been running continuously for 240 years. It is older than the United States Constitution. It is older than every existing Western democracy. It is older than most of the corporate and philanthropic forms today's institution-builders take for granted. The data from that experiment is in.

This paper is about what the data says.

### I.C — What the experiment returns

The data says, briefly, that aspirational structures decay predictably without an active enforcement constituency, and that the decay is structural. It is not the contingent product of bad actors or unlucky historical moments. It is a consequence of features these institutions share with one another, and that today's designers are about to share with them.

The features are three.

First, *hyperbolic discounting* — the human cognitive bias that overvalues immediate rewards relative to future ones. In an individual making a retirement decision, the bias is mildly costly. In an institution managing a 250-year trust, it is fitness-destroying. The trust loses ground every time a near-term political pressure meets a long-term fiduciary duty, and it loses ground in a direction the bias guarantees: toward the present, away from the beneficiary.

Second, *eusocial pathology* in the bureaucracies guarding the trust. Eusociality is the pattern social insects share with mature human bureaucracies: the survival of the hive begins to take precedence over the survival of the species. In a long-running institution, it produces what political scientists have variously called regulatory capture, the iron law of oligarchy, and institutional parasitism. The agency comes to view its mission resources as resources for its own homeostasis.

Third, *the beneficiary class whose political silence the framers rarely engineered for*. Schoolchildren cannot vote. Future generations cannot lobby. Climate beneficiaries are not yet born. Recipients of an AI Targeting Authority's grants have no idea their interests are being traded away. The institutions are designed for the politically silent — and the political silence becomes the vector through which the trust is captured. A perfect-host beneficiary is one whose welfare the institution can sacrifice without political cost. Children, future generations, the unborn, and the beneficiaries of trusts whose corpus is held in geographically distant or technically opaque assets all qualify.

These three features compound. Hyperbolic discounting creates the time-preference asymmetry. Eusocial pathology creates the institutional self-preference. The perfect-host beneficiary creates the absence of pushback. The trust drifts; the bureaucracy reframes the drift as prudent management; the beneficiaries do not show up to argue.

The data also says, importantly, that recoveries are possible. There are periods in the school-trust history — and there are entire states, such as Utah today and Oregon between roughly 1920 and 1990 — in which the trust held its ground, served its beneficiaries, and grew the asset rather than shrinking it.[^I-4] The recoveries share structural features the failures do not. Those features are not philosophical; they are operational. They can be specified. They can be installed in advance. The paper's claim is that they should be — and that the next generation of aspirational structures can be built with the operational features of the recoveries already in place, before the drift begins, rather than retrofitted after the drift has already done its work.

### I.D — The argument of this paper

Section II tells the story of the original design — how the American school trust was built, what its framers thought they were doing, what legal tools they used, and what tools they did not have. The story is American history of a high order; it is also institutional design at its most ambitious. The framers who wrote the May 1785 sentence reached for the most rigid legal device available to them, and they reached well. What they could not reach for is the subject of the rest of the paper.

Sections III and IV diagnose the drift. Section III is a national story told through five state cases — Oregon as the most documented case, Utah as the recovery case, Arizona as the beneficiary-standing case, California as the failure-by-omission case, and a brief survey across the rest. Oregon's case is contemporaneous and live: in 2024, schoolchildren and their advocates filed *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* (Coos County 24CV38372), and on January 28, 2026, the Oregon Court of Appeals held that the schoolchild beneficiaries had standing to enforce the State's fiduciary duties under Article VIII of the Oregon Constitution — the first contemporary appellate decision in the country recognizing beneficiary-side standing to sue a State Land Board for trust breach.[^I-5] Section IV moves from descriptive case study to causal model: it makes explicit the three features named above, traces their mechanisms, and shows why the failures in Section III occurred when and where they did, and why the recoveries occurred when and where they did.

Section V proposes a counter-architecture: seven structural defenses (codified fiduciary duties, asset restoration, individual accountability, direct distribution, independent beneficiary advocacy, specialized legal defense, mandatory fiduciary education), plus a standing constituency — an active community across generational time, capable of enforcing the formal architecture when the institutional immune system fails.[^I-6]

Section VI maps the counter-architecture onto the trusts of the AI age, the climate age, and the longevity age. It shows that the same pathologies operate in the new domains and the same defenses apply, and it draws specific design implications for the AI Targeting Authority, the climate trust, and the longevity escrow.

Section VII names what to do — for policymakers, for foundations and investors, for citizens, and for the designers of the new aspirational structures themselves.

The argument runs across two registers at once. As a piece of American history, it is a story about how a continent and a civilization built a sacred compact for its children and let it slip away. As a piece of governance design, it is a working manual for the people now building structures whose beneficiaries will not be born for another fifty or hundred years. The two registers are not separable. The framers who wrote that one sentence in May 1785 knew they were trying something; they did not know how long the experiment would run, and they could not have predicted what its results would be.[^I-7] The architects of the AI Targeting Authority and the perpetual climate trust and the longevity escrow do know — at least, they will, after this paper.

What that experiment returns is what this paper is about.
