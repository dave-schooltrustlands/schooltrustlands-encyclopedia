**Facts.** The Utah Division of State Lands and Forestry approved a land exchange transferring a parcel of Utah school trust lands to a private party in exchange for other lands and consideration, on the basis of an appraisal furnished by the prospective purchaser. The National Parks and Conservation Association (NPCA), together with other environmental and beneficiary-aligned plaintiffs, challenged the exchange, contending that the school trust lands had been undervalued and that the Division had breached its fiduciary duty by relying on a purchaser-supplied appraisal as the basis for fair market value. Standing was contested.

**Holding.** The Utah Supreme Court held that NPCA had standing to sue because the case raised "issues of significant public importance." On the merits, the Court held that the Division breached its fiduciary duty by relying on a purchaser-supplied appraisal as the basis for ascertaining the fair market value of trust assets. "An appraisal submitted by a party intending to purchase trust asset is suspect on its face, even when performed by an independent and reputable appraiser, because the buyer has the opportunity to shop for favorable appraisals," *NPCA*, 869 P.2d at 919 (slip op. at 19), and to permit such reliance would "leave the trust subject to sharp dealing on the part of the purchaser." *Id.* The Court further held that "the school land trust is not only imposed on the disposition of proceeds from school trust lands, but also on the use of the land itself." *Id.* at 913 (slip op. at 9). To comply with its fiduciary duties, the Division must obtain its own independent appraisals before disposing of trust assets.

**Why it matters.** *NPCA v. Board of State Lands* is the leading state-supreme-court authority on two doctrines simultaneously: appraisal integrity and public-interest standing for school-trust-lands enforcement. The appraisal rule — that a buyer-supplied appraisal is suspect on its face and cannot serve as the trustee's basis for fair market value — has been widely adopted and applied in subsequent trust-lands disputes involving land exchanges, mineral leases, and timber sales. The standing rule — that organizations raising "issues of significant public importance" may sue to enforce school land trusts — operates as a counterweight to the more restrictive standing doctrine deployed in federal court under *ASARCO v. Kadish*. The case also stands for the broader proposition that the trust attaches to the *use* of the land, not merely to the disposition of proceeds — a holding that forecloses the argument, sometimes advanced by trustee-states, that fiduciary obligations apply only at the moment of sale.

**Cited in.** Utah, Idaho, Montana, Washington, Oklahoma, and Wyoming trust-lands jurisprudence; particularly cited in disputes over land exchanges, purchaser-supplied appraisals, and the scope of public-interest standing to enforce school trust obligations.
