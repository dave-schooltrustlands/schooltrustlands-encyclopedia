**Facts.** When Congress withdrew federal land from the public domain to create the Gila National Forest in New Mexico, the reservation included streams running through the forest. Many decades later the United States sought to claim federally reserved water rights for instream uses associated with the national forest — including aesthetic, recreational, fish-and-wildlife, and stock-watering uses — superior in priority to subsequent state-law appropriations. The State of New Mexico contested the scope of the implied federal reservation, arguing that the reserved-rights doctrine extends only as far as necessary to fulfill the specific primary purposes for which Congress reserved the land.

**Holding.** The Supreme Court held, 5–4, that when the United States reserves public land it impliedly reserves only that quantity of water necessary to fulfill the specific primary purposes for which the reservation was created, and not water for secondary uses. Reading the Organic Administration Act of 1897 narrowly, the Court found that Congress reserved the national forests for only two primary purposes — to conserve water flows and to furnish a continuous supply of timber — and that aesthetic, recreational, fish-and-wildlife, and stock-watering uses were "secondary" purposes for which no water rights were impliedly reserved.

**Why it matters.** In the school-trust-lands setting, *United States v. New Mexico* is invoked principally as an interpretive method, not as a rule about water. It frames the doctrine of "primary purpose" — that when Congress sets aside land for a stated objective, the scope of any implied authority is bounded by, and confined to, that objective. Trust-lands litigators apply the same interpretive method to the school land grants: Congress granted sections sixteen and thirty-six (and, in later states, additional sections) for the *primary and exclusive* purpose of supporting the common schools, and the state's authority to manage those lands is bounded by that purpose. Uses or management regimes pursued for "secondary" objectives — local economic development, environmental amenity, recreational access — cannot displace the primary fiduciary obligation. The case is doctrinally allied with *Lassen* and *Andrus* in confining state discretion to the stated purpose of the grant.

**Cited in.** Federal-court trust-land litigation across the Western land-grant states; relied upon in Idaho, Utah, and Montana trust-land jurisprudence on the scope of permissible state action under the enabling acts.
