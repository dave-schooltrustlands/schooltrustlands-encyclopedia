**Facts.** A coalition of Colorado school districts and individual taxpayers brought suit in federal district court challenging Colorado Amendment 16 (1996), a state constitutional amendment that altered the management principles governing Colorado's school-trust-lands estate. Plaintiffs argued that the Colorado Enabling Act of 1875 — the federal-state compact under which Colorado was admitted to the Union — fixed the terms on which Colorado held the school sections in trust, that Amendment 16 unilaterally altered those terms in violation of the federal compact, and that the federal courts had jurisdiction to enforce the original Enabling Act terms against the state. The district court ruled in 1997 (*Branson*, 958 F. Supp. 1501 (D. Colo. 1997)) that the Enabling Act created a binding trust and that Amendment 16 was subject to scrutiny under the Enabling Act's trust terms. The State and the Colorado State Land Board appealed.

**Holding.** The Tenth Circuit affirmed in substantial part. The court held that the Colorado Enabling Act of 1875 created a real and enforceable trust, that the state held the school-section lands as trustee subject to the Enabling Act's terms, and that federal-court jurisdiction was available to enforce those terms against the state. On the merits, the court found that the substantive changes Amendment 16 introduced were consistent with the trust's underlying purposes — preservation of the trust corpus, productivity for the benefit of schools, intergenerational equity — and therefore did not impair the trust in a manner that required federal-court intervention. The case is significant less for the particular outcome on Amendment 16 than for the federal-court doctrinal framework it articulated: enabling acts create enforceable trusts, federal courts have jurisdiction to police the trust terms, and state-constitutional changes that alter trust management are subject to scrutiny under the federal trust framework.

**Why it matters.** *Branson* is the leading modern federal-court statement of the strict trust theory of school-trust lands in the federal-circuit caselaw. Three doctrinal contributions matter. First, the court treated the federal-state Enabling Act compact as a binding trust enforceable in federal court — a holding that places school-trust-lands disputes squarely within the federal-court doctrine on intergovernmental compacts. Second, the court articulated a substantive standard for evaluating state-constitutional changes that affect the trust: the question is not whether the state has formal authority to amend its constitution but whether the amendment is consistent with the trust's underlying federal-compact purposes. Third, the case stands as the federal-court counterpart to the SCOTUS line running from *Vincennes* (1852) through *Cooper* (1855), *Beecher* (1877), *Lassen* (1967), and *Andrus* (1980) — confirming that the strict trust theory remains the federal-court doctrine of school-trust-lands enforcement.

**Cited in.** Cited by federal district courts and circuit courts in school-trust-lands disputes from Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, Idaho, and other admission-era trust-lands states; cited in the academic literature on the enforceability of federal-state compacts in school-trust contexts. Often invoked alongside *Lassen* and *Andrus* as the modern statement of the strict trust theory.
