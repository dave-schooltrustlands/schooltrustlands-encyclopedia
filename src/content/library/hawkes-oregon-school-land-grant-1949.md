# History of the Oregon School Land Grant from 1849 to 1900 (Hawkes, 1949)

## Why this matters

Hawkes's 1949 University of Oregon master's thesis is the most thorough state-specific period history of how Oregon's school-trust lands were monetized in their first half-century. He had access to the state land-board records, to the Mitchell-trial transcripts, to the General Land Office files in Washington, and to the surveys-general reports that documented sale-by-sale what the state did with the corpus it received at statehood. He wrote close to the records, before the post-war reorganizations dispersed some of those archives. For Oregon, this is the canonical archival reconstruction of the drift period — the half-century in which the trust corpus was sold, under-priced, in many cases corruptly, and the proceeds redirected.

## What's in it

- Chapter I — The federal grant. The 1859 Admissions Act, Sections 16 and 36, and the federal disposition framework.
- Chapter II — Early state administration, 1859–1872. The first land board; initial sale practices; the absence of audit.
- Chapter III — The expansion years, 1872–1890. The shift from cash sale to deferred-payment instruments; the rise of speculator entries.
- Chapter IV — The Mitchell era. Senator John H. Mitchell and the federal land-fraud trials of 1903–1905; the cross-cutting state-land implications.
- Chapter V — The under-pricing pattern. State sales below federal minimum-acreage prices; the comparison with neighboring states' practices.
- Chapter VI — The trust-corpus accounting. Where the proceeds went; the divergence between nominal fund balances and actual recoverable principal.
- Chapter VII — The 1900 inflection. The end of the period Hawkes treats; the institutional reforms that followed (and did not follow).
- Appendices — Sale-by-sale tabulations; correspondence with the General Land Office; the 1903 state board reports.

## Most-quoted passages

On the under-pricing: Hawkes documents that Oregon, like several Western states, repeatedly sold school-trust acreage below the federal minimum that the General Land Office charged on adjacent unappropriated public-domain lands. The pattern was not a single transaction but a structural practice — small parcels, deferred payments, speculator entries — sustained across multiple state administrations.

On the Mitchell connection: the federal land-fraud prosecutions of 1903–1905, which sent Senator Mitchell to conviction, were nominally about federal-domain timber claims, but the same land-locating networks that worked the federal entries also worked the state school-trust entries. Hawkes traces specific land-locator names that appear in both categories.

On the corpus accounting: by 1900 the nominal Common School Fund balance materially overstated the recoverable principal, because a substantial fraction was held in deferred-payment instruments that were never collected, in commingled state accounts, and in instruments that depreciated. Hawkes was among the first to put numbers on the gap.

On the absence of audit: Hawkes notes that for long stretches of the 1859–1900 period there was no independent audit of the school-trust transactions — the same officials who made the sales reported the proceeds, and the legislature did not require external verification.

## How it connects to the Library's argument

Hawkes is the archival floor under the Oregon-specific portions of *Schools of the Republic* and under the OASTL litigation's empirical record. When the Library says "Oregon's trust corpus was monetized below market in the second half of the nineteenth century, and the proceeds were never fully recovered to the beneficiary," the citation chain runs through Hawkes. When OASTL counsel argues that the state failed its fiduciary duty in the disposition era, Hawkes is the historian whose numbers anchor the argument.

What makes Hawkes irreplaceable is that he wrote in 1949 with access to records that have since been dispersed across multiple state and federal repositories. A modern researcher reconstructing the same archive would face years of work; Hawkes did the reconstruction in one volume, with sale-by-sale tabulations in his appendices. The drift-of-purpose argument the Library carries — that the corpus was eroded before the modern reform era, and that nominal fund balances overstate recoverable principal — is empirically grounded in this kind of state-specific archival work. Oregon has Hawkes; most states do not yet have their equivalent. Building those state-specific archival reconstructions is part of the Library's longer institutional agenda.

Margaret Bird has cited Hawkes in her own writing on the Oregon corpus. The thesis is a primary working text in the OASTL substrate.
