# Chapter 6 — The Quiet Substitution

> *"Without knowing it, taxpayers bought a state forest that they already owned."*
>
> — John A. Charles, Jr., Cascade Policy Institute

I read the headline on the morning of March 21, 2019, with a cup of coffee in front of me at the kitchen table in Kings Valley. The headline was on Oregon Public Broadcasting's website, and it said *Oregon Sells $100 Million In Bonds For Elliott State Forest*. I read it twice. I remember thinking, in that first reading, that the trust was being made whole. The State had taken away some of the Elliott's harvest revenue with new endangered-species protections; the State was now putting a hundred million dollars back into the Common School Fund to make up for it. That was how the story was being told that day in the Oregon press, and that was how I understood it when I closed the laptop and went out to the trees.

I was wrong about what *made whole* meant. It took me the better part of the next year to see why.

The State did issue the bonds. The Treasurer's office, then under Tobias Read, did sign the offering papers on March 21, 2019. A hundred million dollars in general-obligation debt — debt backed by the full faith and credit of the State of Oregon, paid back over its term from whatever general tax revenue future legislatures chose to appropriate for the debt service — did move from the bond market to the State's accounts, and from there into the Common School Fund. The Department of State Lands described the deposit, in the public materials I read in the weeks that followed, as *compensating* the Fund for revenue the Fund would no longer be receiving from the Elliott because the harvest had been curtailed. On the agency's books and in the Common School Fund's annual report for that year, the hundred million dollars appeared as a new asset of the trust.

That is the surface description. The trust got a hundred million dollars. The Elliott was still on the books at the State Land Board. Nothing had been formally sold. From a casual read of the news that month, you would have come away believing that the State had stepped in to protect the schoolchildren's interest against a temporary revenue dip, and that the schoolchildren were better off because of it. That is what I believed.

What I missed in March 2019, and came to understand over the following months, is that the trust's claim had quietly changed character. Before the bond issuance, the trust held a working forest — 82,500 acres of Coast Range Douglas-fir whose value compounded in the soil, year after year, the way the long-rotation forests of the Pacific Northwest have always compounded. After the bond issuance, the trust held a working forest *plus* a hundred million dollars in bond proceeds, with the trust's effective revenue from the forest now backstopped by, and increasingly indistinguishable from, a line item in the Oregon state budget. The asset had not been sold. But the trust's relationship to its asset had been partially severed, and the severing had been done by the trustee, in favor of the trustee's own IOU.

This is the move I want to walk you through in this chapter. I want to be careful, because the move was a step in a direction rather than the completion of the journey. The completion came later — first with the 2022 OSU plan and then, in 2025, with Senate Bill 147, which formally decoupled the Elliott from the Common School Fund. The 2019 bond was the preparation. It was the moment the trustee began to set up the architecture under which the asset itself would later be replaced, and to teach the public, the press, and the schoolchildren's nominal protectors to accept the trade.

The Cascade Policy Institute's John A. Charles, Jr. — a libertarian critic of the bonds whom I had not, until 2019, ever read — wrote the line that has stayed with me through every subsequent step in this case. *"Without knowing it, taxpayers bought a state forest that they already owned."* The line is exactly right, and it cuts in two directions at once. The taxpayers were paying for an asset their children's trust already held. The schoolchildren's trust was being given, as compensation, a financial instrument backed by the taxes the taxpayers were already paying. The State had managed to put itself in the position of both buyer and seller — and to mark the proceeds as a deposit into a fund the State itself administered. The structural conflict is not subtle once you see it. The reason you do not see it in the OPB headline of March 21, 2019, is that nobody covering the story that day had been asked to see it that way.

<aside class="readers-edition-aside">

#### Why a Trustee Cannot Sell to Itself

The most basic rule of trust law — the one every first-year law student meets and every working trustee learns — is the *duty of loyalty*. The trustee holds the trust's property for the benefit of the beneficiaries. Not for the trustee's own benefit. Not for the benefit of some other party the trustee owes loyalty to. For the beneficiaries.

The duty of loyalty has a sharp edge that the law has held for several hundred years. The trustee cannot be on both sides of a transaction involving the trust's property. A trustee who sells the trust's land to himself, or to a company he controls, or to anyone whose interest is aligned with the trustee's rather than the beneficiary's, has breached the duty regardless of whether the price was fair. The rule is not about whether the trustee meant well. It is about the structural impossibility of negotiating against yourself on behalf of someone else. You cannot honestly bargain hard for the trust when your hand is also on the other side of the deal.

A particular form of this prohibition concerns the trustee's own IOU. A trustee cannot sell the trust's real asset and replace it with the trustee's own promise to pay. The asset was a thing the beneficiaries owned, equitably, that had value independent of the trustee's solvency or good faith. The IOU is a promise from the trustee, redeemable on the trustee's terms, subject to the trustee's discretion, with the trustee setting the interest rate and the priority. The substitution moves the beneficiary's claim from something real into something the trustee controls.

The black-letter rule, in the language of the *Restatement of Trusts* and a hundred and fifty years of American trust cases: the substitution is voidable at the beneficiary's election. The trustee owes the beneficiary the asset back, or its full value, or both. The fairness of the substitution at the moment it occurred does not save it. The structural conflict is enough.

In Oregon, the Common School Fund is held by the State Land Board — the Governor, the Secretary of State, and the Treasurer — in trust for the schoolchildren. When the Board issues bonds to compensate the trust for a reduction in the trust's revenue, the Board is sitting on both sides of the transaction. The same officers who administer the trust are signing the trustee's own IOU to the trust. That hand cannot be neutral. The law has known this for a long time.

</aside>

The 2019 bond, then, was not in itself the prohibited substitution. The Elliott was still in trust. The harvest had been reduced, not zeroed. The bond proceeds were a deposit *into* the Common School Fund, not a swap of the Elliott *for* the bonds. If the State had stopped there — if the bonds had been a one-time bridge across an unusually difficult biennium, and if the trust's interest in the Elliott had been restored to a productive footing after the bridge — the doctrine would have raised an eyebrow but not condemned the move outright. Bridges across hard years happen.

What made the bond a step toward the prohibited substitution, rather than a defensible one-time bridge, was the architecture it built. Once the State had taught itself that compensation to the trust could be accomplished through bond proceeds rather than through restored harvest revenue, the next step became thinkable. Once the trust's books recorded a hundred million dollars in bond proceeds as the equivalent of the Elliott's missing revenue, the next step — replacing more of the Elliott's revenue with more bond proceeds, and eventually replacing the Elliott itself with the bond mechanism — became a procedural matter rather than a doctrinal one. The hard work of normalizing the trade had been done in 2019.

The follow-on appropriation in 2021 made this visible. The Legislature added an additional $121 million — partly more general-obligation bonds, partly direct appropriation — to the Elliott account, bringing the total projected debt service the State would owe on the Elliott-related borrowing to roughly $146 million by the time the bonds matured. The 2021 follow-on was framed in the same language the 2019 issuance had been framed in: compensation to the trust, public benefit, environmental protection. It was the same move, in a larger size, with no one in the press or the Legislature raising the question that John Charles had raised two years earlier about who was buying what from whom.

The forensic accounting analysis I prepared in early 2026 — which now sits in the L0 substrate of this book and has been reviewed by counsel — walked through the Common School Fund Annual Reports from 2016 forward and reconciled them against the cash-flow data the State has been compelled to disclose in discovery in our 2024 case. The analysis surfaced several things that the public-facing accounting had obscured. The bond proceeds were recorded as a one-time addition to the Fund's corpus, with no corresponding entry on the trust's books for the debt-service obligation the State was now carrying on behalf of the Elliott. The Common School Fund's distribution per student continued to decline in real terms despite the addition — because the bond proceeds, once invested in the Fund's investment pool, produced an annual return that was materially less than the harvest revenue the Elliott had produced when it was managed as a working forest. The substitution, even taken at the State's own valuations, was not revenue-neutral for the schools. The schools were getting less.

And then there was the question of who was acting for whom. The Treasurer who signed the 2019 offering documents was the same Treasurer who had voted yes on the Lone Rock sale in February 2017 and had then voted to reverse the sale in May 2017 under public pressure. The same Treasurer signed the bond papers in 2019 that began to accomplish, through quieter means, the underlying conversion the 2017 sale had been intended to accomplish. I am not making a personal accusation against Tobias Read; the actions of a sitting State Treasurer on any individual day are within the range of behaviors a State Treasurer might reasonably undertake. What I am pointing out is the structural pattern. The same trustee, across multiple decision points, moved the trust in the same direction. The mechanism varied. The direction did not.

There was almost no press on March 21, 2019. The Oregonian ran a short piece. OPB ran the headline I had read at my kitchen table. There were no protests, no sign campaigns, no Save-the-Children's-Forest mobilization of the kind the 2017 sale had drawn. A general-obligation bond issuance moving through the Legislature in the ordinary course of a budget cycle does not have the public visibility a Land Board vote on a private sale does. The agencies had learned the lesson of 2017. The next move would not be the kind of move that produced signs. It would be the kind of move that produced footnotes in financial statements.

The conversion, I came to understand, had not been completed. The asset was still there. The trust still held title. The forest was still on the State Land Board's books as a Common School Fund asset. What had been changed was the question being asked of the trust. Before March 2019, the question was *what is this working forest worth to the schoolchildren who own it?* After March 2019, the question was *what is the appropriate level of bond-funded compensation for a forest the State will increasingly manage for purposes other than the trust's?* The first question has a billion-dollar answer that the State has never wanted to put in writing. The second question has whatever answer the Legislature feels like appropriating in any given biennium. The shift from the first question to the second was the work the 2019 bond did.

This is the move I came, eventually, to see clearly. The 2019 bond was not the theft. The 2019 bond was the moment the State began to teach the trust to accept a different kind of compensation than the trust was owed. The theft itself — the formal severance of the Elliott from the Common School Fund's beneficial interest — came later. It came in 2022, when the Oregon State University College of Forestry submitted its proposal to convert the Elliott into a research forest under a memorandum of understanding that would, on its own page 28, project a $2.1 million annual deficit on the trust for the first fifty years. It came again in 2025, when Senate Bill 147 finalized the decoupling that the bonds had been preparing the ground for. Those are the chapters that come next.

What I want you to carry forward from this chapter is the quiet half of the story. The loud half, the 2017 sale and its reversal, looked like a victory and was followed by a defeat. The quiet half, the 2019 bond and the 2021 follow-on, looked like compensation and was the actual conversion. The press covered the loud half. The substitution happened in the quiet half. By the time the OSU plan arrived in 2022, the trust had already been taught that the State's IOU could stand in for the State's forest. The plan only had to finish what the bonds had begun.

I read the headline that March morning and thought the trust had been made whole. I was wrong about what *made whole* meant. *Made whole* meant the trust had been given a budget line item in place of a forest, and had been asked to call them the same thing. They are not the same thing. The schoolchildren own the forest. They have never owned the budget. The work of the next chapters is to walk through how the rest of that distinction came to be erased — and how the case I now lead, in 2026, is the attempt to put the distinction back where it belongs.
