# Chapter 12 — What's Been Stolen, What's Left to Steal

The State of Oregon's chosen appraisal said the Elliott was worth $99.6 million. The market said $1 billion. The difference between those two numbers is what is being taken from Oregon's schoolchildren.

That sentence is the whole chapter. The rest is the arithmetic.

## The two prices

On December 13, 2022, the State Land Board voted to sell the Elliott to itself. The price on the paper was $99.6 million. The number came from an appraisal performed by Mason, Bruce & Girard at the State's request. The appraisal was not a market appraisal. It was an "investment value" — a calculation of what the Elliott would be worth to a particular buyer running it under a particular set of restrictions written into the proposal in advance. The buyer was a research forest. The restrictions ruled out most of the harvest. The number came out small because the restrictions were designed to make it come out small.

Margaret Bird, in her December 11, 2022 letter to the Land Board, written two days before the vote, put the market value at $1 billion to $1.3 billion. That estimate did not come from nowhere. It came from John Beuter's 1994 analysis, commissioned by Oregon's own Department of Forestry, which concluded the Elliott would sell on the open market for "not likely less than $600 million, and could well be in the neighborhood of a billion dollars." It came from the 2016 Request for Proposals, which had set a floor of $220.8 million for a sale that was structurally cheaper than a true open-market sale — a floor that an industrial buyer met immediately. It came from Margaret's forty years inside the school-trust architecture, watching what timber-and-land assets fetch in arm's-length deals across the country.

Bill Lansing is the retired president and CEO of Menasha Forest Products Corporation. He spent his career buying and selling timber assets in the Pacific Northwest. In his declaration filed in the 2021 Sullivan case, and quoted in the materials Margaret circulated to the Land Board, he wrote:

> I was involved in the proposed sale of the Elliott State Forest a few years ago and could not believe the low value of the appraisal. ... I suggest the establishment of the low value was politically motivated to allow for a cheaper decoupling of the common school fund's primary responsibility.

A timber-company CEO with no stake in the OASTL litigation called the State's chosen appraisal politically motivated. He is not the only one. He may be the most credentialed.

The disparity between the two prices is roughly ten to one. The State's number was $99.6 million. The market's number was $1 billion or more. For every dollar of value the children's trust would have received on the open market, the State's chosen appraisal recognized about ten cents.

## The Ford pickup

Margaret has a way of making fiduciary math understandable to people who have never read a trust treatise. In that December 11, 2022 letter to the Land Board, she compared what the State was about to do to a Ford pickup.

Suppose, she wrote, you own a 2018 Ford F-150. You drove it off the lot for $35,000, and over the years you have put another $15,000 into it — tires, transmission work, a new bed liner. Your "investment value" in the truck is $50,000. That is what you have put in.

The "market value" of the truck is what someone will pay you for it today. That might be $25,000. It might be $35,000. It will not be $50,000, because trucks depreciate. The market does not care what you spent.

For most assets, investment value and market value differ — and market value is what you actually receive in a sale. You do not get to charge the buyer your sunk costs.

The Elliott, Margaret pointed out, is the opposite case. The State's investment value was lower than market value, not higher. The State had spent comparatively little buying up the 1930 land swap and managing the forest. The market value, meanwhile, reflected what someone would pay for ninety thousand acres of standing Coast Range timber today. A trustee selling the Elliott at "investment value" is doing the inverse of the Ford-pickup move. It is selling a truck worth $35,000 on the open market for the $15,000 it cost to assemble from parts. And in a trust, the trustee owes the beneficiaries market value, not whatever number the trustee has assigned for its own accounting purposes.

A trustee selling trust property at less than fair market value is not exercising business judgment. It is breaching the duty of loyalty. Margaret named the move plainly in the same letter: "Decoupling is a euphemism for selling." A euphemism is what you use when the plain word would expose what you are doing.

## What the asset was producing

Bob Zybach is a forester with a 2003 OSU PhD on Coast Range fire history. He filed a declaration in the 2021 Sullivan v. Oregon case laying out what the Elliott had been producing when it was actually managed as a trust asset.

The numbers were not small. Jerry Phillips, who managed the Elliott as the ODF Coos Bay District Manager from 1956 to 1989, generated approximately $790 million in revenue to the Common School Fund over his thirty-three-year tenure. The harvest pattern during the productive decades was roughly fifty million board feet per year. The forest was growing seventy-five million board feet per year. Harvesting two-thirds of net growth, the Elliott funded schools and replenished itself simultaneously. That was what a trust asset was supposed to do, and the Elliott was doing it.

Project that forward. Run the Elliott as a trust asset producing fifty million board feet per year for the next thirty years — half a century below the post-2017 zero-harvest line — and the forest produces hundreds of millions of dollars for Oregon schools while the Common School Fund principal continues to grow. That is the counterfactual the OSU plan replaces.

The replacement is not a wash. OSU's own December 2021 proposal admitted, on page 28, that the Elliott State Research Forest "is not self-sustaining from a financial perspective" and would run a "$2.1 million deficit on an average annual basis for the first 50 years." Fifty years at $2.1 million is $105 million in cumulative operating losses, before counting inflation. That is OSU's number, not OASTL's.

Now add the arithmetic. Foregone harvest revenue at fifty million board feet per year for fifty years, plus a $105 million cumulative operating deficit, plus the lost asset appreciation on a $1 billion working asset that under prudent trust management would have continued to grow. Even on conservative assumptions, the cumulative loss to Oregon's schoolchildren over the next half-century runs well over a billion dollars in present value. The State has not simply lowballed the appraisal. It has installed a plan whose own documents project losses every year for fifty years and called the plan a benefit to the children.

## Oregon, alone in 235 years

In October 2016, the Oregon School Boards Association quoted Margaret Bird in a press release. She had been studying school trust lands for twenty-three years at that point, working inside Utah's school-trust architecture, building the national community of trust administrators. She said:

> In the 235-year history of school trust lands that I have been studying for the last 23 years, no state has spent more than they earned like Oregon has. It never has happened.

Two hundred and thirty-five years runs back to 1785, when the Land Ordinance set aside Section Sixteen of every township for the support of the common schools. Across two and a quarter centuries of school-trust history, in dozens of states from Ohio in 1803 through Hawaii in 1959, every state with school-trust lands has at some point mismanaged something. None has crossed the line Oregon has crossed: drawing down faster than the asset produced. None has made it a settled pattern.

Oregon is the only state in 235 years that has spent more than it earned on its trust lands. That is the historical record, named in 2016 by the national authority on the subject. The 2022 decoupling vote did not cause the pattern. The decoupling vote was the formal acknowledgment that the pattern was now the policy.

## The forensic record

The forensic accounting analysis I prepared in April 2026, attached to OASTL's discovery materials in 24CV38372, documents what the State's books actually show. The analysis reconciles the State's public Common School Fund Annual Reports against the data the State has produced in discovery. The two records do not match. The public reports describe a fund that is healthy and growing. The discovery data tells a different story about what is leaving the fund, when, and why. The analysis is in L0 substrate at `L0_Primary_Sources/Discovery/20260401 - Accounting Analysis - Reconciling Public CSF Reports with State Discovery Data (Lawyer Reviewed).pdf`. The motion to compel filed May 2, 2026 is asking the court to require the State to disclose the data behind the reconciliation gap. A trustee that has nothing to hide does not hide its books.

When you put the appraisal disparity together with the forensic-accounting gap, the picture is consistent. The Elliott was being sold at one-tenth its market value. The fund that was supposed to receive the proceeds was being drawn down in ways the public reports did not show. Two ends of the same arithmetic.

## What's left to steal

The Land Board has not stopped. The OSU plan formally collapsed on November 13, 2023, when OSU President Jayathi Murthy sent the Department of State Lands a two-page letter withdrawing the university from leading the Elliott State Research Forest. Murthy named, in his own letter, the carbon-monetization and research-funding incompatibilities that the State and OSU had been arguing about for months. The plan as designed could not work, and the university said so on the record.

That should have closed the question. It did not. On June 14, 2025, the Oregon legislature passed SB 147, which repealed ORS 530.450 — the 1957 statutory provision that had prohibited selling the Elliott State Forest. The repeal happened nineteen months after the OSU plan collapsed. It happened while the OASTL litigation was active. It happened without resolving any of the fiduciary questions that the 2022 decoupling vote had failed to answer.

SB 147 is the second pass at decoupling. The legal mechanism is different. The substantive intent is the same: detach the Elliott from the Common School Fund's claim and convert it to something else. The first pass used a Land Board vote on an "investment value" appraisal. The second pass uses statutory repeal of the 1957 prohibition. Both pass run on the same arithmetic. Both pass the asset at less than fair market value. Both leave the trust beneficiaries — Oregon's schoolchildren — holding a smaller claim than the one they started with.

While this case sits in court, the dollars at stake do not stop accruing. Every year the Elliott does not harvest is a year of foregone revenue to the trust. Every year the OSU plan, or its statutory successor, sits in place is a year the cumulative loss compounds. The arithmetic does not pause for litigation.

## The accounting in one paragraph

The State of Oregon's chosen appraisal said the Elliott was worth $99.6 million. The market — through the Lone Rock $220.8 million bid in 2017, through John Beuter's 1994 ODF-commissioned analysis at $600 million to $1 billion, through Margaret Bird's December 2022 estimate at $1 billion to $1.3 billion, through Bill Lansing's professional judgment as the retired CEO of Menasha Forest Products — said roughly ten times that. Run the difference forward over the fifty years the OSU plan's own documents claim, add the $105 million in projected operating deficits, add the foregone harvest revenue at fifty million board feet a year, and the loss to Oregon's schoolchildren over the next half-century runs well over a billion dollars in present value. In two hundred and thirty-five years of school-trust history across the United States, no other state has made this particular arithmetic the settled pattern of its own conduct toward the children it is sworn to serve.

The accounting is in. The question Chapter 13 takes up is who, exactly, is going to stop the second pass before it lands.
