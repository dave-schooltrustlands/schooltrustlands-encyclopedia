# Chapter 3 — A Century of Drift, 1859–1990

The architecture I walked you through in Chapter 2 is the doctrine. This chapter is the operating record. It is the answer to a question every reader of this book is going to ask sooner or later: if Oregon's Common School Fund was constitutionally walled off, exclusively applied to schools, named irreducible, and protected by a board of three statewide elected officers, *how did the corpus ever shrink in the first place?*

The short answer, which the long answer below will fill in, is that doctrine and discipline are different things. Oregon held the doctrine through the 1859 admission, the disposal era of 1880 to 1910, the Progressive-era land-fraud prosecutions, the 1968 modernization amendment, and the 1992 Attorney General opinion. The discipline lapsed for the first fifty years. It mostly held for the next eighty. And then, in the 1990s and after, it lapsed again — at which point we move into the modern Elliott story that is the subject of the rest of this book.

Two arithmetic facts are worth pinning to the front of this chapter, because they bracket the period in question. In 1907, Governor George Chamberlain put on the legislative record his estimate that *"the irreducible school fund might have been five or six times as large as it is at present"* if the State had managed its school lands honestly. That is the cleanest period measurement of drift-by-attrition I have found in any state's record. And yet, by 1990, the Elliott State Forest was producing what Bob Zybach has documented as roughly 50 million board feet of timber sales per year for thirty consecutive years, generating hundreds of millions of dollars to Oregon schools. For 80 years between 1910 and 1990, Oregon's State Land Board mostly did the trustee's job — and the Elliott was the working asset that funded it.

Both halves of that record are real. Both halves matter. Chapter 4 is going to take up the moment the institution begins to harvest itself. This chapter is the run-up.

## F. G. Young's *unwavering aim*

The most thorough financial history of Oregon's first half-century of school-lands administration was written in 1910 by Frederick George Young, the University of Oregon's first professor of economics, in the *Oregon Historical Quarterly*.[^ch3-1] Young was not a polemicist. He was an economist working through the State's land-disposal records year by year. The sentence I want to start the chapter with is his:

> "It has been Oregon's unwavering aim to get its state lands as rapidly as possible into private hands and under cultivation ... every acre as soon as the state had title, and quite too frequently even when it had only slight basis for expecting to secure it, was on the market."[^ch3-2]

I want you to register the gap between that sentence and the constitutional text I quoted in Chapter 2. *Separate, irreducible, exclusively applied.* That was the doctrine. *Unwavering aim to get its state lands as rapidly as possible into private hands.* That was the operating disposition. The constitution said the school lands were a perpetual endowment to be held in trust for common schools. The State's actual administrative posture, for Oregon's first fifty years, was to convert the endowment into private title as fast as legally possible — and faster than legally possible, when the State could get away with it.

The reason this matters is that an "unwavering aim" is not drift in the usual sense. Drift implies inattention. Drift implies a slow loss to entropy. What Young was diagnosing was attention of a particular kind: a sustained institutional preference for liquidating the trust corpus in service of an immediate political economy that valued private settlement and timber extraction over future schoolchildren. The men running the State knew the doctrine. They sold the lands anyway.

Young also explained, in a sentence I think most school-trust analysts have under-quoted, *how* the disposal era worked at the operational level. The constitutional Land Board — the Governor, the Secretary of State, the State Treasurer — were also performing the duties of their primary offices, which "expanded to engross [their] attention," and the school-land function in operative practice "passed to an irresponsible 'clerk,'" whose offices the speculators learned to navigate.[^ch3-3] The men whose constitutional seats put them in charge of the trust were not, in the operational sense, doing the work. The work passed to the clerk. The clerk could be reached.

This is one of the load-bearing structural lessons of the Oregon record, and it is one Margaret Bird has been making to NASTL audiences for years. Wisconsin, in 1848, named its constitutional Land Board members differently — including the Attorney General, whose office gave him a stronger institutional incentive to enforce the trust against the speculators. Oregon named the Governor, the Secretary of State, and the Treasurer. All three are political offices with primary duties that engross their attention. The trust function fell where the political incentives were weakest. That is not an accident. It is a consequence of a textual choice the framers made. The choice has held through 167 years and is binding now on Tina Kotek, on LaVonne Griffin-Valade, and on Tobias Read.

## The Infamous Act of 1887

Oregon's disposal era had several legislative milestones, but the one Young singled out for special condemnation — and the one most worth knowing if you only learn one fact about the disposal era — is the 1887 statute he labeled the *Infamous Act*.[^ch3-4]

Before 1887, Oregon's school-lands sales had at least nominally proceeded by auction, with a minimum price set by statute (raised to $2.00 per acre in 1878) and certificates of sale that, in theory, allowed the Land Board to maximize the proceeds against bidders. The 1887 act removed the auction mechanism and required the State Land Board to sell remaining state lands at the uniform price of *$1.25 per acre*. The act simultaneously expanded non-settler purchase limits to 320 acres.

Read what just happened. The legislature, in 1887, told the State Land Board that it could no longer accept higher bids. It had to sell at $1.25. The standing timber on much of the school-lands corpus was at that moment worth fifty to a hundred times that price on national markets. The 320-acre limit for non-settlers meant that the speculators who had been buying through dummy applications could now buy more land at lower prices, in their own names, under a uniform-price rule that took the auction's price-discovery function off the table entirely.

The act, in other words, did not just remove a defense. It instituted a structural transfer from future schoolchildren to present timber speculators, written into Oregon's statute books and signed by the governor of the day. Young called it Infamous because that is what it was. The school-lands disposal that followed across the next two decades was the implementation of an act of legislative theft — not the kind of theft that rises to a felony indictment in real time, but the kind that is documented in the trust corpus arithmetic Governor Chamberlain put on the record twenty years later.

I want to be careful here about what Young's diagnosis does and does not say. The Infamous Act of 1887 was not the cause of the Oregon land-fraud trials of 1903–1910 — those trials prosecuted federal-land schemes that ran in parallel to the state school-lands disposal, and the legal architecture was different. But the Infamous Act and the federal-land fraud schemes shared a political ecology. They both flourished in a state where the political economy valued rapid private acquisition of public lands and where the institutional defenses against speculator capture were thin. Where one ran, the other was likely to run too.

## The federal land-fraud trials, 1904–1910

The Oregon land-fraud trials of 1904 to 1910 are the most documented body of federal-criminal record produced by a school-trust failure anywhere in the public-land states.[^ch3-5] I do not know of another comparable record. The trials prosecuted, mostly under federal homestead and timber-and-stone law, schemes by which speculators and complicit federal officials had conspired to convert federal public-domain lands — including but not limited to school-grant lands — into private title through fraudulent applications, dummy entrymen, and bribery of General Land Office officials.

Special Assistant U.S. Attorney General Francis J. Heney, brought in to lead the prosecutions, secured indictments from a Portland federal grand jury that ultimately reached nearly a hundred people. The defendants included former General Land Office Commissioner Binger Hermann, U.S. Representative John N. Williamson, U.S. Attorney John Hall, and — most spectacularly — sitting United States Senator John H. Mitchell.

Mitchell was tried in Portland in 1905 for using his Senate office to aid a law-firm client in obtaining patents to fraudulent land claims. His own law partner, Albert H. Tanner, and his secretary, Harry C. Robertson, testified against him. The jury convicted him on July 3, 1905. He was sentenced to six months in jail and a $1,000 fine. He appealed. He died on December 8, 1905, before his appeal could be heard.[^ch3-6]

Mitchell remains one of the very few sitting United States senators ever convicted of a felony. As far as the historical record marks it, he is the only sitting U.S. senator to die awaiting appeal of such a conviction. The fact deserves to be stated cleanly: a sitting U.S. senator, convicted in federal court of federal crimes connected to Oregon land schemes, died in office in 1905 before the criminal record could be tested on appeal.

Stephen A. Douglas Puter, the self-styled "King of the Oregon Land Fraud Ring," wrote his confessional memoir, *Looters of the Public Domain*, from his federal jail cell. The book was published in 1907. It names the methods and the men.[^ch3-7]

The Marion County grand jury, working at the state level on parallel concerns, reported in April 1905 that of one batch of land applications totaling about 500,000 acres, nearly all were *"fraudulent and should be fully investigated by your body before deeds are issued."*[^ch3-8]

I want to mark, as a structural matter, that the Oregon record produced something the rest of the public-land states' records mostly did not produce: federal-criminal accountability for school-land fraud during the disposal era. Twenty-one convictions of state and federal officials. A sitting U.S. senator dead in disgrace. A confessional memoir from the ringleader. The Oregon federal courts answered the directed-seizure schemes more vigorously than any other state in this cohort.

What the record also tells you, though, is that the prosecutions stopped what was on the indictment table. They did not retrieve the corpus. The acres were already in private hands. The schools did not get them back.

## Chamberlain's 1907 measurement

The cleanest period measurement of how much the disposal era had cost the schools comes from Governor George Chamberlain's 1907 message to the Twenty-fourth Legislative Assembly.[^ch3-9] Chamberlain, working from the State's own land-disposal records and the same kind of arithmetic Young would do more thoroughly three years later, told the legislature in plain English what had happened:

> "But for the policies which have been adopted by the Legislature from time to time with respect to its school lands, the irreducible school fund might have been five or six times as large as it is at present."

I want to slow down at that sentence. As a piece of fiduciary self-diagnosis from the chief executive of a state trustee, it is unusual. As a numerical claim, it is precise enough to mean something. Chamberlain is saying that Oregon's school endowment, after a half-century of state administration, was somewhere between sixteen and twenty percent of what the architecture should have produced if it had been honored. *Eighty cents on every dollar* had gone somewhere else.

Eighty cents on every dollar.

If you want a single number to carry forward from this chapter into the rest of the book, that is the number. It is the period measurement of drift-by-attrition by a constitutional trustee, working from the State's own books, in the second-to-last year of the disposal era. It is the arithmetic that the 1992 Oregon Attorney General opinion's "obligations are binding" sentence was correcting against the next century. And it is the arithmetic the Elliott State Forest divestiture of 2017 to 2022 has, in different language and at different dollar magnitudes, started running again.

## The stewardship interval, 1910–1990

The Oregon school-trust record is not all disposal, and the part of the record that is not disposal is also worth getting on the page.

After the federal-land trials concluded around 1910, after the Infamous Act's worst effects had run their course, after Young's 1910 diagnosis and Chamberlain's 1907 measurement had entered the public record, the State of Oregon's school-trust administration went through what I am going to call the stewardship interval — eighty years between 1910 and 1990 in which the architecture mostly held and the trust corpus mostly performed the function it was designed to perform.

The stewardship interval has several visible features.

First, the Elliott State Forest itself was assembled. In 1927, Congress authorized an exchange in which scattered Common School Fund parcels (acreage left over from the disposal era's chaos) were swapped for a contiguous block from the Siuslaw National Forest in Coos and Douglas Counties.[^ch3-10] The exchange consolidated approximately 83,000 acres of timberland into Oregon's first state forest. The Elliott was established as Oregon's first state forest in 1930.[^ch3-11] The whole point of the consolidation was to give the Common School Fund a working, manageable, revenue-producing asset. The exchange worked. The forest was assembled to do exactly the job that, sixty years later, the State would decide it could no longer be allowed to do.

Second, between 1930 and 1990, the Elliott did exactly what the consolidation had been designed for. Bob Zybach — a forest historian, a former Elliott contractor, and one of the OASTL case's expert declarants — has documented the period record in some detail.[^ch3-12] In Zybach's framing, the Elliott produced something on the order of 50 million board feet of timber sales per year for roughly thirty consecutive years, generating revenues that, cumulatively, totaled in the hundreds of millions of dollars to the benefit of Oregon schools. The numbers were not constant year to year — markets vary, harvest schedules vary, agency choices vary — but the structural pattern was steady. The Elliott was the Common School Fund's working timber asset. It produced revenue. The revenue went to schools.

Third, the constitutional architecture got modernized. The 1968 Article VIII amendment changed the State Land Board's statutory framing from a sale-and-investment board to a land-management board, directing the Board to obtain the greatest benefit for the people of Oregon consistent with sound conservation. The 1988 amendment authorized broader investment of the Common School Fund and permitted the Board to apply certain investment income to operating expenses. Both amendments were ratified by Oregon voters. Both amendments left the *separate, irreducible, exclusively applied* core of Section 2 untouched.[^ch3-13]

Fourth, the Oregon Attorney General's office articulated the trust doctrine cleanly across this interval. The 1975 opinion on exclusive bidding restrictions for Common School timber sales held that the Land Board must obtain full market value and protect the long-term value of the lands. The 1977 opinion on Natural Area Preserves held that non-revenue uses were permissible only when they did not adversely affect the financial contribution to the Fund. The 1989 opinions interpreted the post-1968 architecture. The 1992 opinion concluded that "the school lands granted to the State of Oregon are a trust for the benefit of public education" and that the State's "obligations are binding. They cannot be disregarded." The doctrine, on the State's own legal record, was correct.[^ch3-14]

I want to be precise about what the stewardship interval was and was not. It was not a period in which the Elliott was managed perfectly. There were controversies, environmental conflicts, agency-management critiques, and the early signals of the species-listing pressures that would arrive in force in the 1990s. The Marbled Murrelet was federally listed as threatened in 1992 — within the interval but at its very end. The Northern Spotted Owl was listed in 1990. Both listings would, within a decade, produce litigation that would sharply reduce harvestable acreage on the Elliott and trigger the institutional reframing Chapter 4 takes up.

But across the central span of the stewardship interval — call it 1930 to 1985 — Oregon's State Land Board mostly did the trustee's job. The Elliott produced revenue. The Common School Fund grew. The doctrine and the discipline were, if not perfectly aligned, at least running on the same track.

## What ended the interval

The stewardship interval did not end with a single decision. There was no moment, between 1985 and 1995, when a Land Board chose to abandon the trust posture. What happened instead, and what Chapter 4 is going to walk through in operational detail, was an institutional reframing that built up gradually and then settled, sometime in the mid-1990s, into a new working assumption about what the Elliott was *for*.

The reframing was facilitated by external pressure — the 1990 Spotted Owl listing, the 1992 Marbled Murrelet listing, the species-litigation that followed, the Habitat Conservation Plan negotiations of the late 1990s and early 2000s, the operational difficulty of harvesting timber on a forest with two listed species. It was facilitated by personnel changes inside the agencies. It was facilitated by a general shift in Oregon's political culture toward conservation framings of state forest land.

What the reframing ended up producing, by the early 2010s, was a State Land Board that had come to view the Elliott not as the Common School Fund's working asset but as a net drain on the Fund — defensible inside the agency's own operational frame, structurally a capture of the trust by the trustees' own institutional interests. The argument that the Elliott "costs more than it produces" was deployed with increasing frequency. The argument was, on the agency's books, true at the moment it was being made. It was also a moral hazard masquerading as accounting: if the trustees can declare a trust asset uneconomical and dispose of it on that basis, the trustee duty has been redefined out of recognition.

Chapter 4 is what happens next. The 1990s capture. The institutional reframing of the trust. The structural conditions that made the 2017 sale-and-reverse and the 2019 bond substitution possible. The handoff in this book is from the architecture-and-doctrine record to the modern operational record — from a century of mostly-doctrinal-fidelity to two decades of accelerating departure from it.

The doctrine, all through, has stayed where it was. The discipline is what shifts.
