# Chapter 11 — The Smoking Gun and the Discovery Stonewall

I want to start this chapter with a sentence that, when I read it for the first time on the evening of October 24, 2025, I had to read three times to make sure my eyes were not playing tricks on me.

In a pleading filed in the Circuit Court of the State of Oregon for the County of Coos, in *Siuslaw School District 97J et al. v. State of Oregon et al.*, case number 24CV38372, the State of Oregon — by and through the Oregon Department of Justice, on behalf of the State Land Board, the Governor, the Treasurer, and the Secretary of State — formally asserted as a defense to a complaint brought by school districts and schoolchildren of the State of Oregon that the State *"does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."*

The State of Oregon, asked in court in October 2025 whether it owes the duties of a trustee to its schoolchildren, answered no. The reader of this book now knows what the case is really about.

Let me walk you through what that means.

## I. The pleading and the doctrine it contradicts

The State's Answer in 24CV38372, filed October 24, 2025, denies the First Amended Complaint's allegations that the State holds the Common School Fund and the trust lands that fund it in a fiduciary capacity as a trustee, and that the State owes the schoolchildren of Oregon the duties any trustee owes a beneficiary. The pleading does not finesse the question. It does not say *we owe some of those duties but not others.* It does not say *we have been performing those duties and the plaintiffs are wrong about the breach.* It says, in the operative phrase now on the public record, that the State *does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged.*

Hold that against what the law actually is.

The Oregon Admissions Act of February 14, 1859 conveys sections 16 and 36 in every township of public lands in Oregon "to said State for the use of schools." The federal grant uses the technical phrase "for the use of schools" — *use of* is the language the common law of trusts uses to identify a beneficial interest. Article VIII Section 2 of the Oregon Constitution creates the Common School Fund as "a separate, and irreducible fund" the interest of which "shall be exclusively applied to the support and maintenance of common schools." *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden* (1931) held the relationship "a trust of the highest nature." Attorney General Opinion 8223, issued July 24, 1992, called the State Land Board's role one of "trustees in the full common-law sense." The 2003 AG opinion re-stated the same. Article VIII Section 8, the 2000 amendment, makes the duty enforceable at law.

I am not relying on my own reading of the doctrine here. I am relying on the Oregon Supreme Court, the Oregon Court of Appeals, two sitting Attorneys General across an eleven-year span, and the legislature when it passed the constitutional amendment in 2000.

The State of Oregon, in October 2025, told a court of the State of Oregon that it does not owe those duties to the children of Oregon's schools. That is not a litigation flourish. That is the State's institutional self-conception, finally written down where the public can read it.

## II. What Margaret Bird had said three years earlier

I want to take you back to December 11, 2022, because Margaret Bird had written the State a letter that, in a single sentence, predicted what the State was going to do on October 24, 2025 — and the State did exactly what Margaret had said it would do.

The setting: the State Land Board was scheduled to meet on December 13, 2022, to vote on a transaction that Department of State Lands staff had been calling a "decoupling" of the Elliott from the Common School Fund. The transaction was structured as a sale — the Common School Fund would sell the Elliott to a newly-created Elliott State Research Forest Authority — at a price the State's own appraiser, Mason Bruce & Girard, had set at $99.6 million. The price had been styled an "investment value" calculation rather than a market-value calculation. The buyer was an Oregon state entity established by the legislature in March 2022 for the purpose of receiving the asset.

Margaret had been writing letters to State Land Boards for forty years by 2022. By the time she sat down on December 11, 2022 to write the Oregon State Land Board, she had personally led the institutional work that brought Utah's annual school-trust distribution from under $5 million to over $113 million. She is the foremost living authority on what these trusts are and how they fail.

Her December 11 letter to the Oregon State Land Board was plain-spoken. The Board was about to "decouple" the Elliott. Margaret used a sentence that has become the rhetorical centerpiece of every subsequent piece of beneficiary-side litigation in Oregon:

> *Decoupling is a euphemism for selling.*

The phrase did the work of a paragraph. The State, after 2017, had developed a vocabulary that performed the sale without naming it. "Decoupling." "Investment value." "Research forest." "Children's Forest." Margaret cut through it in seven words.

The letter went further. Margaret explained the difference between investment value and fair-market value using a Ford pickup analogy. Imagine, she wrote, that you owned a Ford pickup truck and the State decided it wanted to take ownership of it — compensating you not at what the truck would fetch in the market, but at the price the State was willing to pay under whatever methodology the State had chosen. *The price the State is willing to pay rather than what it would fetch in the market.* That, she wrote, was what "investment value" meant.

She gave the Board the numbers. The State's $99.6 million "investment value." Independent market-value estimates from Bill Lansing, retired CEO of Menasha Forest Products; from John Beuter's 1994 ODF-commissioned analysis; from forester-investors with decades of experience — running from $1 billion to $1.3 billion. The Board was being asked to dispose of trust property at less than ten percent of its market value.

The letter named the doctrine. Self-dealing. The State Land Board, as the corpus's trustee, was selling the corpus to a buyer — the Elliott State Research Forest Authority — that was itself a creature of the State. The Board sat on both sides of the transaction. Under common-law trust doctrine, as the *Restatement (Third) of Trusts* and the Oregon Uniform Trust Code both make explicit, a trustee cannot sell to itself. The structure was self-dealing on its face, with the price discount being the trustee's compensation for arranging the transaction in favor of the institution rather than the beneficiary.

Margaret's letter told the State Land Board, on the record, what the Board was about to do and why it was a breach of trust.

The State Land Board met on December 13, 2022. The Board voted to proceed. The letter was on the record. The doctrine was on the record. The market-value evidence was on the record. The Board ignored every word of it.

Three years later, when OASTL filed the case that put the same allegations in front of a court of competent jurisdiction, the State — through its own Department of Justice — answered that it did not owe the duties Margaret's letter had spelled out. The October 24, 2025 Answer is not a litigation accident. It is the institutional position the State held in December 2022, in 2019, in 2017, and in 1992 when its own Attorney General told it otherwise — finally written down where a court has to read it.

## III. The first request for production

After the January 28, 2026 Court of Appeals opinion confirmed that the schoolchildren-plaintiffs had standing, the OASTL legal team moved into the discovery phase of 24CV38372.

On **January 28, 2026** — by deliberate timing on the same day the Court of Appeals opinion was issued — Daniel Crowe served on the State of Oregon **Plaintiffs' First Request for Production of Documents.** The RFPs asked, in language any trust lawyer would recognize as routine, for the records of the trust's management. Trust accounting records. Financial ledgers. Internal communications regarding the Elliott decoupling. The CSF distribution mechanism documentation. The decision-making records on the management of the corpus over the period the breach claims cover.

These are not unusual requests. A beneficiary asking a trustee for the trust's books is the most basic interaction in trust law. *Wood v. Honeyman*, 178 Or. 484 (1946), established that the trustee's duty to provide "full and accurate accounts" to the beneficiary is "paramount" — substantive, not procedural, not waivable. The Restatement says the same. The Oregon Uniform Trust Code, ORS 130.650 through 130.710, says the same.

The State of Oregon, served with a routine first request for production of trust-accounting documents, on **February 27, 2026**, served its Response.

The Response, in the language Daniel's later Motion to Compel describes, consisted "largely of 'General Objections.'"

A "General Objection," in Oregon civil procedure, is a paragraph at the top of a discovery response that asserts a set of broad reservations — attorney-client privilege, work product, public-officer privilege under OEC 509, vagueness, overbreadth, burden, proportionality — and then applies them to every request below without responding substantively to any. ORCP 43 B requires that an objecting party "state with particularity the grounds for objecting." A blanket "General Objection" is not particularized. It is the procedural form of refusing to participate in discovery, dressed up in the vocabulary of an objection.

The State did produce some material. The Common School Fund Annual Financial Report for the fiscal year ended June 30, 2025 — a 37-page document the State publishes annually regardless of any litigation. The CSF Fact Sheet, two pages. The Legislative Fiscal Office 2025-27 Budget Review of the Department of State Lands, seven pages, also a publicly-available document. Two one-page Excel ledgers — "Siuslaw_DSL_000001.xlsx" labeled "DSL Statutory Investment Sale to Cover Operating Expenses" and "Siuslaw_DSL_000004.xlsx" labeled "Rev Exp Const."

That is the entirety of what the trustee of the school-trust gave the beneficiaries' counsel when the beneficiaries' counsel asked for the trust's books.

What the State did *not* provide was the substantive trust-accounting data — the native digital ledgers that would let the plaintiffs reconcile the CSF's distributions against the management's decisions over the relevant period. What the State did not provide were the internal communications, the directives, the memos, the meeting notes — the decision-records that would let a plaintiff see *why* the trustee had done what the public-facing reports show the trustee did. *The raw numbers but not the interpretive key,* as Daniel's later motion put it. The "what" without the "why."

In Daniel's words, in the motion that followed: *"Producing the 'what' (the raw numbers) while hiding the 'why' (the internal directives) is a tactical attempt to starve this litigation of the evidence needed to prove a breach of fiduciary duty."*

That sentence is what the discovery stonewall actually consists of.

## IV. The motion to compel

Daniel did what an experienced litigator does when the defendant's discovery response is procedurally deficient. He conferred. He invoked Uniform Trial Court Rule 5.010, which requires good-faith conferral before going to the court. He wrote to opposing counsel Christina Beatty-Walters at the Oregon Department of Justice and requested that the State withdraw its General Objections and produce the native Excel ledgers, the LFO budget data, and the responsive internal communications identified in RFPs 4 through 7. The State refused.

On **April 21, 2026**, Daniel prepared **Plaintiffs' Motion to Compel Production of Documents** along with his **Declaration in Support.** The Motion walked the court through what *Wood v. Honeyman* and ORCP 36 B and the September 5, 2025 Letter Opinion (which had already ruled that common-law trust principles govern the State's fiduciary duties in this matter) plainly required. The State, having been adjudicated a fiduciary, had a substantive duty to produce trust-accounting records. The Court of Appeals on January 28, 2026 had already ruled that CSF management has "practical effects" on school funding, so the records were inherently relevant. The State's "General Objections" were procedurally deficient under ORCP 43 B(2). The State had waived any claim of privilege by failing to provide a privilege log as required by ORCP 36 C.

The Motion to Compel also surfaced a substantive point that I want to make sure you do not miss. The State's distribution mechanism for the Common School Fund, codified at ORS 327.008, classifies CSF distributions as "local revenue" for purposes of the State School Fund formula. Because that formula offsets state contributions dollar-for-dollar by "local revenue," every dollar the Common School Fund pays out reduces the State's general-fund obligation to schools by an equal dollar. As Daniel put the point: *"By classifying CSF money as 'local revenue,' the State uses the Trust to offset its own General Fund obligations dollar-for-dollar. This turns the Constitutional Trust into a 'General Fund Subsidy,' which is a per se breach of the duty of undivided loyalty. The State has effectively made itself the primary beneficiary of its own 'management' of the Trust."*

The Motion and Declaration were formally filed with the Coos County Circuit Court on **May 2, 2026**, following the April 21–28 conferral period and standard rules-compliance work. The State's response will follow under the court's schedule. Oral argument has been requested.

## V. What the discovery stonewall actually is

The State of Oregon, in February 2026, refused to give a beneficiary's counsel the records of the trust the State holds for that beneficiary. The State's refusal was not the kind of routine discovery dispute that gets resolved by a phone call between counsel. It was the State institutionally taking the position that it is not obligated, as a matter of law, to account for what it has done with the Common School Fund. That is the same position the October 24, 2025 Answer asserted. The same position the State Land Board took on December 13, 2022 when it voted, in the face of Margaret's letter, to sell the Elliott to itself for less than ten percent of its market value. The same position the Department of State Lands took when it refused Bob Zybach's December 2019 and April 2020 records requests. The same position the agency has taken at every step since the 1990s capture.

The discovery stonewall is not a litigation tactic in any narrow sense. It is the State of Oregon's institutional self-conception — that the Common School Fund is the State's property, to be managed in service of whatever the State considers its priorities, with the schoolchildren as a downstream recipient of whatever the State decides to let pass through to them — finally rendered into the language of a court pleading. The reason the State will not produce the trust accounting is that the State does not believe a trust-accounting duty exists, because the State does not believe it is a trustee in the way the doctrine says it is.

The smoking gun is not a leaked memo. It is on the public record of the Coos County Circuit Court, in a pleading the State filed on October 24, 2025, in language any reader can verify.

Margaret Bird, in her December 11, 2022 letter, predicted the institutional posture that produced the October 24, 2025 Answer. The State ignored her. Three years later, the State told a court of the State of Oregon that the duties Margaret had spelled out do not, in the State's own institutional view, exist.

What is at stake, in dollar terms, in what the State is asserting it does not owe — that is the next chapter. The accounting comes due.
