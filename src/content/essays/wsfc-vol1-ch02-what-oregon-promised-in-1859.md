# Chapter 2 — What Oregon Promised in 1859

I want to start this chapter with something that matters more than most readers know. Oregon entered the Union with one of the strongest school-trust architectures in the country, and the doctrinal record across the next 165 years is that everyone who has held office knew it.

Hold that sentence in your head while we walk through the documents. Because the question this whole book asks — *how did the Elliott end up where it is now, and how is the State of Oregon arguing it does not owe the duties the law plainly imposes?* — only makes sense if you first see what was promised. Oregon's promises in 1859, in 1931, in 1975, in 1977, in 1992, in 2003, and again in 2000 are unusually clear. The breach is what is being diagnosed, not the doctrine.

## The federal promise: the doubled grant

Congress admitted Oregon to the Union on February 14, 1859, by an act that ran fewer than a thousand words.[^ch2-1] Tucked into Section 4 of that act is the federal half of the trust:

> "Sections numbered sixteen and thirty-six in every township of public lands in said State, and where either of said sections, or any part thereof, has been disposed of, other lands equivalent thereto, in legal subdivisions of not less than one quarter section, and as contiguous as may be, shall be granted to said State for the use of schools."

That single clause did something the public-land system had taken seventy years to work up to. The Northwest Ordinance template that Ohio entered under in 1802 had given the new states one section per township — section 16 — for school support. Wisconsin, Iowa, and the rest of the Old Northwest cohort all got the single-section grant. California in 1853 was the first state to receive sections 16 *and* 36. Minnesota in 1857 received the same doubling and is generally credited as the enabling-act template. Oregon in 1859 received the doubled grant on the inherited Minnesota text.[^ch2-2]

The doubling matters because it told you something about how Congress understood the western public-land territories. The drier the country and the more variable the terrain, the more sections you needed to give to the schools to produce a workable trust corpus. Oregon's roughly 3.4 million acres of school-trust land at admission — sections 16 and 36 of every surveyed township, plus indemnity lands where one of those sections had already been disposed of — was the largest school-land grant any state had received up to that point.[^ch2-3] By the standard of the federal template, Oregon entered the Union ahead.

## The state promise: Article VIII

Oregon went further than the federal text required. The state's framers, meeting in 1857, wrote a school-trust architecture into Article VIII of the Oregon Constitution that I will spend most of this chapter walking through. There are three sections that matter to this book.

**Article VIII, Section 2** sets up the Common School Fund. The constitutional text — preserved in its 1859 form for over a century, before the modernization amendments of 1968 and 1988 — reads as follows:

> "The proceeds of all the lands which have been, or hereafter may be granted to this State, for educational purposes ... shall be set apart as a separate, and irreducible fund, to be called the Common School Fund, the interest of which together with all other revenues derived from the school land mentioned in this section shall be exclusively applied to the support and maintenance of common schools in each school district, and the purchase of suitable libraries and apparatus therefor."[^ch2-4]

I want you to read that twice. *Separate.* *Irreducible.* *Exclusively applied.* Three operative words, each of which the framers chose deliberately. *Separate* means the school fund is not the general fund and cannot be commingled with it. *Irreducible* means the corpus does not get spent down — only the interest is available, and the corpus stands forever. *Exclusively applied* means the interest does not get diverted to other public purposes, even purposes that sound like they help kids.

The constitutional amendments in 1968 and 1988 modernized the Common School Fund's investment authority — the 1968 amendment changed Section 5 from a sale-and-investment board to a land-management board "directed to obtain the greatest benefit for the people consistent with sound conservation," and the 1988 amendment authorized stock investment and certain operating-expense use.[^ch2-5] The amendments did not soften the *separate, irreducible, exclusively applied* core. That core is still on the books. It is still binding. It is what the State of Oregon is arguing, in the case this book documents, that it does not owe.

**Article VIII, Section 5** names the trustee board. This is the textual choice the Oregon framers made that most other states did not, and it is a good one:

> "The Governor, Secretary of State, and Treasurer of State shall constitute a Board of Commissioners, for the sale of school, and university lands, and for the investment of the funds arising therefrom, and their powers, and duties shall be such as may be prescribed by law."[^ch2-6]

Three constitutional ex-officio trustees: the Governor, the Secretary of State, the State Treasurer. The State Land Board. Not a statutory board the legislature can abolish, restructure, or starve of funding. A board the framers wrote into the constitution itself, with three of the state's most senior elected officers sitting as fiduciaries by virtue of holding the offices to which they were elected.

A textual choice in 1857 binds the office-holders of 2026. Tina Kotek sits on the State Land Board because she is Governor. LaVonne Griffin-Valade sits because she is Secretary of State. Tobias Read sits because he is Treasurer. The seats they fill are constitutional seats. They are not optional. The duties that attach to those seats are not optional either, no matter what the State of Oregon's October 24, 2025 Answer in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* says.[^ch2-7]

**Article VIII, Section 8**, added by initiative in November 2000, completed the architecture for the litigation that arrived a quarter-century later. Section 8 is the Adequate and Equitable Funding clause: it requires the legislature to appropriate, in each biennium, sufficient funds for "a system of public education that meets quality goals established by law," or to issue a report explaining why it has not.[^ch2-8] Section 8 is not a school-lands provision in its primary text. It is an education-funding-adequacy provision.

Why does it matter to a book about a forest? Because Section 8 is what gave the OASTL plaintiffs constitutional standing to sue. On January 28, 2026, the Oregon Court of Appeals reversed the trial court's dismissal of the OASTL case in a published opinion, *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, 346 Or. App. 668 (2026). The court held, among other things, that the schoolchildren plaintiffs had pleaded a practical stake — Common School Fund distributions plausibly affect what the legislature has to appropriate under Section 8, and so the trust-lands claim was not barred from the courthouse for want of standing.[^ch2-9] The doctrinal line the court drew runs straight from a constitutional provision adopted twenty-six years earlier by initiative through to a 2026 case that has now pushed the State into discovery in Coos County. The architecture worked. The constitutional architecture, when finally tested by the right plaintiffs in front of the right appellate panel, produced standing. That is not a small thing in a state where the trust-lands plaintiffs had been turned away for almost two centuries.

## The Oregon Supreme Court's word: *trust of the highest nature*

The legislative branch and the framers were clear. So was the judicial branch. In 1931, the Oregon Supreme Court decided *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden*. The case had to do with whether an irrigation district could levy assessments on Common School Fund lands. The court's answer was in part technical, but the doctrinal sentence the case is now cited for is not.

The Oregon Supreme Court called the Common School Fund a *"trust of the highest nature."*[^ch2-10]

I want to slow down at that phrase. It is not a phrase the Oregon Supreme Court used carelessly. It is a phrase that places the Common School Fund inside the highest-duty category of the trust-doctrine universe — the same category as a guardian-and-ward trust, the same category as a court-supervised conservatorship for a person who cannot protect their own interests. The Oregon Supreme Court was saying, in 1931, that the State's duties to the Common School Fund are not contractual duties. They are not regulatory duties. They are fiduciary duties of the strictest sort the law recognizes. *Trust of the highest nature.* The State of Oregon's lawyers know that opinion. It has not been overruled.

## The Attorney General's word, four times

The Oregon Department of Justice has issued at least six Attorney General opinions touching the Common School Fund's trust character since 1975. Two of them are load-bearing for this book.

In 1977, the Oregon Attorney General was asked whether income-producing Common School Grant Land could be designated as a Natural Area Preserve under ORS 273.562. The opinion answered: yes — but only if the designation would *not* adversely affect the financial contribution of that land to the Common School Trust Fund.[^ch2-11] In operational terms, the AG said no. A non-revenue use of trust land is permissible only when it does not reduce the trust's revenue. The opinion is forty-five years old in 2022, and its operational rule is the rule the 2019 bond substitution and the 2022 ESRF/OSU plan have to be measured against.

In 1992, the Oregon Attorney General issued what is now the central trust-doctrine opinion in the Oregon record, restating and reinforcing it again in 2003. The 1992 opinion, asked what obligations govern management of Oregon's Admission Act lands after the 1968 amendment to Article VIII, Section 5, concluded that Oregon's acceptance of the federal grant *imposed binding obligations*: Oregon must use those lands for schools and not for inconsistent purposes; management must conserve the corpus and obtain full market value from sale, rental, or use; non-school uses are permissible only if consistent with trust purposes and long-term Common School Fund benefit.[^ch2-12]

The 1992 AG opinion's operative sentences are worth quoting in full, because they are what the State's own legal officers have said the State owes:

> "The school lands granted to the State of Oregon are a trust for the benefit of public education. ... The State's obligations are binding. They cannot be disregarded. Oregon must use [Admission Act lands] consistent with the trust."[^ch2-13]

That is the State of Oregon, by its own Attorney General, in 1992. It was the State of Oregon, again by its own Attorney General, in 2003. It is the doctrinal posture the State of Oregon's own Department of Justice maintained for forty-eight years before the State filed the *Advocates for School Trust Lands* Answer in October 2025 and pivoted to "does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."

I will leave the reader, for now, to register the size of that pivot. Chapter 11 will document it directly.

## What everyone in office has known

Margaret Bird, in her foreword to this volume, frames Oregon's record one way: the strongest constitutional architecture in the country, paired with the worst documented disposal record in the country. That is a claim grounded in forty years inside the school-trust field. I will let her sentence stand and not try to improve on it.

What I want to note from inside the case is something narrower. The men and women who have held the constitutional trustee seats in Oregon — the Governor, the Secretary of State, the State Treasurer — have not had the option of pleading ignorance of the trust character of the Common School Fund. The Oregon Supreme Court told them in 1931. The Oregon Attorney General told them in 1975, in 1977, in 1989, in 1992, and in 2003. The Oregon framers told them in the constitutional text the trustees took an oath to uphold. The federal Admission Act told them in language Congress chose deliberately to bind the new state. F. G. Young, the University of Oregon economist who wrote the most thorough financial history of Oregon's school-lands disposal in 1910, told them — and I will quote him in the next chapter — that the operating disposition of the State, for its first half-century, was the exact opposite of what the architecture required.

The architecture said: *separate, irreducible, exclusively applied, trust of the highest nature, binding, cannot be disregarded.* The doctrinal record across 165 years says everyone who has held office knew it.

The Elliott State Forest case is what happens when a state with that record decides — not all at once, not by a single bad actor, but in the slow accumulation of small institutional choices the next chapter walks through — that the architecture is something it can work around.

## Where the next chapter goes

The architecture is in place by 1859. The doctrine is articulated cleanly by 1931 and again in 1975, 1977, 1992, and 2003. Article VIII, Section 8 is added in 2000. The constitutional ex-officio trustees take their oaths in every administration. And yet, across the same span, the Oregon school-trust corpus did not behave like a corpus held under a trust of the highest nature. It behaved like a corpus held by a state in a hurry to convert it.

F. G. Young saw what was happening in 1910 and wrote it down. Governor George Chamberlain saw it in 1907 and put a number on it. The federal courts of the District of Oregon saw it from 1903 to 1910 and prosecuted it. The next chapter takes up the century of drift between 1859 and 1990 — the disposal era, the land-fraud trials, and the long stewardship interval that followed, when the architecture mostly held and the Elliott was the working asset that funded the schools.

Drift, in Oregon, is not a metaphor. It is a documented arithmetic fact. The doctrine was right. The discipline lapsed. That is the story Chapter 3 tells.
