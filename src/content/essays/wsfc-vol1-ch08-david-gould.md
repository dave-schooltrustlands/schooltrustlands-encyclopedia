# Chapter 8 — David Gould

> *"Dave, you are a true beaver and work hard keeping the water flowing over the dam."*
>
> — David Gould, in an email to the author, August 3, 2021

I am going to slow this book down for a chapter.

The story I have been telling, up to this point, has moved in the cadence of documents. A FOIA response. A lookout-tower refusal letter. An email about a secret meeting. A bond issuance. A page-28 paragraph. The cadence has been the cadence of the case — one piece of paper after another, each one a step in the long architecture of how a trust gets converted into something other than itself. The case requires that cadence. The case is, finally, a case about documents.

But the case has also been the work of a small number of people who showed up to do it, year after year, mostly without pay, mostly without praise, and in one or two instances without living to see it through. David Gould was one of those people. He was the first president of Advocates for School Trust Lands, in 2023; he held the seat I now hold; he died on the first day of 2024, before the Court of Appeals had ruled on standing and before the State of Oregon had filed the October 2025 Answer that, in his absence, made the case he had been making all along. This chapter is for him.

I did not know David well in the way you know someone you have grown up alongside. I knew him in the way you come to know somebody you work with on something that matters. We met by email, then by phone, then occasionally in person at the meetings around the 2022 OSU plan. He was twelve years older than I was. He was a North Bend man, and I was a Kings Valley man, and the geographies of the Coast Range that we each knew were on opposite sides of the same compass. But the part of the Coast Range we both came to know — the Elliott State Forest, the working asset of the schoolchildren's trust that the State of Oregon was in the process of taking — was the same. And the walk we were both making toward the same lawsuit, from opposite ends of our two lives, brought us into the same room at the same time.

This is what I learned about him, working with him, and what I have come back to thinking about since he died.

He was born in 1942. He grew up in Coos County, in the country his great-grandparents had homesteaded in 1886, when there was no Elliott State Forest yet and the federal grant land that would eventually become the Elliott was still in the working geography of the cattle and timber economy the Goulds had come west to take part in. His great-grandparents were George and Hattie Gould. They built a ranch on the West Fork of the Millicoma River, in a valley you cannot get to today without a four-wheel-drive truck and a willingness to grind through rotting forest roads. The ranch was called the Elkhorn. His grandfather Clarence grew up on it. His father grew up hearing the stories. He grew up hearing them from both of them. By the time we were trading emails in 2021, he was the fourth generation of his family to have walked that ground, and he had been walking it as a working man — not as a tourist, not as a visiting forester, but as a Coos County boy whose family had been on the same square mile of the eventual Elliott for more than a century.

He played football at North Bend High in the late 1950s. He went to Oregon State on a football scholarship, played on the Liberty Bowl team and on the Rose Bowl team, and earned his way into the OSU athletic hall of fame for it. He took his degree from the College of Natural Resources. He went back to Coos Bay and worked, for most of his adult life, in his family's business, Coos Bay Timber Operators. He retired in 2013. He had a wife. He had children. He had a kitchen table in North Bend at which, after he retired, he began to read more carefully the documents the State of Oregon was producing about the forest his family had walked on for four generations, and at which, eventually, he wrote the letters that drew me to him.

I want to put one of those letters into this chapter.

On November 8, 2021, David Gould wrote to the State Land Board. The OSU College of Forestry's plan to take the Elliott as a research forest was in front of the Board for endorsement. The plan, on its own page 28, was projecting a $2.1 million annual deficit on the trust for the first fifty years. The plan was being marketed as the Elliott State Research Forest, with the *Children's Forest* tagline that the next year's signs would carry. David Gould read the plan. He wrote to the Governor, the Treasurer, and the Secretary of State — the three trustees of the Common School Fund — and he told them, in two sentences that I want you to read as he wrote them:

*I want to advise you that the Forest cannot be decoupled from the Common School Fund without FULL compensation for the Value for the beneficiary.*

The capitalization is his. He wrote the word *FULL* in capital letters because he knew, as a fourth-generation Coos County descendant of homesteaders who had watched the country be transferred and re-transferred under different management theories for a hundred and thirty-five years, that what was about to happen on the Elliott was that the trust would be decoupled from the asset, and that *compensation* would be the word the State used for whatever fraction of the asset's value the State decided, in its capacity as the trustee selling to itself, the trust should receive. He had seen the architecture of the move from inside it. He wrote *FULL* in capitals because he wanted the trustees to read the word and understand that he, David Gould, the man whose great-grandparents had homesteaded that ground, was watching.

The trustees did not respond on the merits. The plan moved forward. The bill that authorized the transfer became law. The marketing of the *Children's Forest* began, and David Gould went home and put on a coat and went out into Coos County to do the work he could do at the scale he could do it at.

In the spring of 2022, he went to a Buster Club meeting at North Bend High School. The Buster Club is the booster club for the North Bend ball teams — the same teams he had played on as a boy. He had been invited, that day, to introduce the softball and baseball teams. He used the introduction to tell the players something about themselves I doubt the school had ever told them.

He wrote to me afterward. The whole note was a few sentences long. He said, in his plain North Bend way:

*I introduced the soft ball team and baseball team to their forest at buster club today. As I expected the students did not know they own 43,000 acres in their Elliott State Children's Forest.*

I have read that note maybe forty times in the years since he sent it. I read it again on the morning I sat down to write this chapter. The students did not know. He told them. He told them in plain English at a Buster Club meeting in the gymnasium of the school he had gone to as a boy. He gave to those ballplayers, that afternoon, the one piece of information no one in the institutional architecture of the State of Oregon had ever bothered to give them: that they owned an asset, and that the asset was theirs, and that the asset was being taken.

That is the work. That is what the case is, when the case is stripped of its filings and its discovery and its citations and its General Objections. The case is the work of telling the beneficiary children that they are the beneficiary, before the trustee finishes the conversion that depends on their not knowing.

David Gould did that work. He did it at Buster Club meetings. He did it in letters to the State Land Board. He did it in school-board appearances in North Bend and Powers, where he asked the local board, in February 2022, to formally approve his taking legal action *for our children* — and where both boards voted yes. He did it in his July 2021 declaration in the first OASTL case, in which he warned the court that the State's mismanagement of the Elliott was setting the forest up for catastrophic fire, and warned that *self-dealing by the state against the interests of Oregon schools are not acceptable behavior for trustees.* He did it in the email he sent to me on August 3, 2021 — five days before he wrote the November letter, three months before the OSU plan moved through its endorsement — in which he said the line that is the epigraph of this chapter:

*Dave, you are a true beaver and work hard keeping the water flowing over the dam.*

I keep that email. The "true beaver" line is a Coos County compliment, the kind of compliment a man like David Gould gave when he wanted you to know he had noticed what you were doing and that he thought you should keep doing it. He was a generous correspondent. He was not, in the way I had been trained to expect of men of his generation, especially formal. He told you what he thought. If he thought you had gotten it right, he said so. If he thought you were going to lose, he said that too, and he stayed with you anyway.

He was the kind of person you build a case around. Not because he was strategic — though he was — but because the local-witness register he carried was the register the case needed. He was the man whose family had been on the ground before the State had been on the ground. He was the local who knew the place better than the agency that claimed to manage it. He was the working-class Coos County voice, in a case that was otherwise going to be carried mostly by professors and lawyers, that made the case audible to the people for whom *professors and lawyers* are a different country. He was the four-generation Oregonian whose presence in the room reminded everyone in the room that this place — the Elliott — had been a real place to real families for as long as Oregon had been a state. The Common School Fund's claim on it was not abstract. It rested on land his great-grandparents had walked.

He went into the role of OASTL President in 2023 because the organization needed somebody to hold the seat, and he was willing to hold it. He was not a movement man by temperament. He was a working-business owner from Coos Bay who had been pulled into civic work by his recognition that the place his family had homesteaded was being taken from the children of the state that had inherited it. He was diligent. He showed up. He answered emails late at night. He read the documents.

I missed the announcement of his death. It was on the first day of 2024. I was traveling, or I was not paying attention to the right inbox, or the email had been routed somewhere I did not check, and by the time I understood that he had fallen at home and that he was gone, the news had been in the world for several days. Bob Zybach wrote to me about the memorial in Coos Bay. I was not in town for it. I did not go.

I am going to be honest in this chapter about that, because the chapter would be wrong if I were not.

I have thought, in the year and a half since, about what it meant that I missed the memorial of a man who had held the seat I now hold, who had written the November 2021 letter that articulated the case's core insight a year before Margaret Bird's letter would articulate it in a different register, and who had spent the last working years of his life telling the schoolchildren of Coos County that they owned a forest the State of Oregon was taking from them. I have thought about it more than I have let on to anyone. The honest answer is that I missed it because the case was moving and I was inside the case and I did not see, that week, that the case had just lost the person who had taught me what its local register sounded like. That is what happens when you are in the inside of something. You miss the funerals.

I am writing this chapter, in part, because the funeral I missed is one I have not yet made up.

David Gould's name is on the OASTL board page now, under *In Memoriam: David Gould (1942–2024), Past President*. The line is a small line on a small website. It does not capture him. What I want this chapter to capture is the work he did, and the four generations of Coos County his work stood on, and the fact that the case I am leading in 2026 is the same case he was making in 2021 — that the Forest cannot be decoupled from the Common School Fund without FULL compensation for the value for the beneficiary, and that the trustee owes the beneficiary the asset, or its full value, or both, and that the beneficiary in this case is the schoolchildren whose ownership of 43,000 acres he announced to the ball teams at Buster Club in the spring of 2022.

The schoolchildren still own those acres. The State of Oregon, in its capacity as their trustee, is still in the process of taking them. David Gould did not live to see the case win on standing in January 2026. He did not live to see the State's October 2025 Answer that "does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged" — the answer that is, in every legal way that matters, the State's response to the November 2021 letter he wrote it. He did not live to see the schoolchildren-plaintiffs sit in the audience at Corvallis High School and watch the State argue, to their faces, that they had no legal interest in the forest he had told them they owned. He missed those scenes. The scenes will go on without him.

What I have come to understand, slowly, is that the work he did before his death is the work that made those scenes possible. The four-generation, local-witness, working-class register he brought to the case is the register that made the case real for the people the case has to be real for if it is going to hold. He gave the case its Coos County floor. He gave the case the man whose great-grandparents had walked the ground. He gave the case the voice that told the ballplayers, in the gymnasium where he had played as a boy, that they owned the forest.

#### The Elkhorn Ranch — A Family History of the Elliott

In 1886, George and Hattie Gould homesteaded on the West Fork of the Millicoma River, in the country that would, forty-four years later, be drawn together into the Elliott State Forest. The Goulds were among the first non-Native families to settle on that fork of the Millicoma. The ranch they built — the Elkhorn — sat in a valley in what is now the geographic heart of the Elliott. The federal homestead record, recorded under George Gould's name, is one of the original title documents in the Coos County books.

The family ran cattle. They cleared bottomland. They built a house and a barn. They raised four generations of Goulds on the ranch. Their first-born son Clarence — David's grandfather — would tell stories, decades later, about life on the ranch in the late nineteenth century: about the West Fork rising in winter, about elk in the meadows, about the rough country uphill from the river. Those stories are recorded in Aileen Barker Rickard's 1983 family history, *The Goulds of Elkhorn*, a self-published volume that is the principal documentary record of the ranch's working years.

In the spring of 1894, a heavy rain set off a landslide above the ranch on Elk Creek. The slide moved enough material to dam the creek. The water backed up into a small lake — known then and now, in the local naming, as Gould Lake. The lake is still there. It is one of the small natural features that marks the family on the land. The 1894 slide is in Bob Zybach's PhD thesis on Coast Range fire and landslide history. It is also in the photographs the Gould descendants preserved — early Elkhorn Ranch images from 1885 to 1894 that are now part of the documentary record of the Elliott's pre-forest era.

When the federal government completed the consolidation that created the Elliott in 1930, much of the Gould homestead was inside the new boundary. The family retained a small parcel. The rest of the ground their great-grandparents had walked passed into the Common School Fund's trust. The four-generation continuity of the family's relationship to the place did not end when the boundary was drawn. It continued through David Gould's father, his uncles, his cousins, and into David's own working life. When he wrote to the State Land Board in November 2021 about the Elliott, he was writing about country his great-grandparents had homesteaded, country his grandfather had told him stories about, country he had walked since boyhood. The case he made was a case about his family's land — and about every other family's land that the schoolchildren's trust now holds.

</aside>

I want to close this chapter with a clean sentence and a turn.

David Gould believed the children of Oregon owned the Elliott State Forest because he had read the 1859 Admissions Act and the 1930 consolidation and the 1968 Common School Fund constitutional amendment and the 2021 OSU plan, and because the chain of title in the public record told him so. He told them so at the Buster Club. He told the State Land Board so in November 2021. He told me so, in his short generous way, in the August 2021 email about the beaver and the dam. He died before the case bore him out. He was right.

The next person to enter this story is a person who, before David died, he and I had begun to mention to each other in our emails. She is from Utah. She had built, over thirty years, the institutional architecture that took her state's school-trust distribution from under five million dollars a year to over a hundred and thirteen million. She had not, until the late summer of 2022, looked closely at Oregon. When she did, she saw what David had seen from inside Coos County and what I had seen from inside Kings Valley, but she saw it with a national authority neither of us could supply.

Her name is Margaret Bird. The next chapter is about what happened when she arrived.
