# Chapter 1 — Why I Bought a Tree Farm

> *"My thirty-three years on the Elliott produced $790 million of revenue for Oregon's Irreducible Common School Fund and supported about 400 jobs for the local economy."*
>
> — Jerry Phillips, District Manager of the Elliott State Forest, 1968–2001

I want to take you back to 1996.

I was forty-something, newly arrived at the Oregon State University College of Business as an associate professor, and I had just bought a piece of timberland up the South Fork of the Marys River in the Kings Valley, about twenty miles northwest of Corvallis. It was a working tree farm — second-growth Douglas-fir, some alder along the creek bottoms, a rough gravel road that washed out about once a winter. My wife Barb and I have lived within reach of it ever since.

I bought the tree farm for the obvious reasons. I had grown up the son of the Statesman Journal's editor in Salem, watching my father pay attention to Oregon as a place — the rivers, the timber towns, the agencies that ran them — and I wanted, in my second career, to own a small piece of what I had been raised to take seriously. I also bought it because I had just left Tektronix after fifteen years, and I needed something to think about that was not a quarterly forecast.

What I did not know, when I signed the papers, was that I had also bought a thirty-year seat in a small room called the Oregon Small Woodlands Association, where, a few meetings in, somebody would say the phrase *Common School Fund* in front of me for the first time, and somebody else would say the words *Elliott State Forest*, and the two phrases would sit together in my notebook in a way I would not understand for another twenty years.

This chapter is about why I was the right person to sit in that room, and why it took me as long as it did to see what was being done in front of me. The short version is that I had seen this movie before, in another industry, and I had been on the wrong side of it.

---

I joined Tektronix in the late 1970s as the division finance manager of the Information Systems Division. Tektronix was, at that time, a great Portland-area company — oscilloscopes, instruments, the kind of precision engineering that put Beaverton on the technical map. My division was doing something different. We were building what, looking back, were among the first standalone graphical personal computers in the world — the Tektronix 4051 and its successors. You could draw pictures on the screen. You could store engineering diagrams. Engineers around the world were buying them as fast as we could ship them.

The technical problem we had solved was that computer memory was, in those years, absurdly expensive. There was not enough memory in a desktop machine to hold a full image. So our screens worked like a child's Etch A Sketch — the computer drew only what was needed at the moment, and the screen itself, with a special storage tube, remembered the picture. It was clever. It was ours. And it was selling.

My job was to put together the new-product proposals and help set the prices. I rounded up all the costs I could identify — parts, labor, overhead — and we were making the computers for about two thousand dollars apiece. I multiplied by five. We sold them for about ten thousand. We were making money hand over fist. We were selling at least ten thousand of them a year. And we had a six-to-eight-month backlog of orders we could not fill.

Now hold that picture for a moment, because the next sentence is the one that matters.

Looking back, I can tell you that the market for what we had built was not ten thousand machines a year. It was ten *million* machines a year. The world was about to put a graphical personal computer on every desk in every office, and we had — for a few years — the head start. We had the technology. We had the engineering talent. We had the marketing budget. We had the customer list. We had everything.

What we did not have was the imagination to believe the number was *ten million*. Inside our management team, the working assumption was that we should plan to build maybe five times what we were building now — fifty thousand a year, instead of ten thousand. That, we figured, would solve the problem. So we put our energy into a new production-control system that was supposed to ramp us up to fifty thousand. The new system was a disaster. It took longer to build than anyone predicted, did not work as well as we expected, and by the time it limped into service, the world had moved.

Two guys in a garage in California — Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak — had started Apple Computer with a fraction of our funding and a fraction of our engineering staff. They did not have our market data. They did not have our customer list. They had something else. They believed the number was ten million.

The reason Apple beat us was not that they were smarter or better-resourced. The reason Apple beat us was that the five of us who ran my division at Tektronix did not believe what the market was telling us. The signal was right there. We had a six-month backlog and a price-to-cost ratio of five-to-one. The market was screaming at us. We could not hear it because believing it would have required us to think outside the group think we had built our careers inside. *People tend not to have more success than they plan for.* I have used that sentence a thousand times since 1980, and I have rarely used it in a happy way.

I left Tektronix a few years later, did a stretch of doctoral work, and arrived at the OSU College of Business as someone who had been, in a small but real way, the management team that lost the future. I want you to hold that fact about me as you read the rest of this book. I am not warning you about institutional failure from the outside. I am warning you from inside the failure mode I myself helped commit. When I tell you, later in this book, that the people running the Oregon State Land Board did not set out to break the trust and yet are breaking it — I am telling you that from experience. I have been one of the people who did not see what was in front of him.

That is the lesson I carried into the Kings Valley tree farm in 1996. It is the lesson I carried, a few months later, into my first Oregon Small Woodlands Association meeting. It is the lesson, in the end, that this book is about.

---

The Oregon Small Woodlands Association is exactly what its name suggests — an organization of people who own working forest land in tracts too small to interest the big timber companies. We meet in church basements and county Grange halls. We talk about reforestation tax credits, about elk damage, about how to keep a logging road open through a wet winter. The conversation is not glamorous. It is mostly practical.

But the OSWA conversation has a long shadow behind it, and the shadow has a name. The shadow is the Common School Fund — the public trust that owns about 1.5 million acres of Oregon's land in the name of Oregon's schoolchildren. The flagship working asset of that trust is, and has been since the 1930s, the Elliott State Forest: roughly 90,000 acres of timber land in Coos and Douglas Counties, about a hundred and twenty miles south of where I sit in Kings Valley.

The first time I heard somebody at an OSWA meeting say *Elliott*, I did not know what they were talking about. The first time I heard somebody say *Common School Fund*, I assumed it was a state government bank account that paid for textbooks. Both of those impressions were partly right and almost entirely wrong, in ways I would have to spend the next two decades correcting. *(I will come back to what the Common School Fund actually is. There is a short box at the end of this chapter that lays it out.)*

What I picked up in those early meetings, in pieces, was this. The Elliott was Oregon's working trust forest. It had been managed for decades, mostly competently, by the Oregon Department of Forestry, under the trustee authority of an institution called the State Land Board. The man who had run it for thirty-three of those years — the District Manager from 1968 to 2001 — was named Jerry Phillips. Jerry Phillips's number, the one I wrote in the margin of my OSWA notebook the first time I heard it, was *$790 million*. That was the revenue the Elliott had produced for the schools during his tenure. About 400 jobs in the local economy, year in and year out. About fifty million board feet of timber a year. The Elliott was not a problem. The Elliott was the answer. The answer to *how does Oregon honor the promise it made to its schoolchildren in 1859?*

I want to stop here and tell you what I thought that meant in 1996, because the gap between what I thought it meant then and what I now understand it to mean is the gap this whole book is trying to close.

In 1996, I thought *the Elliott produces money for the schools* in roughly the way I thought *the post office delivers mail*. It was something the government did. It worked. It had worked for a long time. I did not see, in any sharp way, that the Elliott was *trust property* — meaning, that it was owned by the schoolchildren of Oregon and merely held by the State, the way a bank holds a safe-deposit box for the person whose name is on the lease. I did not see, in any sharp way, that the State's relationship to the Elliott was a fiduciary one, with all the legal teeth that word carries. I did not see that the Elliott could be — would be, was already being — administered in ways that broke that fiduciary relationship, because I had been raised to believe that Oregon's institutions, of all the institutions in the United States, were the ones least likely to break their promises.

I was wrong about that. The book is partly about how I came to be wrong about it.

---

Around 2015 or 2016 — I cannot pin the exact date and I am not going to pretend I can — a different kind of phrase started showing up in OSWA conversations and in the trade press. Phrases like *net drain on the fund.* Phrases like *the Elliott is no longer cash-positive.* Phrases like *we need to find a new model.* The harvest numbers, when I went to look them up, told a story I did not at first believe. Jerry Phillips's fifty-million-board-feet-per-year had, over the course of about a decade, dropped toward zero. The $790 million across thirty-three years had become, in the official agency presentations, *a forest that costs the schools money.*

That was the moment, if there was a moment, when the Tektronix bell went off in my head. Not loudly. Not all at once. But unmistakably.

The vocabulary had shifted. The institution that held the asset had begun to describe the asset as a problem. The frame had moved from *how do we make this asset produce* to *how do we relieve the trust of this asset.* If you have spent your life inside a corporate boardroom watching the same shift happen to a graphical personal computer line that ought to be the future of the company, you recognize the language. The institution is preparing itself, quietly, to let the thing go.

I did not believe, at first, what I was seeing. The same group-think that had blinded me at Tektronix was now operating *on me* about the Elliott. I had voted for the people on the State Land Board. I knew, socially, some of the agency staff. I trusted the institution. I assumed the *net drain* language must reflect some real change in the underlying forest, something about endangered species or fire risk or market collapse, that I just did not yet understand.

It would take me another two or three years — and a series of public-records requests, agency-meeting attendances, and conversations with a tree-farmer named Bob Zybach who knew the Elliott better than anyone living — to understand that what I was seeing was not a forest in decline. It was an institution in the process of letting go of a forest it no longer wanted to hold. The harvest had not gone to zero because the trees would not grow. The harvest had gone to zero because the trustees, sitting as the State Land Board, had decided — slowly, defensibly, decision by decision — that they would rather not be in the timber business anymore. The fact that the timber business was, in this case, *the children's trust* was the part nobody at the Land Board's dais seemed to want to say out loud.

I want to be precise here, because the precision is important. I am not saying the State Land Board in 2015 set out to steal from Oregon's schoolchildren. I am saying that the State Land Board in 2015 set out to make a series of decisions, each of which it could defend on its own terms, that would cumulatively — and I now believe knowingly — convert the children's trust forest into a public asset of a different kind, owned and operated for purposes other than the production of revenue for the schools. The cumulative pattern, not any single decision, is the breach. I had seen exactly this pattern at Tektronix. We had not decided, one Tuesday morning in 1980, to surrender the personal computer market to Apple. We had made a sequence of decisions, each defensible, that added up to surrender. The Elliott was now on the same track. The asset was being prepared to be let go.

And so, sometime in 2016, I sat at the kitchen table in our farmhouse in Kings Valley with a coffee getting cold next to my hand and decided I wanted to know what was actually going on. I did not yet imagine that I would, eight years later, be the lead plaintiff in a lawsuit against the State of Oregon. I did not yet imagine that the people I would come to work with on this — Margaret Bird, Daniel Crowe, Natalie Scott, Bob Zybach, David Gould — were waiting somewhere just over the horizon. I imagined only that I would write a public-records request, send it to the Department of State Lands, and find out what was happening to the Elliott.

That is the request that became, two or three years later, the foundational document in everything that followed. It is where the next chapter begins.

#### What is the Common School Fund?

*Skip this if you already know it. The rest of the book will not test you.*

When Oregon entered the Union in 1859, the federal Admissions Act gave the new State two square-mile sections out of every thirty-six in every township — Sections 16 and 36, roughly five percent of the entire land area of Oregon — for one purpose only: the support of common schools. *Common schools*, in the language of the time, meant what we today call K–12 public education. The land was not a gift to the State of Oregon to do with as it pleased. It was a trust. The land, and the money that flowed from selling or managing it, was to be held by the State for the schoolchildren — meaning every Oregon child then alive, every Oregon child to come, every Oregon child forever — as the beneficiary.

The Oregon Constitution, ratified the same year, set this up with three features that will recur throughout the rest of this book.

First, it created a body called the State Land Board to hold the trust. The Board has three members, and the Constitution names them by office: the Governor, the Secretary of State, and the State Treasurer. The current Board, as of 2026, is Governor Tina Kotek, Secretary of State Tobias Read (now the Treasurer; the Secretary of State seat changed hands), and Treasurer Elizabeth Steiner. The Board sits as trustee — the same word a bank uses for the person who holds your safe-deposit box.

Second, it created the Common School Fund — sometimes called the Irreducible Fund — as the place where the proceeds of the school lands are kept. The word *irreducible* is doing important work. The Constitution requires that the corpus — the principal — of the fund cannot be reduced. The income can be spent on schools. The principal cannot be touched.

Third, it established that the lands themselves and the fund that holds the proceeds are *trust property*. The State does not own them in the way the State owns a state office building. The State holds them in the name of the schoolchildren. The State is the trustee. The children are the beneficiaries. The duty the State owes the children is the same duty any trustee owes any beneficiary anywhere in American law — the duty of loyalty, the duty of prudence, the duty to administer the trust for the beneficiary's benefit, not the trustee's convenience.

The Elliott State Forest, consolidated in the 1930s out of school sections in Coos and Douglas Counties, is the working timber asset of this trust. For most of the twentieth century, it was the engine. Jerry Phillips's thirty-three years there produced $790 million in revenue for the fund. That money, distributed through the State School Fund formula, paid for textbooks, school buses, teacher salaries, building maintenance — the on-the-ground daily expenses of running Oregon's public schools.

That is the institution this book is about. That is the trust the State of Oregon, on the documentary record I will lay out in the chapters that follow, has been breaking.
