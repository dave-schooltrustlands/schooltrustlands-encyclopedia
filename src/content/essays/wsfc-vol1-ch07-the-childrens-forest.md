# Chapter 7 — The Children's Forest

> *"I introduced the soft ball team and baseball team to their forest at buster club today. As I expected the students did not know they own 43,000 acres in their Elliott State Children's Forest."*
>
> — David Gould, email, March 3, 2022

I want to take this chapter slowly, because the documentary record on it is the strongest record in the book. If you read no other chapter, read this one.

The story is not about people who failed to understand what they were doing. The story is about people who understood exactly what they were doing, put their understanding in writing, continued anyway, and marketed the move under a name — *Children's Forest* — that used the beneficiaries of the trust as the cover story for taking the asset away from them.

By the early months of 2022, the State of Oregon needed a vehicle. The Common School Fund's claim on the Elliott State Forest had been narrowed by the 2019 bond substitution, but the Elliott itself was still on the State Land Board's books as trust land. The Land Board could not, by its own action, move the parcel out of the Common School Fund — the trust character of the Elliott is set by the Oregon Constitution and by the underlying federal grant in the 1859 Admissions Act. What the Legislature could do was create a new entity to take title, and authorize compensation to the trust on the way out the door.

That is what House Bill 3119 did.

The bill chartered a new public corporation called the Elliott State Research Forest Authority and authorized the State Land Board to transfer the Elliott to the Authority for management as a research forest by Oregon State University. It provided for additional bond issuances to compensate the Common School Fund for the transferred asset. It set up a governance board with seats reserved for OSU, the Department of State Lands, the Confederated Tribes, the Coos County local community, and environmental and timber-industry interests. The bill changed the asset's stated purpose from generating timber revenue for the Common School Fund to serving as a research forest for Oregon State University.

I want to pause and name what the bill did in plain English, because the legalistic surface makes it easy to read past the underlying move. The bill took the asset out of the trust. It transferred legal title from the State Land Board, which holds Common School Fund lands on behalf of the schoolchildren, to a new entity that does not hold the land on behalf of the schoolchildren. The trust gives up the asset; the State gives the trust bonds the State will pay back from general revenue; the trust receives, in exchange for a working timber forest worth somewhere between one billion and one and three-tenths billion dollars, a stream of bond proceeds and future appropriations whose total value is some fraction of the asset's market value, secured by no asset other than the State's promise to honor the appropriations.

The bill passed. Governor Kate Brown had championed the framework before leaving office. Governor Tina Kotek inherited it and continued it. State Treasurer Tobias Read supported it. The Secretary of State did not block it. The bill became law.

Then the State began marketing it.

## The rebrand

Beginning in 2021 and accelerating through the 2022 legislative session, the Department of State Lands, the OSU College of Forestry, and the supporting coalition of conservation groups began referring to the Elliott as *the Children's Forest*. The phrase appeared in press releases, in public-meeting materials, on websites, in legislative testimony, and in school-district outreach. The framing was that the Elliott, in becoming a research forest, would also become a forest dedicated to the children of Oregon — accessible for school field trips, for outdoor education, for the kind of environmental learning that would serve the children of the state in their educational lives.

I want you to sit with the marketing move for a moment.

The Elliott had been the Common School Fund's working asset since the federal grant in 1859. Its purpose, in trust law and in the Oregon Constitution, had been to produce revenue for the schoolchildren of Oregon. The schoolchildren were the beneficiaries. The forest was held in trust for them. The trust's name — *Common School Fund* — included the word *school* because the schoolchildren were who the trust existed to serve.

The conversion the 2022 plan accomplished took the asset away from those schoolchildren. It removed the asset from the trust that named them as beneficiaries. It replaced the asset's revenue stream with bond proceeds whose value was a small fraction of the asset's market value. It assigned management to a state university whose mission was not the schoolchildren's educational funding.

And the conversion was marketed, in the press materials and the public-facing communications and the testimony to the Legislature, under the name *Children's Forest*.

The State took the asset out of the children's trust, and named the new arrangement after the children.

David Gould, who would become a co-plaintiff in the OASTL litigation, wrote to the North Bend and Powers school boards on February 11, 2022, asking for their formal approval of legal action and characterizing the move as what it was: *"The Elliott State Research Forest is a TAKE of promised Common School Funds for the Elliott State Children's Forest established in 1930."* Both school boards voted to support him.

Three weeks later, on March 3, 2022, after speaking to the Buster Club at the North Bend High School ball-team introduction, Gould wrote what for my money is the cleanest articulation of what the marketing accomplished. *"I introduced the soft ball team and baseball team to their forest at buster club today. As I expected the students did not know they own 43,000 acres in their Elliott State Children's Forest."*

The students did not know. Not because they were uninterested. Because the institutional architecture in which the Elliott was their asset had been operated, for decades, without their participation, and the rebrand of the asset they did not know they owned into a Children's Forest they were told would serve them was the marketing that capped the operation.

## The page-28 number

On April 14, 2021, the Oregon State University College of Forestry submitted its Elliott State Research Forest Proposal to the Department of State Lands. The document was the formal OSU pitch for taking the Elliott as a research forest under what would become HB 3119. It ran to several hundred pages. It had been prepared over approximately eighteen months under the leadership of OSU College of Forestry Interim Dean Anthony S. Davis, with OSU President F. King Alexander's institutional sponsorship and, after Alexander's resignation in early 2021, with President Becky Johnson's caretaker support pending the arrival of incoming President Jayathi Murthy in fall 2022.

On page 28 of the Proposal, in the financial section, OSU's own preliminary financial analysis projected that the operation would run a *$2.1 million deficit on an average annual basis for the first fifty years.*

I want to be careful about that number, and I want to be careful in a particular way.

OSU did not present the figure as a settled accounting determination. The document characterized the financial section as a *preliminary financial analysis*, with the modeling assumptions and the caveats one would expect from a preliminary analysis of a fifty-year operation. A later financial determination, with refined modeling, with hedges removed, might have come in higher or lower. OSU did not put $2.1 million annually on the books and sign it.

What OSU did do is put $2.1 million annually for fifty years into the formal proposal it submitted to the State Land Board.

That distinction matters less than it sounds. The proposal was the document the State Land Board had in front of it when the Board endorsed the transfer. The proposal was the document the Legislature had in front of it when it voted on HB 3119. The proposal was the document the Governor's office had in front of it when it endorsed the framework. The number on page 28 was the number the trustees of the trust were looking at when they decided to transfer the trust's working asset to the manager whose own preliminary analysis projected the manager would lose money on the property for half a century.

#### The $2.1 Million Paragraph

The relevant section of the Elliott State Research Forest Proposal, page 28, describes the financial performance OSU projected for itself as the new manager of the forest. The language is the language of a preliminary financial analysis: assumptions stated, sensitivities flagged, modeling horizons named. Within that frame, the document reports that the planned operation produces a deficit of approximately $2.1 million per year, averaged across the first fifty years of the new management.

*Preliminary financial analysis*, in this context, means the modeling has not yet been refined to a settled determination. The hedges allow for revision. The figure could move with different assumptions about timber prices, harvest schedules, federal grants, or research-funding offsets. OSU did not assert that the operation would, in fact, lose exactly $2.1 million a year. OSU asserted that, on the modeling it had developed for the proposal it was submitting, the planned operation produced a $2.1 million annual deficit on an average annual basis for the first fifty years.

The trust-doctrine point does not require OSU to have settled the number.

A trustee, choosing a manager for a trust asset, has a duty to exercise prudence. The duty is the simplest version of fiduciary care: a reasonable person, managing the property of others, does not choose a manager whose own preliminary analysis says the management will lose money for fifty years. That is the floor of prudence. A reasonable trustee, with a $1.3 billion asset under management, asks for a refined analysis before transferring the asset to the manager whose preliminary analysis flags a five-decade running loss. A reasonable trustee, finding the refined analysis does not materially change the picture, declines the transfer.

The State Land Board did not ask. The State Land Board endorsed the transfer. The Legislature authorized it. The bonds were structured to pay the projected deficit, year after year, out of general revenue.

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember the structure. OSU put a preliminary number on the page. The trustee did not test the number. The trustee transferred the asset anyway. The general fund — that is, the same Oregon taxpayers whose taxes were already paying the bond service on the 2019 substitution — now stood behind a fifty-year operating deficit on a forest the schoolchildren had owned outright since 1859.

</aside>

## Jerry Franklin's review

The financial documentation was not the only document in the 2022 record that put the OSU plan under question. The scientific documentation did the same work, from a different angle.

Jerry Franklin is the dean of Pacific Northwest forest ecology. His career, beginning at the OSU College of Forestry in the 1960s and continuing through his long tenure at the University of Washington, defines the field of how Pacific Northwest mature-forest ecology is studied and what counts as rigorous experimental design in it. When the OSU Elliott State Research Forest Proposal was put out for technical peer review, Franklin was the obvious external reviewer to consult. He wrote what became Appendix 13 of the Proposal record.

Franklin's review is short. Franklin's review is direct.

On the experimental design at the heart of the research forest plan — the TRIAD framework that the OSU proposal said would let the forest serve simultaneously as a working timber operation, a reserve, and a research site — Franklin wrote that *"the whole notion that you are doing a meaningful test of the TRIAD concept is nonsense."* On the statistical structure that would have let the research design produce defensible inferences about treatment effects, he wrote, in the kind of plain-English declarative that peer reviewers do not often produce, *"you don't have any true controls!"*

I am not a forest ecologist. I am not a peer reviewer in the field. What I am reporting is what the field's most authoritative figure put in writing, in a formal peer review attached to the proposal that was before the Legislature. The plan that HB 3119 authorized was a plan that the dean of the field did not regard as scientifically valid. The Legislature voted on it anyway. The Land Board endorsed it anyway. The plan was characterized to the public as a research forest plan even after the field's most authoritative figure had said, in writing, that the research design was not a research design.

Stack the two documents next to each other. OSU's own page-28 preliminary analysis said the financial operation would run a fifty-year deficit. OSU's own peer-review appendix said the experimental design did not have controls and the test was not a meaningful test. The Legislature voted on HB 3119 with both documents in the record. The State Land Board endorsed the transfer with both documents in the record. The Governor's office endorsed the framework with both documents in the record.

None of the parties involved was operating in the dark. They knew. They made the plan anyway.

## The resistance

I do not want to close this chapter without naming the resistance the 2022 plan produced, because the resistance is part of the case, and the participants are part of the cast.

The Joint Ways and Means subcommittee of the Oregon Legislature heard testimony on SB 1546A — a companion to HB 3119 from the 2022 session — and the testimony from the Coos County and Coast school districts was substantial. The school boards of North Bend, Powers, and Coos Bay passed resolutions opposing the transfer. Their reasoning was the reasoning the OASTL litigation eventually built on. The Elliott had been held in trust for their districts' children since 1859. The conversion was taking the asset from those children. The substitute compensation was inadequate.

A sign campaign ran on the South Coast. The slogan was *Save the Children's Forest.* The campaign chose the slogan deliberately, because it inverted the rebrand the plan was using. The plan said *Children's Forest* and meant a research forest under OSU management whose funding stream came from bond appropriations rather than trust-beneficiary distributions. The campaign said *Save the Children's Forest* and meant return the asset to its trust status as a working forest for the Common School Fund beneficiaries who owned it. The two phrases used the same words to mean opposite things, which is what is supposed to happen when the framing of a public issue becomes the issue.

Bob Zybach introduced me to the Lars Larson Show audience in February 2022 as a former OSU professor and President of the Oregon Websites and Watersheds Project who had been working full-time on the Elliott for five-plus years. The interview ran. The audience the radio reached was the rural-Oregon timber-country audience that had not been engaged in the 2017 sale controversy and that the 2022 plan was directly affecting.

The resistance did not stop the plan. The plan moved forward. The Legislature passed HB 3119. The Land Board endorsed the transfer. The Elliott State Research Forest Authority was chartered as a legal entity. The framework for the conversion was complete.

What stood, after the 2022 session closed, was the documentary record. OSU's preliminary financial analysis on page 28 of the proposal the State Land Board endorsed. Jerry Franklin's Appendix 13 on the experimental design that did not have controls. The school-board resolutions from the Coos County and Coast districts. The sign campaign that took the State's marketing phrase and gave it back its actual meaning. David Gould at the Buster Club, telling the softball team and the baseball team that they owned 43,000 acres they did not know they owned, in a forest the State was about to take from them while calling it theirs.

The documentary record was what it was. Anyone willing to read it could see what had happened.

The man who told the ball teams about their forest had been walking the land his family homesteaded in the 1880s. His name was David Gould, and he is the subject of the next chapter.
