# Chapter 6 — The 2019 Bond Substitution

*Working draft v1, 2026-05-10. ~2,500 words target. Dave-first-person body, named actors, dated events, dollar figures. The chapter where the conversion the 2017 sale had attempted was accomplished through a different mechanism — quietly, through the legislature, with the Land Board's blessing, and with almost no public attention.*

---

I want to be careful in this chapter, because the move I am about to walk you through did not look like a theft when it was happening. It looked like budgeting. It looked like the Oregon Legislature solving a problem the State Land Board had brought to it. It looked, on a casual read of the newspapers from the time, like compensation — the State making the Common School Fund whole for revenue it had stopped receiving from the Elliott. The Oregon Public Broadcasting headline from March 21, 2019 was *Oregon Sells $100 Million In Bonds For Elliott State Forest*. That headline is technically accurate. It is also, in the way that the most consequential financial transactions are often misnamed in their first public framing, the wrong description of what happened.

What happened on March 21, 2019, and in the legislative actions that authorized it, was this. The State of Oregon converted the schoolchildren's claim on a 93,000-acre Coast Range Douglas-fir forest into the schoolchildren's claim on a line item in the Oregon state budget, and called it compensation. The dollars looked the same on the surface. The fiduciary posture underneath was completely different.

I want to walk you through how the conversion worked, why a trustee cannot do what Oregon did and call it lawful trust administration, and what the conversion set up for the chapters that follow.

## I. The mechanism

The 2019 bond issuance was authorized by the Oregon Legislature in the 2017–2019 budget cycle and executed by the State Treasurer's office in March 2019. The mechanism, in plain English, was this.

The State of Oregon issued $100 million in general-obligation bonds. *General-obligation* is the technical term for debt that is backed by the full faith and credit of the State — meaning the State, not the asset, is the borrower. The State is on the hook to bondholders regardless of what happens to the Elliott. The bonds will be paid back over their term out of general revenue — that is, out of whatever tax dollars the Oregon Legislature appropriates for debt service in each future biennium.

The $100 million in bond proceeds was deposited into the Common School Fund. On the Department of State Lands' books, on the State Treasurer's books, and in the annual Common School Fund Annual Reports that the agency publishes, the $100 million appeared as a new asset of the trust. The Department of State Lands described the deposit in its public communications as *compensating* the Common School Fund for revenue the Fund would no longer receive from the Elliott, because the Elliott's harvest had been reduced by endangered-species protections and was projected to remain reduced indefinitely.

That is the surface description. *The State of Oregon, acting as trustee of the Common School Fund, recognized that the trust's working timber asset was producing less revenue than it once had, and the State, acting in its capacity as the trust's settlor and supporter, replaced the missing revenue with $100 million in bond proceeds.* You can find some version of that description in the State Land Board's December 2022 meeting recap, in the Department of State Lands' Elliott State Forest webpage, in the official record of the 2017–2019 legislative session, and in the financial statements of the Common School Fund itself.

Now let me describe what actually happened, in the language of trust doctrine rather than budgeting.

## II. What a trustee cannot do

The black-letter law of trusts is unambiguous on this question. A trustee holds trust assets for the benefit of the beneficiaries. The trustee's duty is to administer the corpus — the body of the trust property itself — in a manner that preserves and grows its value for the named beneficiaries. The trustee does not own the assets. The trustee holds them. The beneficiaries own them, in the equitable sense; the trustee's title is legal only.

A trustee cannot sell the trust's asset and replace it with the trustee's own IOU. That is the formulation, in its simplest form, of what is prohibited. The conflict of interest is obvious on the face of it. The trustee, who has a fiduciary obligation to maximize the asset's value for the beneficiaries, also happens to be the issuer of the IOU. The trustee's incentive on the asset's value is to maximize. The trustee's incentive on the IOU's terms is to minimize. The two incentives are in direct opposition. The doctrine resolves the opposition by prohibiting the substitution.

The 1992 Oregon Attorney General Opinion on the State Land Board's trustee duties — 46 Op Atty Gen 468, Opinion 8223, dated July 24, 1992 — affirmed the trust character of the Common School Fund and its lands and held the State to the standard duties of a trustee. The Oregon Supreme Court's 1931 opinion in *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden* described the trust the State of Oregon holds in the Common School Fund lands as *a trust of the highest nature*. The 2003 Attorney General Opinion reaffirmed the doctrine. The doctrine has not been overturned. The doctrine, applied to the 2019 bond substitution, says what the doctrine has always said about a trustee that converts an asset to a self-issued IOU. It says the trustee cannot do that. The substitution is voidable at the election of the beneficiary, and the trustee is liable for any losses.

The Oregon Legislature, on the State Land Board's recommendation, did it anyway.

## III. Why the conversion is a different posture

I want to slow down and explain, in concrete operational terms, why the conversion matters even though the dollar values appeared to match.

Before March 2019, the Common School Fund held, among other assets, an undivided interest in approximately 82,500 acres of the Elliott State Forest. *Approximately*, because the trust's interest in the Elliott has always been complicated by the federal-state land transfer history and by the parallel Board of Forestry holdings within the same forest perimeter; the figure that has been most commonly used in the trust accounting through 2022 was 82,500 CSF acres out of approximately 93,000 total Elliott acres. The trust's interest in those 82,500 acres was an *equitable interest in a working asset*. The interest produced timber revenue when timber was harvested. The interest carried with it operating costs when management activities occurred. The interest could be measured in market terms by reference to the value of the standing timber, the regenerative capacity of the land, the long-term harvest schedule, and the comparable sales of similar Coast Range Douglas-fir tracts. That market value, per the independent estimates compiled by Bill Lansing and others over the years, was in the range of one billion to one and three-tenths billion dollars.

After March 2019, the Common School Fund held, in place of part of that working-asset interest, a claim on $100 million in bond proceeds that had been deposited into the Fund's investment portfolio. The bond proceeds were dollars in an account. They earned the return that the Common School Fund Investment Pool earned, which has historically averaged in the range of 5 to 7 percent annually depending on the market cycle. The bond proceeds could be drawn down, invested, distributed under the Common School Fund's distribution formula, and otherwise treated as a financial asset of the trust.

The accounting looked clean. The fiduciary posture was different in four ways that matter, and I want to name each one.

*First, the trust no longer held a claim on a specific asset whose value could appreciate. The Elliott's underlying value as a working timber forest had been growing for over a century. The standing timber inventory had compounded. The land's productive capacity had compounded. The asset was a living thing that increased in value as the trees grew. The $100 million in bonds was not. It was a number. It would earn whatever the Common School Fund Investment Pool earned in any given year, and no more. The trust had traded a compounding asset for a non-compounding one, at one-tenth of the compounding asset's market value.*

*Second, the trust's claim on the bonds is unsecured by the Elliott. The bonds are general-obligation debt of the State, paid back from general revenue. The Elliott has no claim on the bonds. The bonds have no claim on the Elliott. The two are now severed. If, in some future biennium, the Oregon Legislature finds itself unable or unwilling to appropriate the debt service on the 2019 bonds, the bondholders will still get paid — the State's full faith and credit guarantees it. But the trust will have lost the asset and also lost the corresponding stream of replacement income. The bondholders have priority on the State's general fund; the schoolchildren do not. The schoolchildren had priority on the Elliott. They no longer have priority on anything that replaces the Elliott.*

*Third, the legislative discretion that the conversion places into the system is something a trustee is not allowed to introduce. A trustee may not subject the trust to the discretion of a future legislature. A trustee may not say *I will replace this asset with a series of future appropriations and trust that future legislatures will continue the appropriations.* That is not trust administration. It is a political promise. Political promises are revocable in a way trust assets are not.*

*Fourth, the State of Oregon now sits on both sides of the transaction. The State is the trustee. The State is the issuer of the bonds. The State sets the interest rate. The State controls the term. The State controls the priority of the bonds against future appropriations. The self-dealing is structural. It is not in the heart of any particular official; it is in the architecture of the conversion itself. A trustee that has its hand on both sides of the transaction cannot administer it neutrally, and the law does not require us to wait for a particular act of bad faith before naming the conflict.*

These four points are not technicalities. They are the heart of why the law of trusts has held, for several hundred years, that a trustee cannot sell the trust's asset and replace it with the trustee's own IOU. The 2019 bond substitution was a particularly clean example of the prohibited substitution. The Oregon Legislature and the State Land Board treated it as ordinary public finance. The doctrine treats it as a breach.

## IV. The 1977 doctrine, again

I asked you in Chapter 4 to hold the 1977 AG Opinion 7450 in mind, and I asked you in Chapter 5 to notice that the May 2017 reversal had not touched the doctrine even though it had reversed the particular sale that prompted the doctrine's evasion. I now want to ask you to look at the 2019 bond substitution through the 1977 lens, because the lens makes the move visible in a way it is not visible if you only look at the budgeting.

The 1977 doctrine held, with reasoning that has not been overturned, that Common School Grant Land could be designated as a Natural Area Preserve under ORS 273.562 only *when such designation would not adversely affect the financial contribution of such land to the Common School Trust Fund.* The doctrine's operational meaning, given the realities of off-purpose conversion of CSF land in nearly every actual case, was *no.* The doctrine put a floor under the trust. It said the State could not designate CSF land for an off-purpose use that reduced the land's contribution to the school children.

The 2019 bond substitution is the same kind of move, accomplished by different means. The Elliott has not been formally designated as a Natural Area Preserve. The Elliott has not been transferred to another state agency. The Elliott has not, as of the bond issuance, been sold. What has happened is that the Elliott has been treated, in the trust's accounting, as if its contribution had been replaced. The $100 million in bond proceeds is presented as a substitute for the Elliott's revenue stream. The Elliott's actual revenue stream has continued to decline as the harvest has been reduced. The bond proceeds occupy the line in the Common School Fund's books that the Elliott's revenue would have occupied if the Elliott were being managed as a working asset.

The 1977 doctrine does not say *the State may evade the trust by accounting moves so long as the headline numbers add up.* It says the opposite. It says the trust's financial contribution must be honored in substance. The 2019 bond substitution honors the contribution in surface accounting and breaks it in substance. The doctrine, if it means what it has been read to mean since 1977, prohibits the move.

The doctrine, again, was not amended. The doctrine, again, was not put before a court. The doctrine, again, was bypassed.

That is twice now, in the post-1990 record, that the agencies have moved past the 1977 floor without addressing it. The 1990s capture (Chapter 4) walked past it by redefining the Elliott's financial contribution downward to a level the agencies were prepared to accept. The 2019 bond substitution walked past it by replacing the Elliott's financial contribution with a budget line item and treating the substitution as equivalent to the contribution itself. The 2022 OSU plan, which Chapter 7 takes up next, walks past it for the third time, in the most explicit form yet — by converting the Elliott to an entirely different purpose and treating bond-funded appropriations as the trust's compensation for the conversion.

The 1977 floor has not been overturned. It has been moved past, three times now, by agencies that have decided the floor does not bind them.

## V. The 2019 accounting analysis

I will close this chapter with the financial picture, because the financial picture is what makes the bond substitution legible as a breach rather than as a budgeting decision.

The forensic accounting analysis I prepared in March 2026 — circulated to the OASTL Board, to General Counsel Daniel Crowe, and to the superintendents of the school-district plaintiffs in the 2024 case — went through the public Common School Fund Annual Reports from 2016 forward and compared them to the actual cash flows the State has disclosed in discovery in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* (24CV38372). The accounting analysis is on the L0 substrate of this book at `L0_Primary_Sources/Discovery/2024_Case_24CV38372/`, under the filename *Accounting Analysis — Reconciling Public CSF Reports with State Discovery Data (Lawyer Reviewed)*.

The analysis showed several things. First, the 2019 bond proceeds were treated, in the public-facing accounting, as a one-time addition to the Common School Fund corpus rather than as a debt the State will service over the life of the bonds. The cost of servicing the bonds — the interest payments that the State will pay to the bondholders over the bond term — does not appear as an offsetting liability of the trust in the public-facing accounting. From the trust's surface presentation, the $100 million is a clean addition. From the State's actual fiscal position, the $100 million is a liability that the State will retire over decades out of general revenue.

Second, the analysis showed that the Common School Fund's distribution per student has continued to decline in real terms despite the 2019 bond addition. The bond proceeds, invested in the CSF Investment Pool, produce an annual return that is materially less than the harvest revenue the Elliott had historically produced when managed as a working forest. The substitution has not been revenue-neutral for the schools, even on the State's own numbers. The substitution has been a partial replacement at best, with the schools receiving less from the bond proceeds' earnings than they would have received from a working Elliott — and with the principal of the bond proceeds itself representing roughly one-tenth of the Elliott's market value.

Third, the analysis surfaced the question of who, in fiduciary terms, the State Treasurer was acting for when the bonds were issued. The Treasurer is a member of the State Land Board. The Treasurer is responsible for issuing State debt. The Treasurer, on March 21, 2019, signed the offering documents for the bonds the Treasurer's Board had recommended that the Legislature authorize to compensate the trust the Treasurer's Board administers. The Treasurer in 2019 was the same Treasurer who had voted *yes* on the Lone Rock sale in February 2017 and who had then voted to reverse the sale in May 2017 under public pressure. The same Treasurer signed the bond papers in 2019 that accomplished the underlying conversion the 2017 sale had been intended to accomplish. The Treasurer's name was Tobias Read.

I am not making a personal accusation. The Treasurer's actions on each individual date were within the range of behaviors a State Treasurer might be expected to undertake in the ordinary course of office. What I am pointing out is the structural pattern. The same trustee, across multiple decision points, moved the trust in the same direction. The direction was conversion of the Elliott from a working CSF asset into something else. The mechanism varied. The direction did not.

## VI. The conversion was substantial, and almost no one noticed

The press coverage of the 2019 bond issuance was thin. The Oregonian carried a short article. Oregon Public Broadcasting carried a short article. There were no protests. There was no sign campaign. There was no equivalent of the Save the Children's Forest mobilization that the 2017 sale had produced two years earlier. The mechanism was quiet by design. A general-obligation bond issuance moving through the Legislature in the ordinary course of a budget cycle does not have the kind of public visibility that a Land Board vote on a private sale does. The agencies had learned the lesson of 2017. The next move would not be the kind of move that produced signs.

It moved through the Joint Committee on Ways and Means. It moved through the House and the Senate. The State Land Board endorsed it. The State Treasurer's office executed it. The Department of State Lands recorded the proceeds. The Common School Fund Annual Reports for fiscal year 2020 and forward show the $100 million as an asset of the Fund. The transaction was, on the public-facing surface, complete by spring 2019.

What the transaction had actually done was prepare the ground for the next step. The Common School Fund's claim on the Elliott had been partially severed. The asset was still on the books at the State Land Board. The trust's revenue from the asset had been replaced — inadequately, but ostensibly — by bond proceeds. The institutional posture was now ready for the move the 2022 OSU plan would attempt: the formal designation of the Elliott as something other than a CSF working asset, with the trust's compensation accomplished by the same kind of bond mechanism the 2019 move had piloted.

In 2019, Oregon converted the schoolchildren's claim on a 93,000-acre forest into the schoolchildren's claim on a budget line item, and called it compensation. The conversion was incomplete in 2019 — the asset was still there, in trust ownership, and the bonds were a partial replacement at most. The 2022 OSU plan would attempt the completion. That is the chapter we are about to enter.
