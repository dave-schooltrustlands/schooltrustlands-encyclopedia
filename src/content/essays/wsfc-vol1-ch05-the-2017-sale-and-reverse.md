# Chapter 5 — The 2017 Sale-and-Reverse

*Working draft v1, 2026-05-10. ~2,800 words target. Dave-first-person body, named actors, dated events, dollar figures. The chapter where the public showed up, the State Land Board reversed under pressure, and the underlying institutional posture did not change.*

---

The thing to keep in mind, as I walk you through the 2017 sale, is what was already in the room before the public showed up.

Chapter 4 named it. By 2016 the State Land Board's working theory was that the Elliott State Forest had become a net drain on the Common School Fund and that the trust would be better served by selling the asset and reinvesting the proceeds. That theory had been built up over twenty-five years inside the Department of State Lands and on the Board itself, defensible decision by defensible decision, and by the time the disposition process began in earnest in 2015 the staff packages going to the Board carried the conclusion as if it were arithmetic.

The 2017 sale is what happens when the institution acts on its theory in public for the first time. The reversal is what happens when the public, briefly, shows up.

I want to walk you through both, and then I want to tell you what did not change.

## I. The protocol decision and the bid

The Land Board adopted a Real Property Disposition Protocol in December 2015. The Protocol set out the procedure under which Common School Fund land could be sold. The Elliott was the asset the Protocol had been written for. By August 2016 the Board had directed staff to put the forest on the market. The asking price, set by the State's chosen appraiser Mason Bruce & Girard, was $220.8 million. That number was not a market estimate. It was an "investment value" — what the State concluded the Elliott was worth as a constrained asset under existing endangered-species and management restrictions. Margaret Bird, in her December 11, 2022 letter to the State Land Board which we will see again in this book, called the figure what it was: *"the equivalent of valuing a Ford pickup at the price the State was willing to pay rather than what it would fetch in the market."* Independent estimates of the Elliott's actual market value, prepared by Bill Lansing and others over the years, ran from one billion to one and three-tenths billion dollars. The State's appraisal was less than ten percent of the asset's market value.

You should hold both numbers in your hand for a moment. *Ninety-nine point six million dollars.* *One point three billion dollars.* The math is not subtle. Either Lansing was wrong by an order of magnitude — which no one with timber-industry knowledge has been willing to claim — or the State was preparing to dispose of trust property at a price that did not honor the trust.

A consortium led by **Lone Rock Resources** — a Roseburg-based timber company, partnered with the **Cow Creek Band of Umpqua Tribe of Indians** and an unnamed conservation organization — submitted the qualifying $220.8 million bid in late 2016. The bid met the Protocol's terms. The consortium's package proposed maintaining a working timber forest, with conservation easements, and offered some level of public access. By the State Land Board's internal accounting, the bid solved the problem the Board had been trying to solve for a decade: it removed the Elliott from the trust, replaced the forest with $220.8 million in cash, and let the agency move on.

On **February 14, 2017** — a Tuesday, in Salem, with the snow still on the Coast Range — the State Land Board voted on whether to accept the bid.

The vote was two to one in favor of selling.

Governor **Kate Brown** voted no. Secretary of State **Dennis Richardson** voted yes. State Treasurer **Tobias Read** voted yes. By the Land Board's procedural rules, the two-to-one was a vote to proceed with the disposition.

I want you to mark the Treasurer's vote in particular, because we will see Tobias Read again in this book in 2022, in 2023, and in 2025, voting in essentially the same direction every time, in essentially the same room, on essentially the same theory of what the Elliott is. Read has been continuous on this question, across his time as Treasurer and into his current term as Secretary of State, in a way that is worth noticing.

## II. The protest and the reversal

What happened next was the part the agency's planning had not prepared for.

The February 14 vote produced an immediate public reaction that the State Land Board's twenty-five years of internal capture had not anticipated. The framing was simple, and the simplicity is what made it lethal: *the State is selling the schoolchildren's forest to a private timber company.* That sentence — that exact sentence, in some version of it — appeared on signs at protests, in letters to the editor, in op-eds in *The Oregonian* and *The Register-Guard*, on radio, in the legislature, and in school district newsletters across the Coast.

Coast residents, school districts, environmental groups, the state press, hunting and fishing communities, Indian tribes who were not part of the consortium, and a coalition that I would not have predicted three months earlier all converged on a single position: do not sell the Elliott. The protest was framed in beneficiary language. Schoolchildren had been the named beneficiaries of the trust since 1859. The schoolchildren's land was being sold. The schoolchildren had not been consulted. The forest the schoolchildren owned was being given away — at less than ten percent of its value, the more sophisticated voices added — to a private buyer.

I want to give credit where it is due. The public response was substantially organized by people who were not part of any institutional school-trust constituency, because there was no institutional school-trust constituency to organize it. Environmental advocates carried the framing. Coast Range residents carried the practical knowledge of what the forest was. School districts in Coos and Curry and Douglas Counties carried the beneficiary credibility. The OEA and OSBA were largely silent, as they had been for decades on these questions, and as they would be again. The press, briefly, paid attention.

The Board met again on **May 9, 2017**.

Governor Brown's position had hardened. Treasurer Read's had moved. Secretary Richardson's was unchanged but now in the minority. By a two-to-one vote — Brown and Read in the majority, Richardson dissenting — the Board reversed the February decision. The Lone Rock bid was rejected. The Elliott was, for the moment, off the market.

In Oregon political memory, this is the moment that gets remembered as the win. The schoolchildren's forest was saved from a private buyer. The protest worked. Democracy functioned. The trust was protected.

I want to walk you carefully through why that memory is partly true and substantially wrong.

## III. The reasoning of the reversal

The two-to-one vote on May 9 reversed the sale decision. The vote did not reverse the reasoning that had produced the sale decision.

I want you to read this carefully because it is the structural pivot of everything that comes after. Governor Brown's stated reasons for moving against the sale, in her public remarks before and after the May 9 vote, were political. The public outcry made the sale infeasible. The political costs to the Board, and to her administration, of proceeding with the disposition were higher than the agency had anticipated. The trust's interests were better served, she said, by exploring "alternative ownership structures" that would address the underlying drain on the Common School Fund without triggering the public protest a private sale had produced. Treasurer Read's remarks were similar in substance, although shorter on detail.

Neither trustee said *we were wrong about the underlying theory of the case.* Neither said *the Elliott is not a net drain on the Common School Fund and we should manage it as a working asset.* Neither said *the 1977 AG Opinion 7450 binds us and we are reversing the decoupling that the post-1990 management drift has produced.* Neither said *the trust's beneficiaries should be at this table and we are creating institutional space for them.*

What the Board said on May 9, 2017, in plain English, was this. We will not sell the Elliott to Lone Rock. We will continue to look for other ways to address the problem the Elliott has become.

The structural posture inside the Board did not change. The capture documented in Chapter 4 did not lift. The Department of State Lands' working theory — that the Elliott was a net drain, that off-purpose conversion was a fiduciary improvement, that the 1977 doctrine could be reasoned around — remained the institutional baseline. What had changed was the menu of available conversion mechanisms. *Direct sale to a private buyer* had been removed from the menu. Other items remained.

This is the load-bearing observation of this chapter. **The 2017 sale was reversed. The 2017 reasoning behind the sale was not.**

## IV. The DSL public engagement theater

I want to take a moment to explain how the 2017 process actually felt, from the inside, to a member of the public who tried to engage with it.

Beginning in 2018, after the May 2017 reversal, the Department of State Lands convened a series of "public engagement opportunities" to discuss the Elliott's future. The opportunities were extensively advertised. They were held in Coos Bay, in Salem, in Eugene, and in Portland, on weekday evenings and Saturday mornings, and they were attended by people who genuinely wanted to be heard.

I attended several. I documented what I saw on a website called ElliottSecrets.net, which I built over the course of 2020 to make a public record of what the public engagement process actually consisted of. I am going to quote myself, because I want you to read what I wrote at the time and not the smoothed-over version of it I might write today:

> *Each Department of State Lands "public engagement opportunity" follows a theatrical script: First, a series of experts explains what has already been decided and done, then each member of the public is allowed five minutes of polite attention in front of panelists who pretend to listen. If an interested outsider wants to be really involved or find out how decisions are really being made, no one has any time to spare.*

I called this pattern *public engagement theater* because that is what it is. The decisions had been made. The agencies were not soliciting input on the decisions. They were soliciting the appearance of having solicited input. The five minutes per member of the public, in front of panelists with their pens down, is not a fiduciary process. It is the procedural form of fiduciary process, conducted in a way that immunizes the decisions already taken from the public's potential to influence them.

The 2017 process exhibited the pattern. The post-2017 process exhibited the pattern. The post-2019 OSU research-forest process, which Chapter 7 takes up in detail, exhibited the pattern in an even sharper form. By 2020 I was so tired of attending these meetings and being told by the agency afterwards that the public had "weighed in" that I built ElliottSecrets to put on the public record what the engagement actually consisted of.

The public-engagement-theater pattern matters legally as well as morally. The Oregon Public Meetings Law, ORS 192.610–192.690, requires that *"the deliberations and decisions of governing bodies and the information upon which such decisions were made"* be open to the public. A process in which the deliberations and decisions have been completed before the public meeting begins, and the public meeting consists of presenting the completed deliberations and decisions to a public that has no way to alter them, is not a public meeting in the sense the statute means. It is the appearance of a public meeting. The statute does not prohibit the appearance. The statute requires the substance.

The substance, between 2017 and 2024, was missing.

## V. The Zybach records requests

I want to put on the record, before this chapter closes, two specific dates that establish the documentary trail of what the agencies were and were not willing to disclose during the post-2017 period. The dates matter because they are the proximate evidence that becomes the OASTL litigation case in 2024.

On **December 10, 2019** at 9:05 p.m. Pacific time, Bob Zybach sent a written records request to **Meliah Masiba** at the Department of State Lands. The request asked, in specific terms, for the planning and HCP budget figures for the prior twelve months ahead of an article deadline Zybach had on December 16, 2019. The request was concrete. The information Zybach was asking for was the kind of fiduciary accounting any beneficiary of any trust is entitled to receive. The request was not ignored in the sense of going unread. It was acknowledged and not answered.

On **April 1, 2020** at 7:32 p.m. Pacific time, Zybach sent a second, longer, more detailed records request to Masiba. The April email asked for OSU-Elliott budget detail. It noted that Zybach had been periodically requesting historical maps, aerial photos, and survey documents produced by Jerry Phillips and the Oregon Department of Forestry in the 1950s through the 1980s, and that these requests had been refused for three years. It asked for "a reasonably detailed accounting of DSL/OSU/Elliott funding and expenditures for the past two years," including the most recent allocation that Zybach knew about — a $500,000-plus expenditure on the latest Elliott Habitat Conservation Plan. The April request was likewise acknowledged and likewise not answered.

This is the evidentiary trail. It is the foundation on which the 2024 OASTL Motion to Compel Production, which Chapter 11 takes up in detail, eventually rests. The State knew, in April 2020, that a beneficiary's representative was asking for trust-accounting information. The State refused to produce it. The State's refusal was on the public record, in writing, by April 1, 2020.

I will close this chapter with my own editorial observation from the period. I wrote it on the DSL Secrets page of ElliottSecrets in May 2020 and it is what I still think now: *the information exists, it's readily available, and the DSL is unethically and illegally selecting who gets to see it.*

## VI. What the reversal preserved and what it did not

Let me put the structural picture together for you, because this is the chapter the rest of the book builds on.

What the May 2017 reversal preserved:

1. The Elliott State Forest itself, as a continuous trust holding. The forest was not sold. The trust still owned the land.
2. The political memory that public protest could move the State Land Board. That memory mattered in 2018 and 2019 when subsequent decisions were under consideration.
3. The Lone Rock consortium's particular bid and its particular arrangement, which never came back. Lone Rock did not return to the table.

What the May 2017 reversal did not preserve, or change:

1. The institutional capture documented in Chapter 4. The room was the same room. The trustee's coalition was the same coalition. The beneficiary's seat was still empty.
2. The working theory inside the Department of State Lands that the Elliott was a net drain on the Common School Fund. That theory remained the staff baseline.
3. The bypassing of the 1977 AG Opinion 7450. The doctrine sat unchanged on the books; the operational practice continued not to defer to it.
4. The conversion *intent.* The State Land Board had not abandoned the goal of moving the Elliott from a CSF working timber asset into something else. It had abandoned only the *direct-sale-to-a-private-buyer mechanism* for accomplishing that move.
5. The public-engagement-theater pattern. The pattern continued. It deepened. By 2022 it was the operating procedural form of every Elliott-related agency interaction with the public.
6. The records-request stonewall. By April 1, 2020, two formal records requests had gone unanswered. The pattern of selective disclosure that becomes the litigation case had begun.

What was being prepared, in 2018 and 2019, was a different mechanism for accomplishing the same conversion the 2017 sale had been intended to accomplish. The mechanism would not be a private sale. The mechanism would be a *bond substitution* — a state-issued bond that "compensated" the Common School Fund for reduced timber revenue, converting the trust's claim on a working asset into the trust's claim on a line item in the state budget. The mechanism would be quieter than a sale to a timber company. It would not produce a sign campaign. It would not produce front-page coverage in *The Oregonian*. It would move through the Oregon Legislature in the ordinary course of business, with the Land Board's blessing, and it would accomplish the structural conversion the 2017 sale had attempted.

That is what Chapter 6 takes up.

The 2017 sale was reversed. The 2017 reasoning behind the sale was not. And the next mechanism was already being prepared, in offices the public would not see, by people whose names would not appear on a sign campaign, under a logic the agencies had spent twenty-five years building up and which the May 2017 reversal had not touched.

The Elliott was not saved in May 2017. The Elliott's sale was deferred. The capture remained.
