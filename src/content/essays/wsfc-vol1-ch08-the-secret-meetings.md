# Chapter 8 — The Secret Meetings

*Who Steals from Children: Volume 1 — How Oregon Is Trying to Steal the Elliott State Forest from the Common School Fund*

---

There is a sentence in Oregon law that the State quotes in its own glossy publications and forgets when it sits down to do business. It is the opening line of the Public Meetings Law, ORS 192.620:

> *The Oregon form of government requires an informed public aware of the deliberations and decisions of governing bodies and the information upon which such decisions were made.*

That is not a preamble. That is a statutory finding — the legislature's official recital of why Oregon has a Public Meetings Law in the first place. It places the public's awareness on the same footing as the deliberation itself. A decision that the public cannot see being made is not, under Oregon law, a decision Oregon's form of government has authorized.

I want to walk you through what happened to that sentence when the State and OSU sat down to take the Elliott.

## What the law actually says

Oregon Public Meetings Law lives in ORS chapter 192, sections 610 through 690. It defines a *meeting* as the convening of a governing body for which a quorum is required to make a decision or to deliberate toward a decision. It defines *governing body* broadly enough to include advisory groups created by, and operating under, a public body. The companion Public Records Law, ORS 192.311–192.478, says records held by a public body — emails, drafts, minutes, contracts, the records of advisory bodies — are presumptively public, subject to narrow exemptions. The default is disclosure. The burden to withhold is on the agency.

What these two statutes mean together is straightforward. When the Department of State Lands, the State Land Board, and OSU sat down to plan the conversion of a $1-billion-plus trust asset out of the Common School Fund, the public had a statutory right to be in the room — or, at minimum, to be reading the meeting minutes the next morning. The public was not in the room. The minutes do not exist. And when citizens asked for the records that should have memorialized the deliberations, the records did not come.

## The DSL/OSU Memorandum of Understanding

The most consequential single document of the secret-meetings era is the Memorandum of Understanding between DSL and the OSU College of Forestry. The MOU set up the Science Advisory Panel and the working architecture under which OSU's "Elliott State Research Forest Proposal" was developed.

The MOU is a contract between a state agency that holds a trust asset in fiduciary capacity and a state university whose College of Forestry stood to gain operational control of that asset. It was negotiated, drafted, and executed without public process — no public hearing on whether to enter into it, no comment period, no notice to the Common School Fund's beneficiaries, no published trust-impact analysis.

As I wrote at the time on the ElliottSecrets website, the MOU "effectively deputizes OSU as an official part of the State Lands Board public planning process." That deputization had a consequence the agencies seem not to have considered: when you deputize a university as part of your public planning process, you bring it inside the Public Meetings Law. The Science Advisory Panel created under that MOU is the governing body of a public body. Its meetings were required to be public. They weren't.

## "A secret meeting about going public"

On May 27, 2020, Anthony S. Davis, the Interim Dean of the OSU College of Forestry, sent my wife Barb and me an email. He was, on his own initiative, telling us about the next scheduled Science Advisory Panel meeting. The meeting was set for May 29, 2020. The subject of the meeting, Davis wrote, was "the process that the panel will use to receive and share comments, questions, and information from the public."

Read that again. The Science Advisory Panel was about to meet in private to decide how it would later be public.

I have been a systems scientist for forty years, and I have written textbooks about how organizations think. The Davis email is one of the cleanest specimens of bureaucratic self-blindness I have ever encountered. The College of Forestry's interim dean saw nothing wrong with telling two members of the public that the Panel he chaired was about to convene a private meeting whose entire subject was how to manage the public. He did not realize he was confessing.

I called the email what it was on the ElliottSecrets website: "the purpose of this meeting was quite ironic: to discuss in secret how to go about sharing information with the public." It is also, on its face, a violation of ORS 192.620.

The email does something more important than violate a statute. It documents the operating method. The Panel's posture toward the public is not the posture of a fiduciary body deliberating in front of its beneficiaries. It is the posture of an institution that has already decided what to do and is engineering the rollout. The deliberation has already happened. What remains is the choreography. I named this pattern at the top of the ElliottSecrets home page:

> *Each Department of State Lands "public engagement opportunity" follows a theatrical script: First, a series of experts explains what has already been decided and done, then each member of the public is allowed five minutes of polite attention in front of panelists who pretend to listen. If an interested outsider wants to be really involved or find out how decisions are really being made, no one has any time to spare.*

I called it *public engagement theater*. The Davis email is the stage manager's note.

## The redacted Advisory Committee roster

A second artifact tells the same story from a different angle.

OSU produced, in support of its Elliott State Research Forest Proposal, an ESRF Advisory Committee — a body whose members were expected to lend their names and their expertise to the proposal's credibility. When public-records requests were made for the roster of that Advisory Committee, the State produced the document with the membership list redacted.

Stop and consider what that means. A trustee body for a public school-trust asset is operating without disclosing who its members are. The Common School Fund's beneficiaries — Oregon schoolchildren — and the Common School Fund's effective constituency — Oregon parents and school-district administrators — do not know who is making the recommendations that will determine the fate of their forest. The membership of a body whose work is shaping the disposition of a $1-billion-plus trust corpus is treated as if it were the membership of a witness-protection program.

The redactions themselves are evidence. The State cannot, under the Public Records Law, lawfully redact the names of members of an advisory committee constituted under a public agency's planning process. There is no exemption that covers it. The exemption invoked was — and remains — a guess at what might fit; the actual statutory basis is thin to nonexistent. The State redacted because the State chose to redact, and the State chose to redact because the State preferred opacity.

A redacted trustee body is a contradiction in terms.

## Bob Zybach's two ignored requests

The pattern was not theoretical, and I was not the only person noticing.

Bob Zybach holds a PhD in Environmental Sciences from OSU. His dissertation is the definitive history of fire in the Oregon Coast Range. He has spent thirty years documenting the Elliott's history and management. By any reasonable standard he is, as I wrote at the time, "the world's foremost scholarly expert on the Elliott State Forest and its history." He is exactly the kind of citizen any well-functioning agency would treat as a resource. DSL treated him as a nuisance.

On December 10, 2019, Bob sent Meliah Masiba at DSL a public-records request asking for basic planning and Habitat Conservation Plan budget figures for the prior twelve months. He had an article deadline of December 16 and wanted the figures in order to write accurately. DSL did not respond.

On April 1, 2020, Bob sent a second, more detailed request — asking for "a reasonably detailed accounting of DSL/OSU/Elliott funding and expenditures for the past two years," and noting that the most recent allocation in his line of sight was "$500,000+ ... latest Elliott HCP." He also asked again for historical maps and survey documents produced by Jerry Phillips and ODF in the 1950s through 1980s — material he had been requesting "without success, from SLB, ODF, and DSL the past three years." DSL did not respond.

The pattern is the point. DSL did not evaluate Bob's requests on their merits and decline to comply. DSL simply did not respond. The legal right of any Oregonian to inspect a public record was, in DSL's hands, a procedural inconvenience to be allowed to lapse.

Bob's diagnosis was the same as mine. As I wrote on the ElliottSecrets DSL page on May 28, 2020:

> *the information exists, it's readily available, and the DSL is unethically and illegally selecting who gets to see it.*

My own experience was identical. The financial statements I asked for were not produced. The written management plans were not produced. The professional timber cruises any commercial forester would have on file were not produced. The schedules and locations of Science Advisory Panel meetings were not produced until well after the meetings had happened. The underlying data models for the OSU research proposal — the models on which the entire conversion-of-asset case depended — were not produced. On the ElliottSecrets home page I listed five categories of basic information I could not obtain from DSL in early 2020. None was confidential under any plausible reading of the Public Records Law. All five were withheld by default.

## The Cougar Pass refusal

The smallest of the artifacts is the one I keep coming back to.

Cougar Pass Lookout sits on the Elliott. It was one of the dozens of fire lookouts that once protected the Coast Range forests, and like most of them it has been allowed to rot. The Forest Fire Lookout Association tried for four years, from 1999 to 2003, to restore Cougar Pass. Howard Verschoor of the Oregon chapter described what happened: they were "put off until they gave up." Bob Zybach and I, working through ORWW, prepared a Cougar Pass Lookout Education Center proposal in early 2020 and presented it to DSL. The proposal cost the State nothing — we proposed to fund the restoration ourselves through a separate non-profit at cougarpass.org. DSL's formal written response was three short sentences:

> *Because of the potential transfer of the Elliott to Oregon State University, the Department of State Lands is unable to dedicate time and resources to a potential project at this point in time.*

Read it slowly. The agency is not declining because the proposal is unsound or because the cost is wrong. The agency is declining because it is busy planning to give the Elliott away, and a proposal that would attach a small but visible piece of public-good infrastructure to the Elliott during that planning would complicate the giveaway. A lookout that the public visits is a piece of public proprietorship the agency does not want on the books. The Cougar Pass refusal is, in miniature, the whole pattern.

## What trust law actually requires

I am not a lawyer, but I have read enough trust law to know what a fiduciary process looks like when it is functioning. A trustee deliberating about a trust asset is required to deliberate *on the record*. The duties of prudence, of loyalty, and of impartial regard for the beneficiaries' interests all presuppose that the trustee's reasoning is reviewable. The duty to account, codified in Oregon law for over a century and recognized in *Wood v. Honeyman* (1944) as a cornerstone of Oregon trust doctrine, is meaningless if the deliberations producing the accounted-for decisions are sealed. The Restatement (Third) of Trusts is explicit: a trustee must keep the beneficiaries informed of the trust's administration and must respond to reasonable requests for information. There is no exception for school trusts, no exception for state trustees, no exception for trustees who have decided they would rather not.

A fiduciary process conducted in secret is not a fiduciary process. The Elliott decoupling was conducted in secret. The OASTL case is what discovery looks like when the secrecy is forced open.

## Why secrecy was the operative method

I am not claiming that any individual DSL employee, OSU dean, or Land Board appointee sat down and consciously decided to break Oregon's transparency laws. The story is duller and more institutional than that. The conversion of the Elliott from a Common School Fund timber asset into an OSU research forest was, at every step, a project that could not survive sunlight. The financial case was indefensible — a $2.1 million annual operating deficit on an asset that had been the working timber base of the school fund for ninety years. The fiduciary case was indefensible — the State's own 1992 and 2003 AG opinions, the 1977 AG Opinion 7450, and Article VIII of the Oregon Constitution all pointed against it. The public case was indefensible — once Oregonians understood that "research forest" meant "the Common School Fund loses its working asset," they were against it, as the 2017 sale-and-reverse demonstrated.

A project with that profile has two options. It can be abandoned, or it can be advanced in the dark. The agencies chose the second. They chose it not because anyone made a speech in favor of secrecy, but because each individual step — the MOU, the Science Advisory Panel charter, the redactions, the silence on records requests, the Cougar Pass refusal — was easier to defend internally than the alternative of doing the work in public and watching the public say no. Bureaucracies do what is internally easy. The cumulative effect of a thousand internally-easy choices is institutional capture of a trust.

Once you see the pattern, you see it everywhere in the documentary record. The State's October 2025 Answer contains the language "does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged." The State's response to the February 2026 Request for Production was, in Daniel Crowe's words, a stonewall — "general objections" lodged where particular responses were called for. The April 2026 motion to compel that Daniel filed and that the Coos County Circuit Court accepted on May 2 is not, fundamentally, a motion about discovery procedure. It is a motion asking a court to do, six years later, what the Public Records Law was supposed to do in real time: force the trustee to produce the deliberations. The 2020 pattern is the 2026 pattern.

## What the documentary record changed

For four years — from 2017, when the secret meetings began, through 2021, when I filed my own first lawsuit — the conversion advanced because it was opaque. The State did not need to defend a fiduciary process it had not held; it only needed to refer the public, when the public asked, to the deliberations the State had not memorialized. Opacity was structural, and the structural opacity was the State's strongest defense.

The ElliottSecrets website, which I built in May 2020 and which now survives in the Internet Archive's Wayback snapshot, did one useful thing. It captured the Davis email verbatim. It captured the Zybach records requests verbatim. It captured the Cougar Pass refusal verbatim. It quoted the statutory language of ORS 192.620. It named the pattern. It put on the public record, in plain language and with primary-source attachments, what the agencies were doing while they were doing it.

That documentary record is why the 2024 case is winnable. The State has spent six years refusing to deliberate on the record about the Elliott. The plaintiffs have spent six years building a parallel record of that refusal. When the court orders the State to produce its files — and after Daniel's motion to compel, the court will — the State will be answering questions whose evidentiary scaffolding the plaintiffs have already built. The 2026 discovery fight is not the beginning of the documentary case. It is the end of it. The beginning was the Davis email.

The State told us in writing in 2020 that it could not "dedicate time and resources" to a no-cost restoration of a fire lookout on the Elliott because of "the potential transfer of the Elliott to Oregon State University." It did not realize that by writing that sentence it was confessing to having already decided on the transfer — three years before OSU's President Murthy would, in November 2023, formally walk away from it. The decision was made in private. The withdrawal was made in public. The conversion continued anyway. That is the subject of the next chapter.
