# Chapter 5 — The Sale That Didn't Happen

> *"It was a breach of our ethics."*
>
> — Dennis Richardson, Oregon Secretary of State, on the State Land Board's reversal of the Elliott sale, May 9, 2017

I want to take you back to February of 2017, because this is the chapter where I first thought we had won, and where I learned, eventually, that we had not.

I had been paying attention to the Elliott for about twenty years by that point. I knew, in the general way a tree-farmer in Kings Valley knew, that something was wrong with how the State of Oregon was managing the forest. I knew the harvest line had been falling. I knew Jerry Phillips's history of the Elliott — the careful, multi-decade record of a working trust asset producing real revenue for Oregon's schoolchildren. I had walked enough of the Coast Range by then to recognize when a forest was being managed and when it was being left to rot. I did not yet, in February of 2017, know what I would know two years later, which was that the State of Oregon had decided, in offices the public would never see, that the Elliott was a problem to be dissolved.

What I knew was that the State Land Board had voted to sell it.

## The vote

On Tuesday, February 14, 2017, the three trustees of the Common School Fund met in Salem and voted on whether to accept a bid of $220.8 million from a consortium led by Lone Rock Resources — a Roseburg-based timber company, partnered with the Cow Creek Band of Umpqua Tribe of Indians and an unnamed conservation organization. The bid had come in through a Real Property Disposition Protocol the Board had adopted in December 2015. The Protocol was the procedural vehicle. The Elliott was the asset it had been written for.

The vote was two to one in favor of selling.

Governor Kate Brown voted no. Secretary of State Dennis Richardson voted yes. State Treasurer Tobias Read voted yes. By the Land Board's procedural rules, two-to-one was a vote to proceed.

Mark the Treasurer's vote, because we will see Tobias Read again in this book. In 2022, voting to endorse the OSU research-forest transfer. In 2023, voting on the bond compensation that became a substitute for the sale he had voted for in 2017. In 2025, sitting on the Board that authorized the legal posture the State of Oregon currently holds in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* — the posture that the State *does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged.* Read has been continuous on this question across his time as Treasurer and into his current term as Secretary of State. The continuity is worth noticing.

I want to be careful here, because I am writing about people I have voted for and people I have not, and the case I am making is not partisan. It is fiduciary. The trustees who sat on the State Land Board in February 2017 had been told, by twenty-five years of staff packages flowing up from the Department of State Lands, that the Elliott was a drain on the Common School Fund and that the trust would be better served by selling the asset and reinvesting the proceeds. They believed what they had been told. The bid was within the framework the agency had built. The vote was, from inside the framework, the procedurally correct move.

The framework was wrong. But the framework is not what the public reacted to.

## The number

The $220.8 million was not a market estimate. It was what the State's chosen appraiser — Mason Bruce & Girard — concluded the Elliott was worth as a constrained asset under existing endangered-species and management restrictions. The agency called this an "investment value." Independent estimates of the Elliott's actual market value, prepared by Bill Lansing and other timber-industry veterans over the years that followed, ran from one billion to one and three-tenths billion dollars.

The State's appraisal was less than a fifth of the asset's market value. Margaret Bird, who would not enter this story for another five years, eventually wrote the cleanest version of the objection. She said the State had valued the Elliott the way you would value a Ford pickup at the price you were willing to pay rather than what it would fetch on the open lot. The math is not subtle. Either Lansing was wrong by an order of magnitude — which no one with timber-industry knowledge has been willing to claim in print — or the State was preparing to dispose of trust property at less than open-market value.

I want to put that number in your hand now, because it matters in a way the agency did not, at the time, intend. The $220.8 million was the open-market floor. It was the price the consortium had been willing to bid in a real, advertised, qualifying transaction. It was the lowest number that any subsequent appraisal of the Elliott could honestly produce without explaining why the asset had lost value in the interim. The 2017 number was the bid the State had accepted, on the record, as the qualifying price under its own Protocol.

Every subsequent appraisal the State commissioned — and there would be several, leading into the 2019 bond compensation and the 2022 transfer — would come in below $220.8 million. The number that had been the floor in 2017 became the ceiling in 2019, the ceiling became the marker in 2022, and the marker eventually became the basis for moving the Elliott off the trust's books at $99.6 million. The 2017 floor was suppressed by the appraisals that followed it. I did not know to watch for this in 2017. I should have.

## The protest

What happened between February 14 and May 9 was the part the agency's planning had not prepared for.

The framing went up almost immediately. *The State is selling the schoolchildren's forest to a private timber company.* That sentence, in some version of it, appeared on signs at protests, in letters to the editor, in op-eds in *The Oregonian* and *The Register-Guard*, on radio, in the legislature, and in school district newsletters across the Coast. Coast residents, school districts, environmental groups, hunting and fishing communities, Indian tribes who were not part of the consortium, and a coalition I could not have predicted three months earlier all converged on a single position: do not sell the Elliott.

The signs that summer read *Save the Children's Forest*. I want to make a small observation about those signs that will matter in Chapter 7. There were two sign campaigns running the same words. One set of signs was carried by the Coast Range residents, school districts, and environmental advocates who were trying to stop the sale. The other set was eventually printed by the State and its allies, who used the same language to brand the transfer that would come later. The slogan was ours first. The slogan became theirs. By 2022 the children's-forest framing had been folded into the agency's own marketing for the OSU research forest — the same forest, in the same trust posture, going to a different buyer under a different label. I did not, in 2017, anticipate that you could lose a slogan. You can.

The public response was substantially organized by people who were not part of any institutional school-trust constituency, because there was no institutional school-trust constituency to organize it. Environmental advocates carried the framing. Coast Range residents carried the practical knowledge of what the forest was. School districts in Coos and Curry and Douglas counties carried the beneficiary credibility. The Oregon School Boards Association and the Oregon Education Association were largely silent, as they had been for decades on these questions, and as they would be again. The press, briefly, paid attention.

## The reversal

The Board met again on May 9, 2017.

Governor Brown's position had hardened. Treasurer Read's had moved. Secretary Richardson's was unchanged but now in the minority. By a two-to-one vote — Brown and Read in the majority, Richardson dissenting — the Board reversed the February decision. The Lone Rock bid was rejected. The Elliott was, for the moment, off the market.

Dennis Richardson, voting in the minority, said the reversal was *"a breach of our ethics."* He was not making the argument I would have made. He was making the argument of a trustee who had committed to a public bidding process and was watching that commitment be set aside under political pressure. Toby Luther, the chief executive of Lone Rock, made the same point in plainer language. *"You cannot simultaneously encourage bidders and commit to bidders publicly while privately planning a shift in policy."* Luther was speaking for his company, which had spent real money preparing a qualifying bid under the Protocol the State had advertised. He was also, although neither he nor Richardson framed it this way at the time, articulating a fiduciary principle. A trustee that issues a public solicitation, accepts a qualifying bid, and then walks the bid back under political pressure has not behaved like a trustee. It has behaved like a counterparty negotiating in bad faith.

I am not, in this book, going to make the case that the State should have sold the Elliott to Lone Rock. I do not believe that. I believe the underlying premise of the sale — that the Elliott was a net drain that justified disposing of the asset — was wrong, and I believe the price was wrong, and I believe the trustee posture was wrong. But Richardson and Luther were right about something the protest movement was not focused on. The institution had not changed its mind. It had been forced to back away from a particular mechanism. The reasoning was untouched.

#### Who Sits on the State Land Board?

The Oregon State Land Board has three seats. Article VIII, Section 5 of the Oregon Constitution names them: the Governor, the Secretary of State, and the State Treasurer. The seats are ex officio — whoever holds the elected office sits on the Board for the term of the office. The Land Board is the trustee of the Common School Fund. The Department of State Lands is the agency that staffs it. The Board meets roughly every two months. Its decisions, by majority vote, are the final administrative word on the management of the Common School Fund's trust assets — including the Elliott State Forest.

In February 2017, the three trustees were Governor Kate Brown, Secretary of State Dennis Richardson, and State Treasurer Tobias Read.

The Board today, in May 2026, is **Governor Tina Kotek**, **Secretary of State Tobias Read**, and **State Treasurer Elizabeth Steiner**. Kotek succeeded Kate Brown in January 2023. Read moved from the Treasurer's seat to the Secretary of State's seat in January 2025, succeeding LaVonne Griffin-Valade, who herself had been appointed in 2023 after Shemia Fagan's resignation and who did not seek election to a full term. Elizabeth Steiner succeeded Read at Treasury.

Two things to notice. First, the seats are constitutionally designated to elected officers who hold them for other purposes — running the executive branch, administering elections, managing the state's investment portfolio — and who add the trusteeship of a billion-dollar trust to that workload. Second, Tobias Read has now sat on the Land Board through three Land Board postures on the Elliott: as Treasurer voting to sell in 2017, as Treasurer voting to compensate in 2019, and as Secretary of State in the room when the State filed its 2025 Answer denying that the State owes the duties alleged.

</aside>

## What I thought it meant

I drove home from following the May 9 vote thinking the public had won.

The forest had not been sold. The framing had held. The Coast Range had spoken. The Board had listened. The pattern I recognized, from outside the institution, was the pattern of democracy functioning — public pressure on elected trustees producing a course correction in real time. I told my wife Barbara that afternoon that the Elliott was safe. I told the OSWA people I knew that the system had worked. I told myself I had watched a trust be defended by its beneficiaries' larger civic community, and that the Land Board, however close it had come, had ultimately done its job.

I thought, then, that the May 2017 reversal was the win. I was wrong. It took me two more years to see why.

What I did not yet understand was that the State Land Board, in May of 2017, had not reversed its theory of the Elliott. It had reversed its method. Governor Brown's stated reasons for moving against the sale, in her public remarks before and after the vote, were political. The public outcry made the sale infeasible. The political costs of proceeding were higher than the agency had anticipated. The trust's interests, she said, were better served by exploring "alternative ownership structures" that would address the underlying drain on the Common School Fund without triggering the public protest a private sale had produced. Treasurer Read's remarks were similar in substance, although shorter on detail.

Neither trustee said *we were wrong about the underlying theory of the case.* Neither said *the Elliott is not a net drain on the Common School Fund and we should manage it as a working asset.* Neither said *the 1977 Attorney General Opinion that binds this Board to keep school-trust land producing for the schools is the floor we will not go below.* Neither said *the trust's beneficiaries should be at this table and we are creating institutional space for them.*

What the Board said, on May 9, 2017, in plain English, was this. We will not sell the Elliott to Lone Rock. We will continue to look for other ways to address the problem the Elliott has become.

The 2017 sale was reversed. The 2017 reasoning behind the sale was not.

I am repeating that sentence because it is the load-bearing sentence of this chapter, and because the institutional move that followed it took place in offices the public did not see, on a timetable the public did not control, under a logic the agencies had spent twenty-five years building up. The post-May-2017 record is the record of an institution that had been told its preferred mechanism was unavailable, and that had begun, by August of that year, to scout the next one.

The next mechanism would not be a private sale. The next mechanism would be quieter. It would not produce a sign campaign. It would not produce front-page coverage in *The Oregonian*. It would move through the Oregon Legislature in the ordinary course of business, with the Land Board's blessing, and it would convert the trust's claim on a working forest into the trust's claim on a line item in the state budget. The Department of State Lands began calling the conversion *decoupling*. Margaret Bird, when she eventually saw what the agency was doing, called it the euphemism it was. We will get to Margaret in Chapter 9.

What I want you to carry forward into Chapter 6 is the structural picture. The Elliott was not saved in May 2017. The Elliott's sale was deferred. The Board's working theory — that the Elliott was a problem to be dissolved — survived intact. The trust's beneficiaries had no seat at the table where the next mechanism was being designed, because there was, in May of 2017, no institutional school-trust constituency in Oregon that could have put one there. The *Save the Children's Forest* signs came down. The agency went quiet. The framework went underground.

Two years later, on March 21, 2019, the State of Oregon would announce the issuance of $100 million in general-obligation bonds to "compensate" the Common School Fund for reduced timber revenue from the Elliott. When I read the announcement that morning, I thought, again, that the trust was being made whole. I was, again, wrong. The number I should have been watching — the $220.8 million floor, the open-market price Lone Rock had been willing to pay — had been moved off the table along with the sale itself. The new number was less than half of it. Nobody asked why.

That is the next chapter.
