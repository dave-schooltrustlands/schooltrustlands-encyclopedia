# Chapter 10 — The First Lawsuit and What I Learned

> *"Dave you are a true beaver and work hard keeping the water flowing over the dam."*
>
> — David Gould, in correspondence to the author

I want to tell you about the first lawsuit. Not because the first lawsuit won — it didn't. Not because the first lawsuit was a good lawsuit — by the standards a lawyer would apply, it wasn't. I want to tell you about it because the first lawsuit is the part of this story that I, looking back from the desk where I am writing this chapter, am least proud of and most grateful for. It taught me almost everything I did not know about how the law works when the State of Oregon is the defendant. And it would not surprise me if, by the end of this chapter, you have come to the same conclusion I came to a few years after I filed it: that the most important thing about the first lawsuit was that I was wrong about almost every premise on which I filed it, and that being wrong, in writing, in a courtroom, in front of a judge, is the only way I could have learned what the law was actually responding to.

I filed it in 2021. Coos County Circuit Court. *Sullivan v. State of Oregon*, case number 21CV15024. I was the plaintiff. I was sixty-five years old. I was not a lawyer. I am still not a lawyer. I am a retired Tektronix engineer and Oregon State University business professor who, twenty-five years earlier, had bought a tree farm in Kings Valley and become slowly, over those twenty-five years, a person who could not stop thinking about what the State of Oregon was doing to its school trust.

The facts, when I filed in 2021, were already strong. The Elliott State Forest had been pulled out of harvest in stages across the 2010s. The Common School Fund, which the Elliott was supposed to be feeding, was being fed by the State's own debt instead — $100 million in general-obligation bonds in 2019, with more on the way. The State Land Board had voted in 2017 to sell the Elliott to Lone Rock for $220.8 million, reversed under public pressure, and then quietly resumed the conversion through a different mechanism. Bob Zybach had filed a declaration on July 11, 2021 walking through what the Elliott had produced when the State managed it as a trust asset rather than a problem to be dissolved. David Gould had filed his own declaration that summer. I had filed an Affidavit and Timeline of Facts on August 16, 2021.

I thought, when we filed, that the law would respond to the facts. The facts were strong. The law moved slowly. And the slowness, I had to learn, was the State's strongest defense.

## What I thought I was doing

I want to say this honestly. In 2021 I thought I was filing a lawsuit. What I was actually doing was something closer to filing a complaint in the colloquial sense — the sense in which a person who has been wronged writes down what happened, signs it, and hands it to whoever is in a position to make it stop. I had the documents. I had the witnesses. I had a forester with a PhD on Coast Range fire history willing to swear an affidavit. I had a four-generation Coos County family man — David Gould, whose great-grandparents had homesteaded the Elkhorn Ranch that sits inside today's Elliott — willing to swear an affidavit. I had what I thought a court would recognize as the truth.

What I did not have was a lawyer who had spent twenty years filing the particular kind of pleading that the Oregon Supreme Court would later, in a different case, hold sufficient to confer standing on a schoolchild-plaintiff to enforce the school trust against the State. I did not have the doctrine. I did not have the procedural posture. I did not have the body of case law a lawyer organizes a complaint around when the lawyer knows what the appellate court is going to want to see if the case gets that far.

I had filed, in other words, the sort of lawsuit a retired professor files when he has read enough to know there is a wrong and not enough to know what the law's particular instruments are for vindicating the wrong. The trial court did with that lawsuit what trial courts do with that kind of lawsuit. The State of Oregon moved to dismiss. The trial court granted the motion. The matter, on its own merits, did not advance.

I want to be careful here. The 2021 case did not establish a holding on the substantive school-trust questions. It did not survive to discovery. The merits — whether the State had breached its fiduciary duties; whether the bonds were a permissible substitution for the Elliott corpus; whether the conversion to a research forest was self-dealing — were never reached. The 2021 case, as a matter of formal Oregon case law, accomplished nothing.

And I would file it again tomorrow.

## What I learned

What the 2021 case did do, and what I did not understand it was doing until later, was establish the record. The State's response to a citizen-plaintiff in 2021 became a fact a court could later notice. Zybach's declaration is on file. Gould's declaration is on file. My Affidavit and Timeline of Facts is on file. The State's posture — that the question of how the Common School Fund is managed is not a question a citizen of Oregon has any business asking a court of Oregon about — was made of record. The next set of lawyers who walked into the Coos County Circuit Court on the school-trust question did not have to invent that record. It was there.

I learned three things from the dismissal. I am going to name them plainly because they are the things I would say to any reader of this book who is angry on her children's behalf and is wondering whether to file something herself.

The first is that the law, when the State is the defendant, moves on a clock the State controls. The State files for an extension. The State asks the court for time to confer. The State moves to dismiss; the dismissal is appealed; the appeal is briefed; the briefing is responded to; the response is replied to; the case is set for oral argument; oral argument is held; the opinion is reserved; the opinion is issued; the matter is remanded; the trial court takes the remand; the trial court sets a status conference. Each of those steps is months. Stacked together, they are years. The clock the State runs is the clock no individual citizen can outlast. The State's lawyers go home at five and come back in the morning. The State's lawyers do not bear the cost of the delay because the delay is what the State is paying them to produce.

The second is that standing — the legal doctrine that decides whether a particular plaintiff is allowed to bring a particular claim in a particular court — is the gate the State will close first. Standing sounds like a technicality. It is not a technicality. Standing is the question of whether the courthouse door is open to you at all. When the trial court dismissed the 2021 case, the dismissal did not address whether the Common School Fund was being mismanaged. The dismissal addressed whether Dave Sullivan, retired professor from Kings Valley, was the right person to ask the court the question. And on that question, in 2021, the answer the court gave was no.

The third is that being wrong, in writing, in a courthouse, leaves a record the next plaintiff inherits. The 2021 case was not the case that won. The 2021 case was the case that taught the next case what the State would say, what the State would not say, what the State's preferred procedural ground would be, what the State's silence would cover. Natalie Scott, when she filed the 2023 case, did not have to guess what the State would do. The 2021 record told her.

<aside class="readers-edition-aside">

#### What is Standing?

Standing is the doctrine that decides whether a particular person is the right person to bring a particular legal claim. The federal courts and the Oregon courts use the doctrine to keep courts from deciding abstract questions — to keep them, in the textbook phrase, deciding *cases and controversies* rather than hypotheticals. To have standing, a plaintiff must show three things: a concrete injury, fairly traceable to the defendant's conduct, that the court has power to redress.

The school-trust problem is that the most obvious beneficiaries of the trust — the schoolchildren of Oregon — are, as a class, the people whom courts have historically been quickest to deny standing to. They are minors. They have no money. They do not vote. They cannot, as a practical matter, hire a lawyer themselves. For one hundred and sixty-seven years after the 1859 Admissions Act created the trust, no Oregon court had held that a schoolchild-plaintiff could walk into a courtroom and assert the trust against the State that holds it for her.

Article VIII, Section 8 of the Oregon Constitution — the 2000 amendment that requires the Legislature to fund schools at a level sufficient to meet the standards adopted by the State Board of Education — is what eventually broke that wall. Section 8 makes the State's duty to fund the schools an enforceable duty rather than a hortatory one. And once the duty to fund is enforceable, the management of the trust that the funding flows from becomes reviewable too — because a court's decision on how the trust is managed has, as the Court of Appeals would later write, *practical effects on the amount that the common school fund contributes to the schoolchildren plaintiffs' education.*

That sentence is what we waited 167 years to read.

</aside>

## Natalie

In 2023, Natalie Scott of The Scott Law Group filed a new case. Coos County 23CV39056. Schoolchildren-plaintiffs as the named beneficiaries. Article VIII fiduciary-breach claims. A clean re-rendering of what I had filed in 2021, drafted by a lawyer who had clerked on the Oregon Court of Appeals for Judge David Brewer, who had graduated *summa cum laude* from the University of Oregon School of Law, and who knew exactly which sentence the appellate court was going to need to see if the trial court denied standing.

The trial court denied standing. In February 2024 the case was dismissed.

Natalie filed the appeal. Her Opening Brief, filed August 2, 2024, argued the case the 2021 record had taught her to argue: that Article VIII Section 8 gives schoolchildren-plaintiffs the standing the trial court had denied them. The State responded. Natalie replied. The Court of Appeals scheduled oral argument.

I will say more about that oral argument in Chapter 11. Natalie made a decision, when the Court set the date, that I want to tell you about now because it bears on this chapter. She asked the Court whether oral argument could be held in front of the named plaintiffs. The Court agreed. Oral argument in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* was held at Corvallis High School, with real Oregon schoolchildren — the named beneficiaries of the trust the case was about — sitting in the audience while the State of Oregon's lawyer argued that those schoolchildren had no legal interest in the management of the trust their constitutional rights named them as the beneficiaries of. I sat in the back. The contrast between what the State's brief was saying and the room the brief was being read in did more rhetorical work for our side than any of us had planned for.

Natalie won. On January 28, 2026, the Court of Appeals reversed the trial court and held that the schoolchildren-plaintiffs had standing under Article VIII Section 8 to enforce the school trust against the State. The opinion was unanimous. The case was remanded for further proceedings on the merits.

That is the standing victory that Chapter 11 takes up. What I want to mark in this chapter is the through-line: the 2021 case that did not win taught the 2023 case what to file. The 2023 case that did not win at trial taught the appeal what to argue. The appeal won. The constituency that had been missing inside the courthouse for one hundred and sixty-seven years finally had the doctrinal posture from which to assert the trust.

## October 2025 — looking for the right lawyer

I want to tell you about a stretch of weeks in the fall of 2025 that I think about often. The 2024 case had been filed the previous August. Natalie was carrying the 2023 appeal. The Coos County Circuit Court had scheduled a hearing on a Motion to Strike for October 28. We needed a lawyer to make a limited-scope appearance for that hearing. I did not yet have one.

On October 20, 2025, I sat at my kitchen table in Kings Valley and sent the same email, with small adjustments, to six Oregon attorneys. John Whitty. J. MacNeille at the Oregon Law Center. James Martin. J. DePlois. Amber Gies. A lawyer named Klessig. The email said, in essence, that there was a hearing on October 28 in Coos County, that the case was a school-trust fiduciary-breach case against the State of Oregon, that we needed appearance counsel for that one hearing, and that we would be grateful for any of their time. I included a one-page case summary. I included Natalie's contact information. I signed it Dave Sullivan, OASTL President.

I want to tell you what it is like to send those emails. You are not asking for someone's help in a sense that they are required, by any obligation they have to you, to respond. They have not heard of you. They have not, in most cases, heard of the case. They have their own dockets. They have their own clients. They are members of the Oregon Bar in a state where the legal community is small enough that the State of Oregon's appearance on the other side of a case is not a neutral fact. You are asking strangers to step, on short notice and for limited compensation, into a courtroom where the State will be the opposing party. Most of them, very kindly, said no.

James Martin said maybe. James, who corresponds through the email address jamesm@epuerto.org, reviewed the case files I sent him. He wrote back, on November 25, that he was willing in principle to represent OASTL in the 2024 case and to consider taking on the 2023 case as well, "whether on further appeal or on" — the email was longer; that phrase is what I remember. We had several exchanges through early December. James was the bridge.

The week before Christmas, Daniel Crowe came on board.

## Daniel

I want to introduce Daniel Crowe with the same care I tried to give David Gould in Chapter 8, because Daniel is a character in this story for the same reason David was: each of them, at the moment they walked in, brought something the project did not have without them.

Daniel Zene Crowe is a graduate of West Point. He served on active duty in the U.S. Army as an Infantry officer and as a Ranger, retired from the Army Reserve at the rank of Lieutenant Colonel, and spent the bulk of his military legal career as a JAG officer. He is admitted to the Oregon State Bar (OSB Bar Number 050245). He served as the inaugural Oregon Veterans' Advocate, appointed to that statutorily-created role at its first creation, and has served on the Mt. Angel School Board. He is the named plaintiff in *Crowe v. Oregon State Bar*, in which he challenged a state institution in federal court on constitutional grounds and prevailed. He is, in short, the kind of lawyer the State of Oregon would, if it had a choice, prefer not to see across from it in a Coos County courtroom.

Daniel and OASTL signed a Joint Engagement and General Counsel Employment Agreement on December 19, 2025. He came on as General Counsel. Three weeks later, on January 28, 2026, Natalie won the standing appeal. On the same January 28, Daniel served the State with Plaintiffs' First Request for Production of Documents in 24CV38372 — the discovery request that would, by the State's February 27 response, produce the documentary record this book's next chapter is built around.

I want to be honest about what Daniel's arrival changed. The case did not become a different case. The facts did not change. The doctrine did not change. What changed was that the case, for the first time since 2021, had a lawyer in the lead-counsel seat who had spent his life in the kind of room the case was eventually going to be argued in. The State's lawyers, when they read a Crowe-drafted RFP, knew within the first page that the response they had been giving for thirty-five years was not going to be enough. That recognition is not a small thing. It is, in fact, most of the value of having the right counsel.

The State recognized it. The State's October 24, 2025 Answer in 24CV38372 — drafted before Daniel's joint engagement was signed, but filed under the schedule the State controlled — contained a sentence I am going to spend the whole of Chapter 12 on. The sentence asserted that the State "does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged." When I read that sentence at my desk in Kings Valley, late on a Friday afternoon in October, I sat for a long time without moving. The State of Oregon, having served as trustee of the school lands since 1859, had answered a complaint by a coalition of Oregon school districts and Oregon schoolchildren by stating, on the public record of a court of competent jurisdiction, that it did not owe those schoolchildren the duties of a trustee.

That is what the State's strongest defense looks like when the State is asked, in court, what its duties are. The strongest defense, after the slowness has run out, is to deny that the duties exist.

## What I learned about institutional time

I want to close this chapter on the lesson I think is the load-bearing one — not the legal lesson, but the institutional one.

I thought, when I filed in 2021, that the law would respond to the facts. The facts were strong. The law moved slowly. I had to learn what the law was actually responding to.

The law, when the State of Oregon is the defendant, responds to the patient assembly of the right plaintiffs, the right counsel, the right doctrine, the right record, and the right courtroom moment — in something close to that order, and over something close to a decade. The State's institutional time horizon is the time horizon of an entity that, in its corporate person, does not die. The State's lawyers retire and are replaced. The State's commissioners are term-limited and rotated. The State's Land Board changes its three constitutional officers every four years. None of these turnovers reset the clock the State is running on its trust beneficiaries; the clock is continuous, because the institution is continuous, even when no individual inside the institution remembers what the clock is for. That continuity is what makes the State an almost-uncatchable defendant for any individual citizen-plaintiff. The State's slowness becomes the State's strongest defense, because the State can outlast any plaintiff who is not, himself, an institution.

The only thing that can match the State's institutional time horizon is another institution. OASTL is that institution. The Oregon school districts who joined the case — Siuslaw, Powers, Oakland — are that institution. The children, through their guardians, are that institution. Margaret's national network is that institution. Daniel is that institution's general counsel. Natalie is its appellate counsel. The clock the State has been running, since 1859, is finally being met by a clock the beneficiaries can run too.

I filed in 2021 as a plaintiff. I did not understand, in 2021, what the case I was filing was actually for. I thought the case was for the children currently in Oregon's schools. The case is for them — but the case is also for the children fifty years from now, and for the children fifty years after that. The trust is a trust across generations. The defense of the trust has to operate on the same horizon the trust does. That is what I learned. That is what the first lawsuit was for.

The next chapter takes you to Corvallis High School on the morning the Court of Appeals heard oral argument. The State of Oregon, in front of the named beneficiaries of the trust, argued that the named beneficiaries had no legal interest in the trust. The named beneficiaries sat in the audience and listened. The Court of Appeals, on January 28, 2026, told them otherwise.
