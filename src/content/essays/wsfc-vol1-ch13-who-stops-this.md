# Chapter 13 — Who Stops This

A book that documents a theft and then walks away from the reader has not done its work. You have read this far. You are now one of the people who knows. The question this chapter answers is what each kind of person who has read this far can do, beginning the week the book lands in your hands.

I am going to address you by cohort, because the move available to a parent in Coos Bay is not the move available to a reporter at *The Oregonian*, and the move available to an Oregon state senator is not the move available to a trust-law practitioner in Portland. The architecture only works where someone in each chair does the thing that chair can do. So I am going to name the thing.

---

## To Oregon parents and school-district administrators

The asset that was supposed to fund your children's education is being taken. Not in 1907. Now. The Land Board voted, the bonds issued, the Answer was filed, and the State of Oregon is currently on the record in court saying it does not owe your children the duties the 1859 Admissions Act and the Oregon Constitution put on it.

Here is the move. At the next public meeting of your school board, ask the board, on the record, a single question: *What did this district do when the State took the asset that was supposed to fund our children's education?* Bring this book. Set it on the table. The answer you receive will tell you who in your local district has been paying attention, who has been told and forgot, and who has not yet been told.

Then ask the Oregon School Boards Association the same question. OSBA is the institutional voice of the boards that represent the beneficiary class. In October 2016, Margaret Bird stood at an OSBA conference and told them that no state in the 235-year history of school trust lands had spent more than it earned the way Oregon had. Ten years on, the question for OSBA is what it has done with that information.

The Court of Appeals on January 28, 2026 confirmed that schoolchildren have standing to enforce the trust. That is not an academic ruling. It opens the path for organized parent and school-district challenge inside the courthouse, where the State of Oregon now has to answer for the position it has taken. The plaintiffs in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* are five named schoolchildren. The constituency standing behind them is every parent in the state who would like the 1859 promise honored for the children sitting in front of them.

You are that constituency. You were that constituency before you opened the book. You are it more consciously now.

## To Oregon legislators

The path back is statutory and constitutional, and the doctrinal floor for the path back is already on Oregon's books and has been since 1977.

Attorney General Opinion 7450 ruled in 1977 that off-purpose use of the Elliott was prohibited where it adversely affected the fund's financial contribution. That opinion was never amended. Every move the State has made on the Elliott since 1990 has bypassed Opinion 7450 without overturning it. A legislature that wishes to restore the trust architecture does not need to invent new doctrine. It needs to enforce the doctrine that the State's own chief legal officer wrote down forty-nine years ago.

The 2019 bond substitution did not extinguish the trust's claim on the Elliott. It converted the form of the claim — from a claim on a working asset to a claim on a line item in the state budget. That conversion is reversible. The bonds can be retired against an audited accounting that returns the working asset to the trust. The fiduciary posture is recoverable.

SB 147, signed in June 2025, decoupled the Elliott from the Common School Fund. SB 147 is a statute. Statutes can be repealed. The 2022 OSU plan was political; it can be reversed politically, in the same way the 2017 Lone Rock sale was reversed under public pressure once the public could see what was happening.

Court Boice's April 8, 2024 open letter to Governor Kotek, Secretary of State LaVonne Griffin-Valade, and Treasurer Tobias Read — addressing all three Land Board members by name — is in the legislative record as the witness that the structural plan was visible to anyone willing to look. The letter exists. It was sent. It was received. The three names it addresses are the three names that, in the year since, have continued to vote the Land Board in the same direction.

Tobias Read, by name, is the State Treasurer who has voted on this conversion at every Land Board meeting since 2017. Tina Kotek, by name, is the Governor who could intervene. The legislators reading this book have constitutional offices that can act. The Education committee can hold a hearing. The Ways and Means committee can decline to appropriate further support for the conversion. The committee of inquiry the Oregon Constitution permits is on the table any session a legislator chooses to convene one.

You took an oath of office. The oath says you will uphold the Oregon Constitution. Article VIII §§ 2, 5, and 8 of that Constitution put the school trust on you. The path back is not difficult. The path back requires that the people who have sworn the oath act on it.

## To the Oregon press corps

*The Oregonian*. OPB. *Willamette Week*. The Coos Bay *World*. The Eugene *Register-Guard*. The Coast newspapers. The document trove is open.

The OASTL litigation discovery is producing evidence in real time. The State's October 24, 2025 Answer is a public filing in Coos County Circuit Court. The language is on the record: *"does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."* A state, in court, on a public docket, claiming it owes no fiduciary duty to the children it has been holding land in trust for since 1859. That is a story. It has not been written.

The accounting analysis showing public Common School Fund reports diverging from the discovery data is a story. The 2022 OSU $2.1 million annual deficit admission, in OSU's own December 2021 document, is a story. The MOU between DSL and OSU executed under the public-meetings law as the architecture required and then implemented in a sequence of meetings that did not meet the requirements of that law — that is a story.

Margaret Bird's December 2022 letter to the State Land Board is a prelitigation warning that every reporter on the education beat should have seen at the time. *"Decoupling,"* she wrote, *"is a euphemism for selling."* Three and a half years later, the editor of every Oregon paper of record should be asking why no one on the beat surfaced that letter in 2022 or 2023, and what the paper's coverage of the Elliott would have looked like if they had.

I am not asking the press corps to take a side. I am asking it to read the documents that are now public, ask the Land Board members the questions that are now obvious, print what the documents say, and let the reader of the paper draw the conclusion.

## To the trust-and-environmental-law bar

The January 28, 2026 *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* Court of Appeals opinion is the doctrinal anchor for the next case. The standing question is settled. The merits await.

The 1910 New Mexico–Arizona Enabling Act trust framework is the comparative-strength template — the federal architecture other states have used to discipline trustee conduct where Oregon's enabling language is weaker on the page. The 1992 and 2003 Oregon AG opinions are the in-state doctrinal anchors affirming the trust character. The 1977 AG Opinion 7450 is the doctrinal floor that the post-1977 decisions bypassed without amending.

There is a generation of trust-and-environmental law practice waiting for the practitioners who take this case forward. The *Advocates* matter is in trial-court discovery in Coos County. The appellate posture has been opened. The 2023 case is at the Oregon Supreme Court on the petition for review. The work is concrete, the doctrinal posture is favorable, the documentary record is rich, the beneficiary class is sympathetic, and the defendant is the State of Oregon — which has now committed itself, on the record, to the position that it owes the beneficiaries no duty. A bar that wishes to test the limits of state fiduciary liability has been handed a teaching case.

## To the national state-trust-lands community — and to the readers Margaret Bird will be addressing at NASTL in 2026

The Elliott case is the cleanest documentary record currently in flight on the question Margaret has been working on for forty years: how the eight-anchor architecture either holds or fails when the institution begins to harvest itself. Oregon is the worked example. Oregon is the case file.

Volume 1 of *Who Steals from Children* names Oregon as the case study. Volume 2 will name Utah. Volume 3 will name Nebraska. Volume 4 will name Colorado. Subsequent volumes will be added as the cases mature. The series is the field's case file — the documentary record the national community has been waiting for, assembled state by state, from the discovery records and trustee meetings and AG opinions that show where the architecture held and where it gave way.

The architecture you have spent your career inside is being tested now. The volumes are the test results.

## To the watchful crew

That phrase belongs to Margaret Bird. The architecture works where someone is willing to defend it across the decades it has had to run, and the willingness has to be cultivated in each generation because the architecture cannot defend itself. The Elliott case is the live test of whether Oregon's schoolchildren still have such a crew. The reader of this book is, by reading it, candidate membership.

The membership is not formal. There is no roster. There is a recognition, in each chair, that the trust has been put on us and that we are the generation that either keeps it or loses it on our watch. The people in 1859 who wrote the constitutional architecture cannot defend it from where they are. The schoolchildren who are the beneficiaries cannot defend it from where they are. Margaret Bird, Daniel Crowe, the OASTL board, the five named plaintiffs, the Coos County reporter who has been covering the case, the school-board member in eastern Oregon who has begun asking questions — these are the watchful crew currently on station. There are not enough of them. The ones who have just finished this book are who fills the gap.

---

The 1859 architecture said the Elliott belongs to Oregon's schoolchildren forever. The State of Oregon does not currently believe that. The work in front of us is to make Oregon believe it again, on the documentary record this book has put on the page.

Who steals from children? On the evidence we now hold, the State of Oregon.

The next move belongs to the people who, having read this far, are going to refuse to let that stand.

— Dave Sullivan, Coos County, Oregon, 2026.
