# Chapter 13 — Who Stops This

> *"The forest belongs to the children. The trust says so. The Constitution says so. The State of Oregon says, in writing, that it does not."*
>
> — from the Preface

A book that documents a theft and then walks away from the reader has not done its work. You have read this far. You are now one of the people who knows.

I am going to address you by cohort, because the move available to a parent in Coos Bay is not the move available to a state senator, and the move available to a state senator is not the move available to a reporter at *The Oregonian*. The architecture only holds where someone in each chair does the thing that chair can do. So I am going to name the thing.

---

## To Oregon parents and school-district administrators

The asset that was supposed to fund your children's education is being taken. Not in 1907. Now. The Land Board voted. The bonds issued. The Answer was filed. The State of Oregon is currently on the record in Coos County Circuit Court saying it does not owe your children the duties the 1859 Admissions Act and the Oregon Constitution put on it.

Here is your move. At the next public meeting of your school board, ask the board, on the record, a single question: *What did this district do when the State took the asset that was supposed to fund our children's education?* Bring this book. Set it on the table. The answer you receive will tell you who in your local district has been paying attention, who has been told and forgot, and who has not yet been told. If you are a superintendent or a business manager, the same question is yours to put to district counsel: *Has this district preserved its standing to be heard on the Common School Fund?* The Court of Appeals settled the standing question on January 28, 2026. The next move is for districts to use it.

Then carry the question one rung up. The Oregon School Boards Association is the institutional voice of the boards that represent the beneficiary class. In October 2016, Margaret Bird stood at an OSBA conference and told them that no state in the 235-year history of school trust lands had spent more than it earned the way Oregon had. Ten years on, the question for OSBA is what it has done with what she told them. Your board can put that question on the OSBA agenda. One resolution from one district, picked up by ten more, becomes a statewide caucus inside the association that represents you.

If you are a parent who is not on the board and not on a district staff, the move is smaller and just as load-bearing. Lend the book to one other parent. Forward the Library link to your PTA. Show up to a State Land Board meeting once — they are public; the agendas are online; the schoolchildren who are the named plaintiffs in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* have already been in that room. Their constituency is every parent in the state who would like the 1859 promise honored for the children sitting in front of them. You were that constituency before you opened this book. You are it more consciously now.

## To Oregon legislators

The path back is statutory and constitutional, and most of the doctrinal floor for the path back is already on Oregon's books and has been since 1977.

Attorney General Opinion 7450, issued that year, ruled that off-purpose use of the Elliott was prohibited where it adversely affected the fund's financial contribution. The opinion was never amended. Every move the State has made on the Elliott since the early 1990s has bypassed Opinion 7450 without overturning it. A legislature that wishes to restore the trust does not need to invent new doctrine. It needs to enforce the doctrine the State's own chief legal officer wrote down forty-nine years ago.

The 2019 bond substitution did not extinguish the trust's claim on the Elliott. It converted the form of the claim from a working asset to a line item in the state budget. The bonds can be retired against an audited accounting that returns the working asset to the trust. The fiduciary posture is recoverable.

SB 147, passed in June 2025, repealed the statutory provision that had since the 1950s indefinitely withdrawn the Elliott from sale. SB 147 is a statute. Statutes can be repealed. The 2022 OSU plan was political; it can be reversed politically, in the same way the 2017 Lone Rock sale was reversed under public pressure once the public could see what was happening.

Court Boice's April 8, 2024 open letter — addressed by name to Governor Tina Kotek, Secretary of State LaVonne Griffin-Valade, and Treasurer Tobias Read — is on the public record as the witness that the structural plan was visible to anyone willing to look. The letter exists. It was sent. It was received. Tobias Read, by name, is the State Treasurer who has voted on this conversion at every Land Board meeting since he was Treasurer. Tina Kotek, by name, is the Governor who could intervene and has not. Each of you reading this who holds a legislative office knows what your committee can do. Education can hold a hearing. Ways and Means can decline further support for the conversion. The committee of inquiry the Oregon Constitution permits is on the table any session a legislator chooses to convene one.

You took an oath of office. The oath says you will uphold the Oregon Constitution. Article VIII, Sections 2, 5, and 8 of that Constitution put the school trust on you. We are not asking you to violate your oath. We are asking you to act on it.

## To the Oregon press corps

*The Oregonian*. OPB. *Willamette Week*. The Coos Bay *World*. The Eugene *Register-Guard*. The Coast newspapers. The document trove is open. The story has not been written.

The State's October 24, 2025 Answer is a public filing in Coos County Circuit Court. The operative language is *"does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."* A state, on a public docket, claiming it owes no fiduciary duty to the children it has been holding land in trust for since 1859. That is a story. It is also a story your archives can be searched against — the years of Land Board coverage your paper has run, set next to the Answer your state's lawyers have just filed. A reporter who walks that comparison will have a feature inside a week.

The OSU page 28 admission of a $2.1 million annual deficit projected for fifty years, in OSU's own December 2021 document, is a story. The MOU between the Department of State Lands and OSU, executed as the architecture required, then implemented across a sequence of meetings that did not meet the requirements of the public-meetings law — that is a story. The 2019 bond substitution, which John Charles described as the taxpayers buying a state forest they already owned, is a story your business desk should have on the calendar.

Margaret Bird's December 11, 2022 letter to the State Land Board is a prelitigation warning every reporter on the education beat should have seen at the time. *"Decoupling,"* she wrote, *"is a euphemism for selling."* Three and a half years later, the question for every Oregon paper of record is why no one on the beat surfaced that letter in 2022 or 2023, and what the coverage of the Elliott would have looked like if they had.

I am not asking the press corps to take a side. I am asking it to read the documents that are now public, ask the three named Land Board members the questions that are now obvious, print what the documents say, and let the reader draw the conclusion.

## To the trust-and-environmental-law bar

The January 28, 2026 Court of Appeals opinion in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* is the doctrinal anchor for the next case. The standing question is settled. The merits await.

The 1977 Attorney General Opinion 7450 is the in-state doctrinal floor — the unrescinded Oregon authority that holds off-purpose use of the Elliott unlawful where it adversely affects the fund. The 1992 and 2003 Oregon AG opinions are the in-state doctrinal anchors affirming the trust character. The 1910 New Mexico–Arizona Enabling Act trust framework is the comparative-strength template — the federal architecture other Western states have used to discipline trustee conduct where Oregon's enabling language reads weaker on the page but the trust character is no less real.

There is a generation of trust-and-environmental law practice waiting for the practitioners who take this case forward. *Advocates* is in trial-court discovery in Coos County. The companion 2023 case is at the Oregon Supreme Court on the petition for review. The appellate posture has been opened. The documentary record is rich. The beneficiary class is sympathetic. The defendant is the State of Oregon — which has now committed itself, on the record, to the position that it owes the beneficiaries no duty. A bar that wishes to test the limits of state fiduciary liability has been handed a teaching case.

If you are a young lawyer reading this, the case file is reproducible. Your state has trust lands too. The pattern is portable.

## To the national state-trust-lands community

This audience knows Margaret. Many of you will hear her at the NASTL meeting in 2026. The reason to read this volume is not to learn what Oregon is — you know — but to have the documentary record in hand when your own state begins the moves Oregon has made.

The Elliott case is the cleanest documentary record currently in flight on the question Margaret has been working on for forty years: how the trust architecture either holds or fails when an institution begins to harvest itself. Oregon is the worked example. Oregon is the case file. The pattern repeats. The bond-substitution move, the research-forest framing, the public-meeting-law theater, the *adverse-financial-effect* test bypassed without amendment — your jurisdiction will see versions of each of these. The volumes that follow this one will name Utah, Nebraska, Colorado, and beyond. The series is the field's case file, assembled state by state from the discovery records, trustee meetings, and AG opinions that show where the architecture held and where it gave way.

Bring this book to your trustee. Bring it to your AG's office. Bring it to your state's school-finance committee. The architecture you have spent your career inside is being tested in Oregon now. The volume in your hand is the test result.

## To the watchful crew

That phrase belongs to Margaret. The architecture works where someone is willing to defend it across the decades it has to run, and the willingness has to be cultivated in each generation because the architecture cannot defend itself. The Elliott case is the live test of whether Oregon's schoolchildren still have such a crew.

The membership is not formal. There is no roster. There is a recognition, in each chair, that the trust has been put on us and that we are the generation that either keeps it or loses it on our watch. The people in 1859 who wrote the constitutional architecture cannot defend it from where they are. The schoolchildren who are the beneficiaries cannot defend it from where they are. Margaret Bird, Daniel Crowe, Natalie Scott, the OASTL board, the five named plaintiffs, the Coos County reporter who has been covering the case, the school-board member in eastern Oregon who has begun asking questions — these are the watchful crew currently on station. There are not enough of them.

If you read this book and recognized yourself, the move is to join. Sign the Library mailing list. Send Margaret's NASTL chapter to one colleague. Pick up a state — yours, or one whose trust you have known about — and start writing down what its documentary record actually says. The book in your hand was assembled from one person reading discovery, one document at a time, with help. You can do that too. The architecture needs you to.

---

The 1859 architecture said the Elliott belongs to Oregon's schoolchildren forever. The State of Oregon does not currently believe that. The work in front of us is to make Oregon believe it again, on the documentary record this book has put on the page.

Who steals from children? On the evidence we now hold, the State of Oregon. The next move belongs to the people who, having read this far, are going to refuse to let that stand.

— Dave Sullivan, Coos County, Oregon, 2026.
