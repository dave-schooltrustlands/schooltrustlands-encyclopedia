# Chapter 4 — The Secret Meeting About Going Public

> *The next Science Advisory Panel meeting will discuss the process that the panel will use to receive and share comments, questions, and information from the public.*
>
> — Anthony S. Davis, Interim Dean, OSU College of Forestry, in an email to Dave and Barb Sullivan, May 27, 2020, 5:46 PM

On the evening of May 27, 2020, I sat at the kitchen table in Kings Valley with a cup of coffee in my hand and read an email from a man named Anthony Davis.

I had been expecting it. Or I had been expecting something like it. I had been writing to the Department of State Lands and the OSU College of Forestry for months by then, asking simple questions about the Elliott — what the Science Advisory Panel was doing, when it was meeting, who sat on it, what the panel had been told to deliberate about. The answers, when answers came at all, were the kind of answers an agency gives when it wants you to feel that you have been answered without actually having to say anything. I had stopped expecting much.

But Davis had answered. He was the Interim Dean of the OSU College of Forestry, the same college that was building, on behalf of the State, a proposal to take the Elliott State Forest out of the Common School Fund and convert it into something the College would run. He had, on his own initiative, written to my wife Barb and me to let us know that the Science Advisory Panel — the body of scientists OSU had assembled to give the conversion proposal its credentials — was going to meet privately two days later. He told us the date. He told us the time. He told us, in one carefully bureaucratic sentence, what the meeting was about.

I read the sentence. Then I read it again.

The Science Advisory Panel, he wrote, would meet on May 29, 2020, to discuss *"the process that the panel will use to receive and share comments, questions, and information from the public."*

I set the cup down.

I am a systems scientist by training. I have spent forty years thinking about how organizations process information, and I have written textbooks about how people think when they sit down at a computer or a meeting table. There is a particular kind of bureaucratic sentence that, when you read it slowly enough, tells you more than its author meant to say. The dean's sentence was one of those. The interim dean of a state university's college of forestry had just told me, in writing, that a panel chartered to advise a state agency on the disposition of a billion-dollar public trust asset was going to meet *in private* to decide how it would later be *public*.

I sat at the kitchen table for a long time after that. Barb came in and asked what was wrong. I told her. She read the email over my shoulder and said, "He doesn't even know what he just said, does he."

He did not.

## What Davis didn't realize he was saying

Let me back up, because the meaning of the Davis email depends on what was already in the room before the email arrived.

By May 2020, the conversion of the Elliott had been in motion for three years. The 2017 sale to Lone Rock Resources had collapsed under public protest — the chapter that follows this one tells that story — and the Department of State Lands had pivoted, with the OSU College of Forestry's help, to a different mechanism for accomplishing the same conversion. Instead of selling the Elliott to a private timber company, the State would *transfer* it to OSU as a "research forest." The forest would leave the Common School Fund. The proceeds would be replaced, eventually, by a state-issued bond. The schoolchildren who were the Fund's named beneficiaries would lose a working timber asset and acquire, in its place, a line item on the state budget.

The vehicle for designing this conversion was the Science Advisory Panel, a body that DSL and OSU had stood up under a Memorandum of Understanding. The Panel met. It deliberated. It drafted recommendations. It produced, by the end of 2020, the document that would become the Elliott State Research Forest Proposal — the proposal whose page 28 we will see in Chapter 7, the one that projects a $2.1 million annual deficit for fifty years.

The Panel did all of this in private.

That was the part the public was not supposed to focus on. The Panel was framed, in DSL's external communications, as a group of distinguished scientists giving the State the benefit of their expertise. The framing was true enough — the panel members were in fact distinguished, and they were in fact scientists. The framing left out one thing. Under Oregon law, the Panel was a *governing body* of a *public body*. Its meetings were required to be open. Its records were required to be public. The MOU that created the Panel did not exempt it from those requirements. Nothing exempted it. The Panel was operating, by May 2020, in plain violation of Oregon's Public Meetings Law.

Anthony Davis did not realize this. I am almost certain he did not. The Davis email is not the email of a man covering up an illegal meeting. It is the email of a man who has internalized the operating practice of his institution so completely that he can describe an illegal meeting to a member of the public, in writing, and not hear what he is saying. The Panel met in private because the Panel always met in private. The next meeting would be a meeting about how to handle the public. The fact that *meeting in private to plan public engagement* was itself the thing the law was written to prevent — that fact had not occurred to him.

That was the part that put the cup down on the table.

## The pattern, named

I had been watching the pattern for a year before the Davis email arrived. The Panel's private meetings. The unanswered records requests. The redactions on the Advisory Committee roster. The Cougar Pass refusal — that one I'll come back to in a moment. The selective release of information to favored parties and the stonewalling of citizens who asked the questions the State did not want asked. The "public engagement opportunities" that DSL had been advertising and that I had been attending, where the format was always the same: a panel of experts at the front of a room explaining what had already been decided, followed by a brief and ceremonial period during which members of the public were allowed five minutes apiece to speak in front of panelists whose pens stayed on the table.

I had been reaching for a name for the pattern. After the Davis email I had one.

That evening I sat down and wrote what would become the opening section of a website called ElliottSecrets.net. I put on the home page, in the second paragraph, the sentence that named what I had been watching:

> *Each Department of State Lands "public engagement opportunity" follows a theatrical script: First, a series of experts explains what has already been decided and done, then each member of the public is allowed five minutes of polite attention in front of panelists who pretend to listen. If an interested outsider wants to be really involved or find out how decisions are really being made, no one has any time to spare.*

I called it *public engagement theater*.

The phrase has stuck with me for six years now. I have used it in op-eds. I have used it in trustee letters. Margaret Bird borrowed it once in a private letter and made it sharper than I had. The OASTL litigation team uses it as shorthand. It has become the operating description of how the State of Oregon does its business when the business is taking a children's trust asset and converting it into something else.

The Davis email was the moment the phrase became necessary. The dean had handed me, without realizing it, the cleanest specimen of the pattern I would ever see. A private meeting to plan public communication. The deliberation in the back room; the appearance of deliberation in the front room. The substance hidden; the procedural form on display. *Public engagement theater* was not a metaphor I had reached for to score a rhetorical point. It was the literal description of what the dean had described.

## The Memorandum of Understanding

To understand why the Davis email mattered legally — and not only morally — you have to understand one document that the public never saw a public hearing on and that the agencies negotiated quietly between themselves in the period after the 2017 sale collapsed. The document is the DSL/OSU Memorandum of Understanding.

I am going to keep this short, because the chapter is not a treatise. The MOU was a contract. On one side, the Department of State Lands, the state agency that holds the Elliott in trust for the Common School Fund. On the other side, the OSU College of Forestry, a unit of the state university system whose dean stood to gain operational control of forty-three thousand acres of public-trust forest if the conversion went through. The MOU set out what each side would do, what each side would pay, and who would sit on the working bodies that would design the conversion.

The Science Advisory Panel was one of those bodies. The MOU constituted it. The MOU funded it. The MOU set the terms under which it operated.

I wrote at the time, on the ElliottSecrets Meetings page, that the MOU *"effectively deputizes OSU as an official part of the State Lands Board public planning process."* That deputization had a consequence the agencies seem not to have considered. When a state agency deputizes a university as part of its public-planning process, the university comes inside the Public Meetings Law along with the agency. The Panel was created by a public body, was paid by a public body, was deliberating on behalf of a public body, and was producing recommendations that would direct a public body's decisions. Under Oregon law that makes the Panel itself a public body. Under Oregon law the Panel's meetings were required to be open.

They were not open.

The Davis email is the State's own dean confirming, in writing, that they were not open. The dean did not call them closed. He did not say *secret*. He told me, plainly, that the next meeting was scheduled and that the subject of the meeting was the process the Panel would use to handle the public. He told me, plainly, that the Panel was meeting without me, and without any member of the public, and that it was meeting on Friday.

I am the public. Barb is the public. The Common School Fund's beneficiaries — the Oregon schoolchildren whose forest the Panel was being paid to recommend the conversion of — are the public. None of us would be in the room on Friday. None of us would see the minutes. None of us would receive the materials the Panel was working from. The dean had just confirmed that.

He had also, in confirming it, handed me documentary evidence of a violation. I saved the email. I saved a copy of it on the ElliottSecrets website. I have it still.

#### Oregon Public Meetings Law

Oregon's Public Meetings Law lives in the Oregon Revised Statutes at chapter 192, sections 610 through 690. The law has been on the books since 1973. It opens, in section 192.620, with a sentence that the legislature wrote as a statutory finding — not a preamble, not a hope, but an official recital of why the law exists. The sentence reads:

> *The Oregon form of government requires an informed public aware of the deliberations and decisions of governing bodies and the information upon which such decisions were made.*

The law defines a *meeting* as the convening of a *governing body* for which a quorum is required to make a decision or to deliberate toward one. The law defines *governing body* broadly enough to include advisory groups stood up by a public body and operating under that body's authority. The law presumes that meetings of public bodies are open to the public, and it places the burden on the agency, not on the citizen, to justify any closure.

What does this mean for the Science Advisory Panel? The Panel was constituted by a Memorandum of Understanding between two state agencies — the Department of State Lands and the Oregon State University College of Forestry — for the purpose of advising the State Land Board on the disposition of a public trust asset. The Panel deliberated toward a decision the State Land Board would later take. The Panel was funded with public money. Every test the statute applies for whether a body is a "governing body" of a "public body" is met. The Panel was required to meet in public. Its records were required to be available. Its deliberations were required to be reviewable.

The MOU did not give the agencies the power to exempt the Panel from the statute. No memorandum between two state agencies can override a law passed by the Oregon Legislature. The Panel met in private not because the law permitted it, but because the agencies chose to.

</aside>

## The other person who was watching

I was not the only person noticing what was happening. I want to put one paragraph on the record before this chapter closes, because the records-request stonewall that DSL was running in 2019 and 2020 was not aimed only at me, and was not invented for me. Bob Zybach — who holds a PhD from OSU and who has spent thirty years documenting the Elliott — sent two formal records requests to DSL during the same period. The first went to Meliah Masiba at the agency on December 10, 2019, asking for planning and Habitat Conservation Plan budget figures so that Bob could write accurately on a December 16 deadline. DSL acknowledged the request and did not answer it. Bob sent a second request on April 1, 2020, asking for two years of DSL/OSU/Elliott budget detail and noting that for three years he had been periodically requesting historical maps and survey documents from the Phillips era and being refused. DSL acknowledged the second request and did not answer it either. I tell that story in fuller detail in Chapter 2; here I want only to note that the pattern Davis confirmed in his email was already the operating practice the agency had been running against citizens who asked questions. Davis did not invent the secrecy. He inherited it. He confirmed it without realizing he had.

## Why the dean wrote

I want to give Anthony Davis his due. He did not have to write to Barb and me. He could have continued to do what his predecessors had done, which was to let the meetings happen and let the public find out about them, if at all, after the fact. He wrote. He used his own name. He used his own email. He told us when the meeting was. The fact that he told us in a sentence that was, on its face, a confession does not change the fact that he told us. I have wondered, in the years since, whether he wrote because he was beginning to see that something was wrong; or whether he wrote because he genuinely thought the sentence was unremarkable; or whether he wrote because some part of him wanted a piece of the truth in a place where I could see it. I do not know. I do not need to know. The email exists. It is what it is.

What it is, for the project this book is about, is the documentary anchor for everything that came after. The 2024 OASTL lawsuit — the case I am the lead plaintiff in — depends, at its evidentiary core, on the proposition that the State did not deliberate in front of its beneficiaries about the disposition of their trust asset. That proposition is, of course, supported by years of records-request stonewalls, redacted rosters, refused refusals on small public-good proposals like Cougar Pass, and the State's October 2025 Answer that the Plaintiffs are not owed the duties they say they are owed. But the cleanest single piece of evidence, the one that can be reproduced in two paragraphs in a brief and that requires no expert testimony to interpret, is the Davis email. The interim dean of the OSU College of Forestry, on the night of May 27, 2020, sat down and wrote to two members of the public that the panel he chaired would meet privately to plan its public communications. He sent the email. He copied his director of communications. He pressed send.

It is in the record now. It will be in the record when the trial is held. It will, if I have anything to say about it, be quoted in the State's own court filings — by the plaintiffs, on the way to the State explaining why the plaintiffs are wrong that a fiduciary process requires the trustee to deliberate in the open.

## Closing scene

The forest itself, that May, was doing what the forest does. The Coast Range was green. The roads up to Cougar Pass were rutted and twisting and would have been impassable to anyone without a high-clearance four-wheel drive. The fire-lookout tower at the top — the one Bob and I had proposed to restore at no cost to the State, and that DSL had refused on the ground that the agency was busy preparing the conversion — was rotting under its own roof. The Common School Fund's working forest, at forty-three thousand acres, sat where it had sat since 1859. The schoolchildren who owned it, all six hundred thousand of them, did not know that on Friday a panel was going to convene by phone in Corvallis to decide how they would later be told what had been decided for them.

Three years earlier, in February 2017, the State Land Board had voted in public to sell that forest to a private timber company. The public had shown up. The public had said no. The Board had reversed itself in May 2017 and the political memory of Oregon recorded the reversal as a victory.

I sat at the kitchen table on May 27, 2020, and I understood for the first time that the reversal had not been a victory. The reversal had moved the work. The work was still happening. It was happening on Friday. It was happening in a room I would not be in. The dean had just told me so.

That is the chapter Chapter 5 takes up: the 2017 sale, the reversal that looked like a win, and the work that continued.
