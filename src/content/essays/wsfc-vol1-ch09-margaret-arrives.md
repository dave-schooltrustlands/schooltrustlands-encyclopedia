# Chapter 9 — Margaret Arrives

> *"Decoupling is a euphemism for selling."*
>
> — Margaret Bird, letter to the Oregon State Land Board, December 11, 2022

By the autumn of 2022 I had been at this for a quarter of a century, in one form or another. I had bought the tree farm in 1996. I had joined OSWA. I had walked Cougar Pass with Bob and Barb. I had filed the FOIA that came back saying no harvesting was occurring on a forest that had been managed for the Common School Fund since 1930. I had read the Anthony Davis email about the secret meeting and the science panel that would meet in private to decide how to talk to the public. I had stood in the parking lot at the State Capitol on the day in February 2017 that the Land Board voted to sell the Elliott to Lone Rock, and I had stood in the same parking lot three months later when the Board reversed. I thought, then, the reversal was a victory. I was wrong. I just did not yet have anyone who could tell me, plainly, what I was looking at.

What I had was a long file cabinet and a longer instinct. What I did not have was a witness.

The witness, when she arrived, came by way of a phone call from an ASTL friend who told me there was someone in Salt Lake City I needed to talk to. He said her name three or four times before I wrote it down — Margaret Bird, Margaret Bird, Margaret Bird — the way you do when you know a name is going to matter. He said she had spent more than two decades inside Utah's school-trust system. He said she had helped take Utah from a place very much like Oregon was now to a place where the schools received more than a hundred million dollars a year from the trust they were the beneficiaries of. He said she would talk to me. He gave me her number.

I called her. I do not remember the date precisely. I remember the call. I remember that within about ten minutes I understood I had been talking to the wrong people for twenty years.

Margaret listens the way good auditors listen — patiently, without interrupting, with the question already forming about three sentences ahead of where you are. She does not flatter. She does not rush. When she finally speaks she lands one sentence and lets it sit, and then she lands another. *"You are describing,"* she said, *"a trustee that has decided the trust is in the way."* I had not put it to myself in those words. I had been calling it many things — mismanagement, conflict of interest, capture, bad faith — and each of those words was correct as far as it went. *In the way* was the one I had been missing. *In the way* explained the harvest line going to zero. *In the way* explained the science panel meeting in private. *In the way* explained the appraisal that priced a billion-dollar working forest at ninety-nine point six million dollars, and the State writing itself an IOU for the difference between what the trust was worth and what the trustee felt like paying.

Margaret told me about Utah. I want to give the reader the version Margaret gave me. Not the academic version. The version a person tells over the phone when she has been carrying the story for thirty years.

#### The Utah Recovery

Utah came into the union in 1896 with a school-trust grant much like Oregon's — a piece of land in every township, dedicated to the support of the public schools, held by the state for the children's benefit. For the better part of the next century, Utah did with its grant roughly what most public-land states did. The lands were managed for the convenience of the agencies that managed them, the schools received whatever was left over, and the schools and their constituency were not in the room when decisions about the land were made. By the mid-1980s, the annual distribution to Utah's schools from the trust had fallen below five million dollars. The fund existed on paper. In practice, it was a footnote.

Margaret started inside that system in the early 1980s as an economist working state fiscal analysis. She figured out, by the end of her second year, that the architecture was wrong — not that the people inside it were wrong, but that the institution was structured so that the trust could not perform the duty it was created to perform. There was no separate trustee. There was no separate beneficiary representation. The schools, the actual beneficiaries, had no seat at the table. The lands were inside a state agency that answered to a governor who answered to politics, and the schools answered to whatever the politics produced.

What she did over the next decade and a half was not glamorous. She built a coalition. She named it the Utah Reform Coalition. She brought in the Utah PTA — which never stopped showing up. She brought in the state school boards association, the local school boards, the state superintendent, the Utah Education Association. She wrote the legislation. She testified, year after year, until what had been impossible became inevitable. She got the trust lands moved into a separately-governed agency — the School and Institutional Trust Lands Administration — with its own board, its own director, and a fiduciary duty that ran directly to the beneficiaries, not through the political layer above. She got a permanent fund built. She got a distribution mechanism that paid the income to the schools every year, by formula, with the corpus preserved.

The numbers tell the rest. From under five million dollars in annual distribution in the mid-1980s, Utah's school-trust distribution crossed one hundred million dollars by the early 2020s and stands today above one hundred and thirteen million dollars a year. The corpus did not shrink to produce that income. The corpus grew. Utah's children, today, get more from the trust than the trust ever produced in any year of its first century, and they get it because of an institutional architecture that puts the beneficiary's interest where it has to be — at the center of the trustee's job.

Oregon, by contrast, never adopted any of that discipline. Oregon's school-trust lands remained inside the agency that managed them for convenience. The Land Board — the Governor, the Secretary of State, and the Treasurer — sat as ex-officio trustees, on top of the political layer, not separated from it. There was no separately-governed beneficiary representation. There was no formula that protected the income against political claims on it. And so when Oregon's trustees decided the trust was *in the way* — when the harvest line started going to zero, and the bonds started getting written, and the *decoupling* memos started circulating — there was no architectural friction to slow them down. The discipline Margaret spent thirty years building in Utah was the discipline Oregon's children never got.

By the time Margaret finished the Utah story I had been quiet on the phone for longer than was probably polite. What she had given me was not just a fact pattern. It was a yardstick. For the first time I had a measurement for what Oregon had failed to do. I had been describing the failure in Oregon's own vocabulary — the State Land Board, the Common School Fund, the Department of State Lands, the ESRF. Margaret put those terms inside a frame where they could be compared with their alternatives, and the comparison was devastating. Utah had not done anything miraculous. Utah had done its job. Oregon had not done its job. Oregon had done, instead, the long, slow opposite of its job, and called the opposite *modernization*.

In the months that followed I sent Margaret everything I had. The DSL refusal letters. The Anthony Davis email. The Joint Ways and Means subcommittee testimony. The Save the Children's Forest organizing emails. The Jen Hamaker letter. The David Gould Buster Club note. The OSU page-28 paragraph projecting a $2.1 million annual deficit for fifty years. Margaret read all of it. She wrote back, often, with the question that I had not thought to ask. She did not tell me what to think. She told me what to look at next.

On December 13, 2022, the Oregon State Land Board was scheduled to vote on the next stage of the so-called *investment value* sale — the maneuver by which the State, as trustee, would buy the Elliott from itself at a price the State itself had picked. Margaret wrote a letter. She dated it two days before the vote. December 11, 2022. She sent it to the trustees and copied the parties she thought needed to read it. I have the letter in front of me as I write this. It is short. It is in Margaret's voice — declarative, plain, unhedged.

She told the Board what the word *decoupling* meant. She wrote: *decoupling is a euphemism for selling.* A trustee that takes the trust's primary working asset, transfers it to an outside arrangement at a price the trustee itself has chosen, and substitutes a stream of general-fund bonds for the asset's earnings, has *sold the asset.* The bookkeeping is more elaborate than a normal sale. The doctrine is the same. The receivable substituted for the asset is an obligation of the state's general fund, not of the trust corpus. *"That,"* Margaret wrote, *"is the difference between owning a piece of property and owning a promise from a politician."*

She then offered an analogy I have repeated more times than I can count. She wrote that the State, in this transaction, was behaving like a man who takes his neighbor's Ford pickup truck, hands the neighbor an IOU for the truck's blue-book value, drives the truck home, and tells the neighbor that he should be grateful because now he owns a *promise* instead of a vehicle. The neighbor still cannot drive to work. The neighbor cannot put gas in a promise. If the man who wrote the IOU later loses his job, the IOU becomes paper. The truck, meanwhile, sits in the new owner's driveway, still useful, still working, still earning its keep — for the new owner. The neighbor has been *compensated.* The neighbor has also been *robbed.* Both things are true.

What I had been trying to say for years about the Common School Fund — that the bonds were not equivalent to the forest; that an obligation of the state's general fund is not the same fiduciary instrument as a working trust asset; that a politician's promise to pay debt service is not a promise the next legislature is bound to keep — Margaret said in three sentences and a pickup truck. The Board voted anyway. Two days later, on December 13, 2022, the trustees moved the so-called *investment value* sale forward by the margin Land Boards always seem to vote by when they have already decided in private what they will do in public.

But the letter was in the record. It was on the Department of State Lands' own published meeting materials. It was in the inbox of every trustee. It was in the inbox of every reporter who had ever covered the Elliott. And the line — *decoupling is a euphemism for selling* — was now available to anyone who would later have to describe, in front of a court or a legislative committee or a public hearing, what had been done. The word stopped doing the bureaucratic work it had been doing for a decade. After Margaret's letter, you could not say *decoupling* in Oregon without the listener knowing what the word actually meant.

I want to be careful about how I describe what changed for me. I did not gain a co-author that fall. I gained something I had not had in twenty years of working alone, or with allies who were as out-of-state-experience-poor as I was: I gained a witness who could not be dismissed. Margaret had spent more time inside the architecture of school trust lands than anyone alive. She had read the admission acts. She had sat in trustee meetings in nearly every public-land state. She was, by training and by long habit, slow to call something a theft — and she had used the word, in writing, about Oregon. The trustees could not say she did not know what she was looking at. The press could not say she was a partisan. The trust-lands community across the country, whose silence had been one of Oregon's many quiet defenses, was now on notice. If Margaret Bird had written *decoupling is a euphemism for selling* on Oregon Land Board letterhead day, then Oregon was no longer an Oregon story. It was a national story. It just had not yet been picked up.

She and I have worked together every week since, in one form or another, for the four years that followed. The case I am the lead plaintiff in today, *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, is the case it is because Margaret Bird arrived in 2022 and showed me what I had been looking at. The chapters that follow this one — the lawsuit Natalie Scott took to the Court of Appeals; the standing victory the Court handed down on January 28, 2026; the Answer the State filed on October 24, 2025, in which the State of Oregon told a court of law that it *"does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged"* — none of those would read the way they read, and none would have been brought, if Margaret had not, on a phone call in the fall of 2022, said the sentence about the trustee deciding the trust was in the way.

What I learned that fall is that a long fight is not won by the person who has been in it the longest. It is won by the moment a witness who knows the larger pattern shows up and tells you, plainly, what you are inside of. Margaret was that witness for me. She is, I think, the witness this book is, in part, for — a way of putting on the public record what Margaret has been telling the few of us who would listen, for the better part of forty years, about what happens to a school trust when no one is watching.

The next chapter is about the first lawsuit. I filed it in 2021, before Margaret arrived. I want to tell the reader what I thought I was doing then, and what I understand now, four years on, I was actually doing. The two things are not the same. They were never going to be. The first lawsuit was the one I knew how to bring. The second one — the one we are inside of as I write this — is the one I learned to bring because Margaret arrived and told me what I had been looking at.
