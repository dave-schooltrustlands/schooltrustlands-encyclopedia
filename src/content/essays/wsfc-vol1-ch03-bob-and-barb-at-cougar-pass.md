# Chapter 3 — Bob and Barb at Cougar Pass

> *Because of the potential transfer of the Elliott to Oregon State University, the Department of State Lands is unable to dedicate time and resources to a potential project at this point in time.*
>
> — Department of State Lands, written response to the Cougar Pass Lookout Education Center proposal, early 2020

The way I tell people I got into this is that I went looking for a hobby and found a lawsuit. The hobby was a fire lookout.

I had retired from the OSU College of Business by then. The tree farm in Kings Valley had a knoll on it that, on a clear day, gave a view east to the Cascades and west across the Coast Range. I had read enough about the old wooden lookouts the Forest Service built across the West in the 1930s to know that what I really wanted, on top of that knoll, was a fire lookout tower of my own — staffed by me, in my retirement, with a thermos and a pair of binoculars. The county building inspector, when I asked, said no one had ever requested a permit for one. He concluded eventually that I could build it, but only if I met the structural codes designed for tall commercial buildings. Forest Service lookout drawings did not meet those codes. There was, as far as I could tell, no published modern blueprint that did. I started a small effort to assemble open-source plans, and to do that I joined the Forest Fire Lookout Association.

That is where Bob Zybach called me.

I had known Bob twenty years earlier, when I was teaching at the business school and he was finishing his doctorate. Bob is a fire historian. His 2003 PhD dissertation at OSU — *The Great Fires* — documents Indian burning patterns and catastrophic fires on the Oregon Coast Range going back five centuries. He cofounded the Oregon Websites and Watersheds Project, ORWW.org, which has digitized a small library of Coast Range survey notes, aerial photographs, and oral histories. He is, by my reckoning and by the reckoning of every working forester I know who has spent time with him, the world's foremost scholar on the Elliott State Forest and its history.

When Bob called he was direct. *Dave,* he said. *You're interested in fire lookout towers. There's one on the Elliott called Cougar Pass. It's in rough shape, but I think we could fix it up.*

I said yes inside of a sentence.

---

Cougar Pass Lookout, when Bob and I first looked into it, turned out to be the last one standing on the Elliott. There had been four originally, built in the 1930s when the Coast Range was thick with logging slash and the State was learning, fire by fire, how to keep it from going up. Three of the four had been torn down or had collapsed. Cougar Pass alone remained — wooden cab, four legs, set on a knob near the head of the West Fork Millicoma drainage, with a view that on a clear day reached most of the western half of the forest. It had not been staffed in decades. The roof leaked. The stairs were rotten. Birds and small mammals had moved in. Whatever historical-preservation files the Department of State Lands kept on it had gone quiet years before.

Bob's idea was simple, and mine was the same. The lookout was a piece of the Elliott's working history. We would form a small nonprofit, raise the money privately, restore the structure to a safe and visitable condition, staff it on a volunteer basis through the Forest Fire Lookout Association, and operate it as a Cougar Pass Lookout Education Center — a place where school groups, hikers, and the general public could walk up the stairs and stand inside the cab and see the forest the way the lookouts had seen it. The cost to the State would be zero. We would build the site ourselves, design the signage ourselves, and recruit the volunteers ourselves. We registered cougarpass.org, drafted a proposal, lined up early funding, talked with the State Historic Preservation Office about period-correct restoration methods, and brought the package to the Department of State Lands.

The response, when it came, was an email from a DSL forester named Ryan Singleton. It was three short sentences long. I have read it many times. It is one of the documents I think about when people ask me when I first knew something was wrong on the Elliott.

> *Because of the potential transfer of the Elliott to Oregon State University, the Department of State Lands is unable to dedicate time and resources to a potential project at this point in time.*

Read it slowly. The State was not declining because the proposal was unsound — it had not engaged with the proposal at all. The State was not declining because the cost was wrong — the cost to the State was nothing. The State was declining because it was busy preparing to give the Elliott away, and a small piece of visible, public-facing infrastructure on the forest, attached to a small nonprofit operated by ordinary Oregonians, would complicate the giveaway. A lookout that the public visited would be a piece of public proprietorship on the books at exactly the wrong moment. So the lookout would be left to rot.

I read the letter twice the day it arrived. I read it a third time the next morning. I called Bob and read it to him. Then I called Howard Verschoor.

---

Howard Verschoor was the Oregon chapter director of the Forest Fire Lookout Association. He had been working on these towers for years. I told him about Cougar Pass, and the proposal, and the DSL letter. There was a long pause on his end. Then he laughed, the way you laugh when something is not funny.

*Dave,* he said. *I tried to do that twenty years ago.*

From 1999 to 2003, Howard told me, he and the FFLA had made four formal attempts to restore Cougar Pass. Each time they had brought money to the table; each time they had offered volunteer labor; each time they had been put off. *They didn't say no,* Howard said. *They just kept not saying yes. Eventually we gave up.* The same agency, the same forest, the same lookout. Eleven years apart, the same answer.

That was the thing I sat with for a week. It was not a single bureaucrat's bad day. It was not a misunderstanding between offices. The Department of State Lands had a position on Cougar Pass that had been stable across two decades and across at least two staff turnovers, and the position was that the lookout would be allowed to fall down rather than be helped to stand up by anyone outside the agency.

My wife Barb said the thing out loud at our kitchen table the next morning. *Dave,* she said. *They want it to rot.*

---

Two weeks later, on a Saturday in February 2020, Bob and Barb and I drove out to see what the lookout had become.

The road in to Cougar Pass had been a logging road once, and then a maintained Elliott access road, and then a road that was allowed to lapse. Past a certain gate the gravel ran out and the alders and salmonberry took over. We had a four-wheel-drive truck and we still spent the last mile and a half crawling — first gear, low range, easing over washouts and root-balls and the occasional rotted-out culvert. Barb counted the alders the truck pushed flat. She got to eleven. Bob, who has spent more of his working life on Coast Range roads than most people have spent in cars, was the calmest of the three of us. *They stopped grading this two summers ago,* he said. *Maybe three.* The Elliott was eighty thousand acres of some of the most productive timberland in the world, and the State was paying one Forester out of an office in Bend, four and a half hours away, to manage it. We were looking at what one Forester out of an office four and a half hours away could keep up with.

The lookout, when we reached it, was worse than the photographs had suggested. The bottom flight of the staircase had collapsed entirely; the second flight was holding by the grace of a few ten-penny nails and the dry summer air. The cab roof had a hole in it the size of a serving platter. Inside the cab, the fire-finder pedestal was gone — pried up, by the look of it, years before. The windows were broken on three sides. There was bird droppings on every horizontal surface and a small skeleton of something — a packrat, maybe — in the corner. The whole structure smelled of wet wood and mouse.

Bob walked the perimeter slowly, the way he walks all old structures, taking it in. *It would still come back,* he said finally. *We could still do this.* He meant it. The frame was sound. The footings, where you could see them through the brush, were good. With four months and a crew of volunteers and the historic-preservation guidance we had already lined up, Cougar Pass Lookout could be standing and staffed and open to the public by the next summer. Everything we needed was already in our hands. The only thing in the way was a state agency that did not want us to do it.

I stood on what was left of the platform and looked west. You could see, from up there, the working part of the forest — the alders and the second-growth Douglas-fir and the patches that had been cut and replanted in the 1960s. You could see what eighty thousand acres of school trust looks like when nobody is looking. Barb stood beside me and did not say anything for a long time. Then she said, *Dave, this is not a place that is being managed. This is a place that is being abandoned.*

That was the sentence the diagnosis came out of.

I do not want to make it sound like I knew, that day, what I know now. I did not. I knew we had been told no for reasons that did not match the proposal. I knew Howard Verschoor had been told no for reasons that did not match his proposal either. I knew the road to the lookout was rotting and the lookout itself was rotting and the agency that was supposed to be standing watch over both was, by its own written admission, busy with something else. The thing I did not yet know was how large the *something else* was.

But on the drive home that afternoon, with Bob asleep in the back seat and the truck creaking over the same washouts on the way out, the question that became the question put itself together in my head. The State had been letting the Cougar Pass lookout rot for at least twenty years. The State had been actively rejecting outside efforts to save it for at least twenty years. The State's only written reason — the only reason it had ever put on paper — was that it was preparing to transfer the Elliott to Oregon State University. If the State was managing the lookout this way, on the documentary record, then: *Is the entire Elliott being managed in essentially the same manner?*

That is the sentence I keep coming back to. It is the load-bearing sentence of this whole book. I did not have the documents yet to answer it. I had the question. Everything that comes after this chapter — the public-records requests, the secret meetings, the OSU plan, the bond substitution, the lawsuits, the trial — is the work of answering it.

The Cougar Pass refusal letter became, for me, the smallest piece of paper in the case and one of the most important. It is three sentences long. It does not contain a dollar figure. It does not mention the Common School Fund. It does not say the words *fiduciary duty.* It does say, in its own dry agency voice, that the trustee of the Elliott has other priorities than the upkeep of the Elliott. A trustee with other priorities than the upkeep of the trust is not, by the meaning of the word, a trustee at all.

<aside class="readers-edition-aside">

#### What is a Trust of the Highest Nature?

A trust, in plain English, is what happens when one person — the **settlor** — hands property to a second person — the **trustee** — to be held and managed for the benefit of a third — the **beneficiary**. The trustee does not own the property. The trustee holds it. The trustee's whole job is to manage it the way the beneficiary would manage it if the beneficiary could do it herself.

Some trusts are casual; some are sacred. A grandfather who tells his daughter to *hold this for the kids until they're eighteen* has set up a trust. So has the State of Oregon, in 1859, when it accepted from the federal government five hundred thousand acres of land and a constitutional duty to hold those lands for the common-school fund. The first kind of trust is a family courtesy. The second kind is what the Oregon Supreme Court, in a 1931 case called *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden*, named *a trust of the highest nature*.

What does that phrase mean? It means three things, all of them in ordinary English. First, the trustee has a **duty of loyalty**: every decision the trustee makes about the trust property has to be made for the beneficiary's benefit, not the trustee's own. Second, the trustee has a **duty of prudence**: the trustee has to manage the property the way a careful person would manage it — keep the buildings standing, keep the timber growing, keep the cash invested, keep the records straight. Third — and this is the part the Cougar Pass refusal letter touches — the trustee has a **duty to defend the corpus**. The *corpus* is the legal word for the trust property itself. A trustee who lets the trust property rot, or who refuses outside help that would keep it from rotting, has not defended the corpus. A trustee who refuses to spend the time and attention the property requires has not defended the corpus.

The trustees of the Elliott State Forest are the Governor, the Secretary of State, and the Treasurer of Oregon, sitting together as the State Land Board. The corpus they are charged with defending is the working forest itself: the soil, the timber, the roads, the watersheds, and yes, the lookouts. The Cougar Pass refusal letter is one agency's written notice that it would not defend a piece of that corpus because it had other plans for the rest of it. Reasonable people can disagree about what *defending the corpus* looks like on any given day. Reasonable people cannot disagree about whether *letting it rot* counts.

</aside>

Bob dropped me at the farmhouse late that afternoon. Barb made coffee. I sat at the kitchen table with the DSL letter in front of me and the photographs from the trip spreading out across the table as Barb pulled them off the camera.

*What are you going to do,* she said.

I did not have an answer yet. What I had was a question, and a feeling that the question pointed at something larger than a lookout. Cougar Pass was one lookout on one ridge. The question was about the rest of the forest, and the rest of the forest was being decided somewhere I could not see.

Three months later, on a Wednesday evening at the end of May, an email arrived at our home address from the interim dean of the OSU College of Forestry. The subject line was bland and the time stamp was 5:46 PM and the contents told me, in writing and without meaning to, where the rest of the forest was being decided. That email is where the next chapter begins.
