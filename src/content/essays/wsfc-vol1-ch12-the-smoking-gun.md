# Chapter 12 — The Smoking Gun

> *"Defendants … do not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."*
>
> — State of Oregon, Answer in *Siuslaw School District 97J et al. v. State of Oregon et al.*, Coos County Circuit Court Case No. 24CV38372, filed October 24, 2025.

I read it three times.

I want to take you to the office where I read it, because the room matters. It is the corner of our house in Kings Valley where my desk looks out at a stand of Douglas-fir my wife Barb and I planted in 1998, the second year we owned the tree farm. The light comes in over my left shoulder in the afternoons. The Friday afternoon of October 24, 2025 was unseasonably warm. Daniel Crowe had emailed the State's Answer to me as a PDF attachment, with a short cover note that I do not remember the wording of because by the time I had read the third paragraph of the pleading itself I had stopped processing the cover note.

The Answer is the document a defendant files in response to a complaint. It is the defendant's formal first statement of position. Ours had been a long time coming. We had filed the First Amended Complaint in *Siuslaw School District 97J et al. v. State of Oregon et al.* — case number 24CV38372 in the Circuit Court of the State of Oregon for the County of Coos — naming as plaintiffs three Oregon school districts and a group of named schoolchildren, against the State of Oregon, the State Land Board, and the Department of State Lands. The complaint alleged, in substance, that the State held the Common School Fund and the trust lands that fund it in a fiduciary capacity, and that the State had breached the duties any trustee owes its beneficiaries — duties of loyalty, prudence, accounting, productivity, and undivided allegiance to the beneficiary's interest. The complaint walked the court through the federal grant of 1859, the Oregon Constitution's Article VIII, the Admissions Act, the Attorney General opinions of 1992 and 2003 that named the State Land Board a trustee in the full common-law sense, the 2000 amendment that made the duty enforceable, and the line of Oregon cases beginning with *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden* in 1931 that called the relationship a trust "of the highest nature."

That was what the Plaintiffs had alleged. And the State of Oregon, in the pleading I was reading at my desk, formally answered that it *does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged.*

The first time I read the sentence, I assumed I had misunderstood the pleading's grammar. There are seven words inside that quotation, and on a first pass they read as a routine litigator's denial — the kind of paragraph that says, in effect, *we deny the plaintiffs' characterization of our obligations.* I had read a thousand denials like that in business school case studies and in twenty-five years of board work; you skim them and move on.

The second time I read it, I read it slowly. The Answer's drafters had been careful. They had not said the State owed no duties at all. They had said the State *does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged.* The qualifier was doing work. The State was not, in this pleading, conceding that it owed Oregon's schoolchildren the trustee duties our complaint had named. The State was also not, in this pleading, conceding that it was a trustee in the sense our complaint had alleged. Whatever the State considered itself to be in relation to the Common School Fund, it was reserving the right to define that relationship on its own terms, and it was telling the Coos County court that the duties our complaint had described were not the duties the State recognized.

The third time I read it, I understood what I was looking at.

The State of Oregon, asked in a court of competent jurisdiction whether it owed Oregon's schoolchildren the trustee duties our complaint had alleged, had answered no. Not *we owe some of those duties.* Not *we owe those duties and have been performing them.* Not *the plaintiffs misstate the doctrine.* The State, through its own Department of Justice, had filed a pleading that placed in front of the court the proposition that the duties Oregon's schoolchildren were asking the State to perform were duties the State did not, in its own institutional view, owe them.

I sat at my desk a long time. The light moved across the floor. I read the sentence a fourth and a fifth time. I read the surrounding paragraphs. I read the prayer-for-relief clause at the end. I closed the PDF, opened it again, and re-read the operative paragraph. The qualifier did not change. The State of Oregon was telling the court, in a pleading that would now sit on the public record of the Coos County Circuit Court forever, that the duties the Plaintiffs had alleged were not duties the State conceded it owed.

I want to be precise about what I understood and what I did not understand in that moment, because the precision matters.

What I understood was that the State of Oregon's litigation position, in the case I had been working on for the better part of a decade, was not that it had been performing its trustee duties imperfectly. The State's position was that the trustee duties our complaint had described — the duties of loyalty, of prudence, of accounting, of productivity for the beneficiary, of undivided allegiance — were not duties the State acknowledged it owed in the terms we had alleged them. A state trial lawyer reading the Answer carefully will say, with technical accuracy, that the pleading does not deny *all* trustee duties; it denies the *specific* duties the Plaintiffs alleged. That is true. It is also, on the record of the case, the State's chosen language, and it is bad enough on its own terms. If the duties Oregon's schoolchildren had asked the State to perform are duties the State will not acknowledge it owes, the work of explaining to a court what duties the State *will* acknowledge falls to the State. Margaret Bird had spent forty years writing letters to state land boards. Daniel Crowe had spent thirty years in courtrooms. Neither of them, when I called them in the hours after I read the Answer, was able to tell me what duties the State of Oregon thinks it owes to the beneficiaries of the Common School Fund.

What I did not yet understand on the afternoon of October 24 was that the Answer was not a litigation accident, a poorly-chosen phrase, or a placeholder the State would later soften. The Answer was the institutional position the State had held in 2022, when the State Land Board voted to dispose of the Elliott to itself for $99.6 million in the face of $1 billion to $1.3 billion fair-market estimates. The same position the Department of State Lands had taken when it refused Bob Zybach's December 2019 records request and his April 2020 follow-up. The same position the State had taken when, in the 2023 case, it argued that Oregon's schoolchildren had no legal standing to question the management of the trust the State held for them. The Answer of October 24, 2025 was the State's twenty-five-year institutional posture finally rendered into the language of a court pleading the public could read.

<aside class="readers-edition-aside">

#### What "Does Not Owe Plaintiffs the Duties Alleged" Actually Means

*The State of Oregon's Answer in 24CV38372 uses a phrase a careful reader should not let pass without examination.*

What the language **does not** do: it does not, on its face, deny that the State is a trustee of any kind. A state trial lawyer can stand in front of a Coos County jury and say, with technical accuracy, that the State has never denied being a trustee. The pleading does not contain that denial in those words. A reader of this book should be ready for the distinction; it is the door the State will walk through if it can.

What the language **does** do: it denies the specific duties the Plaintiffs alleged. The Plaintiffs alleged duties of loyalty, prudence, accounting, productivity for the beneficiary, and undivided allegiance to the beneficiary's interest. These are the duties named by *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden* (1931), by the 1992 and 2003 Attorney General opinions, by the *Restatement (Third) of Trusts*, and by the Oregon Uniform Trust Code at ORS 130.650 through 130.710. By formally answering that it does not owe the Plaintiffs *the duties alleged*, the State has, on the public record of the Coos County Circuit Court, declined to acknowledge that those specific duties — the ones the doctrine has named — are duties the State concedes it owes.

What a state trial lawyer **will** be able to say: "We never denied being a trustee." Technically correct. He will say it.

What a state trial lawyer **will not** be able to say: that the State of Oregon, when given the chance to acknowledge that it owes Oregon's schoolchildren the trustee duties the common law has spelled out for two hundred years, did so. That chance was October 24, 2025. The State declined.

The reader does not need the Latin or the case citations to understand the asymmetry. A trustee that will not say, on a court pleading, that it owes the standard trustee duties has, by its silence and its qualifier, told you what it thinks of the trust.

</aside>

Three months after the Answer, on January 28, 2026, the Oregon Court of Appeals issued the opinion that restored the schoolchildren-plaintiffs' standing. I wrote to about forty plaintiffs and allies that evening with the subject line *MAJOR VICTORY: Court of Appeals Rules for School Trust Lands!* and the first line *It brings the entire Elliott back in play.* That same day — by Daniel's deliberate design — Daniel served on the State of Oregon Plaintiffs' First Request for Production of Documents in 24CV38372.

A First Request for Production is the document that lets one party in a lawsuit ask the other for the relevant records. In a case where the State of Oregon stands as the trustee of an intergenerational fund and the Plaintiffs include school districts and schoolchildren whose education depends on that fund's distributions, the records Daniel asked for were the records any trust lawyer would have asked for first. The accounting ledgers. The distribution data. The internal directives that show what the trustee told itself it was doing. The communications surrounding the Elliott transaction. The financial records of the corpus over the years our breach claims cover.

The State of Oregon responded on February 27, 2026.

Daniel's later motion describes the response in plain language: it consisted *"largely of 'General Objections.'"* I sat with the response for a week before I fully understood what Daniel meant by that phrase. A General Objection, in Oregon civil procedure, is a paragraph at the top of a discovery response that asserts a broad set of reservations — attorney-client privilege, work product, public-officer privilege, vagueness, overbreadth, burden — and then applies them across the whole response without engaging the specifics of any particular request. Oregon's Rules of Civil Procedure require that an objection "state with particularity" the grounds for objecting. A blanket General Objection does not particularize anything. It is the procedural form of declining to participate in discovery, written in the vocabulary of a participation.

The State did produce some documents. The Common School Fund Annual Financial Report for the fiscal year ended June 30, 2025 — a thirty-seven-page document the State publishes every year on its public website regardless of any litigation. A two-page CSF Fact Sheet. The Legislative Fiscal Office's seven-page 2025–27 Budget Review of the Department of State Lands. Two one-page Excel spreadsheets labeled, respectively, *DSL Statutory Investment Sale to Cover Operating Expenses* and *Rev Exp Const.*

That was the entirety of what the trustee gave the beneficiaries' counsel when the beneficiaries' counsel asked for the trust's books.

What the State did not produce was the substantive trust-accounting data — the native digital ledgers that would let the Plaintiffs reconcile the Common School Fund's distributions against the management's decisions over the period our claims cover. What the State did not produce were the internal communications, the directives, the meeting notes, the decision-records that would let a Plaintiff see *why* the State had done what its public reports show it did. The "what" without the "why."

I want to give you Daniel's own language for what the State was doing, because Daniel's reading of the discovery posture turned out to be the most important sentence in the entire 2026 record of the case. In the Motion to Compel that followed, Daniel wrote:

> *"Producing the 'what' (the raw numbers) while hiding the 'why' (the internal directives) is a tactical attempt to starve this litigation of the evidence needed to prove a breach of fiduciary duty."*

That sentence is what the discovery stonewall actually consists of. It is not a routine procedural dispute. It is the State of Oregon refusing to produce the records that would let the beneficiaries' counsel see the decision-trail behind the distributions — refusing, in other words, to account for what it has done with the trust.

Daniel did what an experienced litigator does next. He conferred. Oregon's Uniform Trial Court Rule 5.010 requires that the moving party engage in good-faith conferral before filing a motion to compel. Daniel wrote to the Department of Justice attorney, Christina Beatty-Walters, and asked the State to withdraw its General Objections and produce the native Excel ledgers, the budget data, and the responsive internal communications. The State declined.

On April 21, 2026, Daniel prepared **Plaintiffs' Motion to Compel Production of Documents** along with his Declaration in Support. The Motion walked the court through what Oregon law requires of a fiduciary defendant in discovery — the *Wood v. Honeyman* duty to provide full and accurate accounts, the procedural requirements of ORCP 36 and 43, the September 5, 2025 Letter Opinion in this same case that had already ruled common-law trust principles govern the State's fiduciary duties, and the Court of Appeals opinion of January 28, 2026 confirming that the management of the Common School Fund has practical effects on the Plaintiffs.

The Motion and Declaration were formally filed with the Coos County Circuit Court on **May 2, 2026**, after the April 21 through April 28 conferral period and standard rules-compliance work. Oral argument has been requested. The State's response will follow on the court's schedule.

That is where the procedural record sits at the moment I am writing this chapter. The State of Oregon, in October 2025, told a court of the State of Oregon that the duties Oregon's schoolchildren had asked it to perform were not duties it concedes it owes in the terms alleged. The State of Oregon, in February 2026, refused to give the beneficiaries' counsel the records of the trust the State holds for them. The State of Oregon, on May 2, 2026, is now subject to a motion to compel that asks the Coos County Circuit Court to order it to produce those records.

The smoking gun is not a leaked memo. It is on the public record of the Coos County Circuit Court, in a pleading the State filed on October 24, 2025, in language any reader can verify. The State's posture is not a litigation accident, not a placeholder, not a draft. It is the institutional position the State has held for twenty-five years, finally written down where a court has to read it.

I closed the PDF that Friday evening in October and walked outside. The Douglas-fir we planted in 1998 are now about sixty feet tall. The light was going. I stood at the edge of the field where the trees come down to the meadow and thought about what we were going to have to do.

The next chapter is what we are going to have to do.
