# Chapter 11 — Corvallis High School

> *A court's decision on the law governing the management of the common school fund will have practical effects on the amount that the common school fund contributes to the schoolchildren plaintiffs' education.*
>
> — Oregon Court of Appeals, *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, January 28, 2026

I want to start with the room.

The Court of Appeals heard oral argument in our 2023 case in the auditorium of Corvallis High School. Not in a marble courthouse. Not in the appellate panel's usual chamber in Salem. In a public-school auditorium, with the seats raked toward a stage that, for ordinary use, was set for student assemblies and visiting orchestras and Friday-night plays. On the morning of the argument the stage held a long table, a pair of microphones, a pitcher of water, and three judges in black robes who had driven down from the capital to be there.

The seats were not empty.

Natalie Scott — our appellate counsel — had asked the Court of Appeals to hold the argument under the court's longstanding program of taking oral arguments on the road to Oregon high schools and community colleges. The judges said yes. Natalie picked Corvallis High. She picked it because Corvallis High School is the school the named schoolchildren-plaintiffs in our case attend. She picked it because, when the State of Oregon stood up to argue that those named schoolchildren had no legal interest in the management of the trust the State holds for them, the schoolchildren would be sitting in the room.

I want to take you into that room now.

## I. Natalie's choice

I have practiced enough professional restraint over the course of this case to know when restraint is the wrong move, and I want to set it aside for one paragraph. Natalie Scott's decision to argue our 2023 appeal at Corvallis High School was the single most graceful piece of legal stagecraft I have ever watched a lawyer execute on our side of this fight.

She did not need to do it. The Court of Appeals would have heard the argument in Salem. The reasoning on the page would have been the same. What Natalie understood — what she had understood from the first time she walked our case file — was that the State's standing argument had a specific weakness, and that the weakness was not legal. The State's brief was going to argue, in language as careful as the State's lawyers could make it, that Oregon's schoolchildren are not the kind of party Oregon's courts can hear from on the question of how Oregon's school-trust is managed. The legal weakness of that position was the doctrine. The rhetorical weakness of that position was the room.

Natalie put the schoolchildren in the room.

She did not need to plant them. Corvallis High is in Corvallis. The plaintiffs lived where the plaintiffs lived. The school's principal posted a notice. Civics teachers brought their classes for the morning. Parents who had been following the case found out the argument was open to the public and drove in. A few of our board members — Bob Zybach, Barb, me — sat in the back. The named plaintiffs — the actual children whose names were on the caption of the case — sat with their parents in the middle rows.

I do not know whether the State's lawyers, when they walked in, fully registered who was in front of them. I think they did. I think the lawyers understood, the same way Natalie understood, that the room was now part of the argument.

## II. What the State said

I will not pretend the State's lawyers were not skilled. They were. The argument the State of Oregon had brought to the Court of Appeals — and that the State of Oregon had won at the trial-court level when our 2023 case was dismissed for lack of standing — was a clean argument as appellate arguments go. It went, in compressed form, like this:

The Common School Fund is a fund. It is administered by the State. The State has discretion in administering it. Decisions about how the State invests the Fund, how the State manages the lands that produce revenue for the Fund, how much the State chooses to distribute to schools in any given year — these are decisions that belong to the State, not to a court. The schoolchildren receiving the distribution are receiving a *benefit* from a state program. They are not, in any legally meaningful sense, *beneficiaries* whose rights a court is equipped to enforce. If they want the program managed differently, the venue is the legislature, the State Land Board, the ballot box. Not a courtroom.

It is a clever argument. It works on a reader who has never been told, in plain language, what Article VIII actually says. It works on a reader who has not been walked through the difference between a *benefit* — the kind of thing a state hands out at its discretion — and a *trust*, the kind of thing the State holds for somebody else and must answer for. It worked on the trial-court judge who had dismissed our 2023 case the first time around.

It did not work in the room.

The State's lawyer stood at the lectern and made the argument with care. He was professional. He did not raise his voice. He laid out the standing doctrine, the cases he wanted the panel to read, the reasons the trial court had been right to dismiss. He used the word *plaintiffs*. He used the word *children*. He did not look out at the rows.

I watched the faces of two of the named plaintiffs while he spoke. They were teenagers. They had been told, before the argument, what was going to happen. They had been told that the State of Oregon was going to argue that they had no legal right to ask about a fund that the Oregon Constitution had been pointing at them for one hundred and sixty-seven years. They had not, I think, fully understood until they heard a state attorney say it in their hearing what it meant to be told that on the record. Their faces did not move much. Their parents' faces moved more.

I have watched a lot of meetings over the years in which the State of Oregon argued, in front of an audience of the people the State's policies were about to harm, that the harm was not the State's responsibility. I have watched it at Land Board meetings. I have watched it at Joint Ways and Means. I have watched it at hearings in Salem where the people in the back of the room were waved off by the chair as having had their three minutes. The argument at Corvallis High was not the loudest of those moments. It was the quietest. It was also, for the case, the moment that turned.

## III. What Natalie said

When Natalie stood, she did not raise her voice either. She rarely does. She walked the panel through Article VIII. She walked the panel through the 1859 grant. She walked the panel through *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden* — the 1931 Oregon Supreme Court decision that called the State's relationship to the school lands a *trust of the highest nature* — and through the 1992 Attorney General opinion that restated the doctrine for the modern State. And then she walked the panel through Article VIII, Section 8.

Article VIII, Section 8 is the part of the Oregon Constitution that the 2000 amendment added. It says, in language Oregon's voters approved at the polls, that the legislature *shall* — not *may*, not *should*, but *shall* — appropriate sufficient funds to ensure that the State's system of public schools is adequately funded. The amendment made the legislature's duty to fund schools enforceable at law. It did so deliberately. The drafters of the amendment had been frustrated, across multiple decades, by the legislature's habit of treating school funding as a discretionary line item. They wrote the amendment to remove the discretion.

Natalie's argument, in compressed form, was this:

Whatever else the State's standing doctrine might do to a generalized citizen who wanted to sue the State about a state program, it cannot bar from the courthouse the named beneficiaries of a constitutional trust whose own constitution makes the State's duty to them enforceable at law. The trust is real. The duties are real. The beneficiaries — *those children, in the rows in front of you* — are real. The State's standing argument is a request that the court rule, as a matter of doctrine, that Article VIII is not what it says it is. The court should decline.

She did not point at the rows. She did not need to. The rows were already there.

<aside class="readers-edition-aside">

#### What Is Article VIII, Section 8?

Oregon's Constitution has had Article VIII — the article on education — since the state's admission to the Union in 1859. The original Article VIII set up the Common School Fund, named the State Land Board as its trustee, and identified the schoolchildren of Oregon as the beneficiaries of the trust the Fund holds.

For most of the next 141 years, the architecture was beautiful on paper and quiet in practice. The Fund existed. The trustee existed. The beneficiaries existed. But Oregon's courts, when asked whether the schoolchildren could *sue* to enforce the trust against the State, mostly said no — the duty was there, but it was not the kind of duty a court would order the State to perform.

Article VIII, Section 8 changed that. The amendment was placed on the ballot in 2000 and approved by Oregon's voters. Its text is short. Its operative phrase tells the legislature that it *shall* appropriate sufficient funds to ensure that Oregon's system of public schools is adequately funded. The word *shall* is the word that does the work. It is the word that converted a moral duty into a legal one.

What Section 8 gave the schoolchildren-plaintiffs in our 2023 case was the standing argument the trial court had refused them. The trial court had said, in effect, that even if the State was failing the trust, the schoolchildren were not the kind of plaintiff a court would hear. The Court of Appeals, on January 28, 2026, said back: Section 8 is the Constitution Oregon voters wrote. The duty it creates runs to the schoolchildren. The schoolchildren can ask a court to enforce it.

One sentence in a state constitution, added at the turn of a century by a generation of Oregon voters who had grown tired of waiting, turned out to be the sentence that opened the courthouse door.

</aside>

## IV. The wait

I will not pretend the months between the argument and the opinion were the calmest months I have spent on this case. They were not. We did the work. We organized the 2024 case. We sat through the trial-court rulings in Coos County that, by autumn 2025, had us moving toward discovery on the merits. We waited.

The Court of Appeals takes the time it takes. The panel that had heard the argument at Corvallis High was deliberating. The opinion would land when the opinion landed. Natalie said the same thing every time I asked, in the same tone: *We made the argument. The panel will write what the panel writes. Wait.*

I did what I always do when I am waiting on a thing I cannot move. I read. I worked on the substrate. I sat at my desk in Kings Valley and watched the rain come across the Coast Range and tried to picture the room at Corvallis High, and the rows, and the faces of those teenagers, and tried to imagine what an opinion that did right by them would actually say.

## V. The morning of January 28

The opinion came down on January 28, 2026.

I read it on my desktop at home. Natalie had emailed the link. The PDF opened in my browser. I scrolled to the disposition first — every plaintiff does — and read the word *Reversed*. Then I went back to the top and read the opinion straight through, slowly, the way I read the State's filings, the way Jerry Phillips taught me to read State documents twenty-five years ago when he was teaching me what the Elliott actually was.

The Court was unanimous. The reasoning was clean. The panel held what we had asked them to hold — that the schoolchildren-plaintiffs, the named beneficiaries of the trust the Oregon Constitution describes, have standing to assert the trust against the State that holds it for them. The opinion remanded the case to the trial court for further proceedings on the merits.

And somewhere in the body of the opinion, in language careful enough that I read it three times to be sure I had it right, was the operative sentence the OASTL litigation has been quoting at every subsequent procedural step:

> *A court's decision on the law governing the management of the common school fund will have practical effects on the amount that the common school fund contributes to the schoolchildren plaintiffs' education.*

I sat at my desk and read it again.

The sentence is, on its face, a sentence about standing. It says, in technical doctrinal terms, that the schoolchildren-plaintiffs satisfy the *injury-in-fact* test that Oregon's standing law requires — they have a real, concrete, redressable interest in how the law governing the Fund is decided. That is the legal work the sentence does.

What the sentence also does, for the constituency I had spent the last decade trying to organize, is something else. It is the first time, in 167 years, that an Oregon appellate court has written down — in the State's own court, in language the State will be bound by — that the schoolchildren of Oregon's schools have a legal interest in the management of the trust the State holds for them. That sentence is what the architecture had been waiting for since 1859.

167 years from Oregon's admission to the Union. 167 years from President Buchanan's signature on the Admissions Act. 167 years through every Land Board vote, every Department of State Lands report, every legislative session, every Common School Fund distribution. For 167 years the trust had been in place and the architecture had been in place and the State had been managing the corpus and the beneficiaries had been receiving — or, more often, not receiving — what the State chose to send them. And on January 28, 2026, an Oregon court wrote down, in twenty-eight words, that the beneficiaries had the right to ask.

## VI. The email I sent on January 30

Two days later I sent an email to about forty people — co-plaintiffs, allies, school-district superintendents, Margaret in Salt Lake, Bill Lansing, John Charles at Cascade Policy, the OASTL board, the Goulds. The subject line was *MAJOR VICTORY: Court of Appeals Rules for School Trust Lands!* The body was the kind of email I write when I am trying to be measured in public and have not yet stopped being moved in private.

The sentence in the email that I want to land in this chapter is this one:

> *It brings the entire Elliott back in play and gives Judge Combs the cover to rule as I think he believes.*

I meant it as a strategic note. Read at a distance, that is what it is. The standing victory in the 2023 case clears the doctrine in front of Judge Combs in our 2024 case in Coos County; what we had to fight to establish in the appellate court is now law in the trial court. The State's standing-based defenses, after January 28, 2026, are done. The Elliott — the actual ground, the actual trees, the actual revenue stream — is back on the table as something the court can reach.

Read up close, though, the sentence is a sentence about what it had felt like to walk through the years of this case with the sense that the law was not on our side. *Cover* is a strange word for a plaintiff to use about a judge. What I meant by it was the recognition that even the judges most inclined to hear us had been operating in an environment where the doctrinal cover for them to rule with us did not yet exist. After January 28, it did.

The replies came in over the next several days. Bill Lansing, who has been with this since the early years, wrote back his congratulations. John Charles wrote back from Cascade Policy. Margaret wrote back from Utah; she had been watching the opinion for the same reason the rest of us had — because what was about to happen in Oregon was going to set precedent for every other school-trust state on Margaret's national list. And a half-dozen school superintendents wrote back asking the question I had been hoping they would ask: *what do we do now?*

I will tell you what we did then.

## VII. What came next

On the same day the Court of Appeals opinion came down — January 28, 2026 — Daniel Crowe served the State of Oregon with our First Request for Production of Documents in the 2024 case. The timing was deliberate. The standing victory had cleared the doctrine in front of Judge Combs. The case in front of Judge Combs was now moving toward discovery on the merits. And the first ask of any beneficiary to a trustee — once the beneficiary has standing to ask at all — is the records of the trust.

The State had thirty days to respond.

The State responded on February 27, 2026.

The Response is the subject of Chapter 12. What I will say here, by way of foreshadowing, is that the Response was not what a trustee's counsel files when the trustee believes it owes the duties the beneficiaries' counsel had just won, in the State's own appellate court, the right to ask about.

The room at Corvallis High had held the answer to the standing question. The Response on February 27, 2026 — and the Answer the State had filed three months earlier, on October 24, 2025, in which the State of Oregon told a court of competent jurisdiction in plain English that it *does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged* — held the answer to a different question.

The question was: when the schoolchildren of Oregon finally do walk into the courthouse with standing to ask, what is the State going to say back?

We are about to find out.
