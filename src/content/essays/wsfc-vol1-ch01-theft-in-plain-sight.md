# Chapter 1 — The Theft in Plain Sight

Who steals from children?

I want to walk you through the answer, because we have caught the State of Oregon in the act, and the act is not subtle.

Oregon is trying to take a $1-billion-plus asset that belongs in trust for schoolchildren and convert it into a research forest at OSU paid for by general-fund bonds, and we have caught them in the act. That is the whole indictment. The rest of this book is the evidence.

The asset is the Elliott State Forest. Ninety-three thousand acres of Douglas-fir in the Coast Range, between Coos Bay and Reedsport. The Elliott is the largest contiguous block of working timberland the people of Oregon ever held for the explicit benefit of their schoolchildren. It was assembled by the State of Oregon in 1930 around a 1859 land grant that came in with statehood — the federal grant of every Section 16 and Section 36 of each township, doubled for Oregon, given to the State to hold in trust forever for the support of the common schools. State Forester Francis Elliott pulled the scattered school sections together by swapping them into the burnt-over Siuslaw block in the late 1920s, and the consolidated forest carried his name. Between 1955, when serious harvesting began, and 2001, when the last large sale was held, the Elliott produced for Oregon's schoolchildren what one of the foresters who ran it, Jerry Phillips, calculated at $790 million in revenue and roughly 400 jobs a year for the towns on the coast.

That is what was put in trust. This is what is being taken.

Between 1990 and 2022, the State of Oregon, through its State Land Board — the Governor, the Secretary of State, and the State Treasurer, sitting as trustees of the Common School Fund — decided to take the Elliott out of the trust. Not all at once. Not by amendment to the Constitution. By a sequence of administrative decisions that, taken together, transfer a working trust asset of fair-market value somewhere between one billion and one-and-three-tenths billion dollars out of the school children's trust and into a research forest owned and operated by Oregon State University, paid for not by the buyer at fair value but by Oregon taxpayers issuing general-obligation bonds against their own tax base. The trust loses the asset; the schoolchildren lose the income; the taxpayers pay for the conversion; OSU's College of Forestry gets a research forest; and the State Land Board, having spent down the corpus, gets to call it stewardship.

That is the theft. Now let me show you the receipts.

## The 2017 sale that named the price

The cleanest documentary record of what the Elliott is worth on the open market is the 2017 sale that did not happen.

In August 2015, after a decade of litigation losses that had driven harvest revenue to near zero, the State Land Board adopted Resolution and Order 2015-7, the "Elliott Property Comprehensive Ownership Transfer Opportunity." The Department of State Lands issued a formal Request for Proposals in July 2016. The protocol set a fixed price — bids had to come in at exactly $220.8 million; anything above that would be "considered outside the protocol and deemed non-responsive." The State, in other words, asked the open market what the Elliott was worth and then capped the answer.

In November 2016, a consortium led by Lone Rock Timber Management Company and the Cow Creek Band of Umpqua Tribe of Indians submitted the only qualifying bid: $220.8 million on the nose. In February 2017, the State Land Board voted to accept it. In May 2017, under public pressure, the Land Board reversed itself. Then-Secretary of State Dennis Richardson, a Republican, said for the record at the May 7 meeting that "when a group of potential buyers spend over a half a million dollars to comply with protocols and then they comply with them, and then for the State to back out, I think that that's a breach of our ethics." Lone Rock's CEO Toby Luther was sharper. "You cannot simultaneously encourage bidders and commit to bidders publicly while privately planning a shift in policy." Lone Rock filed a tort claim for $1.3 million in reliance damages and $2 million in lost-opportunity damages. The State settled.

I do not raise the 2017 sale to argue that Oregon should have sold the Elliott to Lone Rock. I raise it because $220.8 million is what the open market said the Elliott was worth on a constrained protocol that suppressed every value above that floor. That is the number the State accepted and then walked away from. Hold it in your mind. Two hundred and twenty million is the floor; it is not the price.

## The 2019 bond substitution

Having reversed the sale, the State faced an arithmetic problem. The Elliott was costing the trust money to hold under the new no-harvest regime. Schoolchildren are constitutionally entitled to the income from the trust. The State Land Board needed a way to make the income line stop bleeding without selling the asset.

In 2019, the Oregon Legislature authorized the State to issue $100 million in general-obligation bonds. The proceeds went into the Common School Fund to "compensate" the trust for reduced timber revenue from the Elliott. On the surface this looks like the same dollars going to the same place. Underneath, it is a different transaction entirely. The trust used to have a claim on a particular working asset — 93,000 acres of growing Douglas-fir, with a measurable annual yield, that the trustee was obligated to manage productively. After the 2019 bond, the trust has a claim on a line item in the state budget, serviced by the taxpayer-funded debt service of a general-obligation bond. The asset is now severable. The line item is reviewable. The "compensation" is no longer tied to anything the trust actually owns. That is the legal substance of what came to be called "decoupling" — separating the Common School Fund's income from the Elliott as a corpus asset, by replacing the corpus claim with a budget claim.

In 2021, the Legislature added a further $121 million appropriation. The total bonded indebtedness associated with decoupling now stands at $146 million in projected debt service. As John A. Charles, Jr. of the Cascade Policy Institute put it: "Without knowing it, taxpayers bought a state forest that they already owned."

## The 2022 vote and the OSU "research forest"

On December 13, 2022, the State Land Board voted to sell the Elliott to itself for $99.6 million, based on an appraisal by Mason Bruce & Girard establishing the forest's "investment value" — a constrained valuation reflecting heavy use restrictions and harvest caps imposed by the buyer, the State of Oregon, on itself. The asset is then conveyed to a new statutory body — the Elliott State Research Forest Authority, created by SB 1546A earlier the same year — which would manage it under a Forest Management Plan designed and operated in partnership with Oregon State University's College of Forestry. The plan: roughly two-thirds of the forest in reserve, less than 20 percent each for intensive and extensive research treatments, with rotation ages of 60 and 100 years, and an aggregate harvest of about 17 million board-feet a year against an annual growth on the land of about 75 million.

Two things to notice about that number — $99.6 million.

First, the same forest that brought $220.8 million on the open market in 2017 was, five years later, valued at $99.6 million by an appraisal commissioned by the buyer. Bill Lansing, retired CEO of Menasha Forest Products Corporation — a man who built and ran one of the larger private timber companies in the Pacific Northwest — said this on the record: "I was involved in the proposed sale of the Elliott State Forest a few years ago and could not believe the low value of the appraisal. ... I suggest the establishment of the low value was politically motivated to allow for a cheaper decoupling of the common school fund's primary responsibility."

Margaret Bird, the national authority on school-trust lands and one of the founders of the Advocates for School Trust Lands, put the fair-market figure at one billion to one-and-three-tenths billion dollars in her December 11, 2022 letter to the Land Board, two days before the vote. The earlier independent analysis by economist John Beuter — commissioned by Oregon's own Department of Forestry in 1994 — had concluded the Elliott's market value was "not likely to be less than $600 million, and could well be in the neighborhood of a billion dollars." The State accepted $99.6 million from itself for an asset its own retained expert had pegged at six to ten times that figure thirty years earlier. The State is the trustee. The State is the buyer. The State set the price.

Second — and this is the part that I want every Oregon parent to understand — the new Elliott State Research Forest was rebranded, in the political marketing surrounding the 2022 vote, as the "Children's Forest." That rebrand happened while the children's trust was losing the asset. The rhetorical move was breathtaking: take the asset out of the schoolchildren's trust, give it to a university, and call it children's. Two yard signs went up in coastal towns in those months — "Save the Children's Forest." One was the State's; one was ours.

The plan's own numbers tell on it. In December 2021, OSU's College of Forestry submitted its formal proposal for the Elliott State Research Forest. On page 28 of that proposal, OSU's own analysts wrote: "Based on the current research platform design and allocation of watersheds across the different treatments, preliminary financial analysis demonstrates that the ESRF is not self-sustaining from a financial perspective without an alternative source of revenue ... Currently there is a $2.1 million deficit on an average annual basis for the first 50-years."

OSU's own number. A $2.1 million annual deficit, every year, for fifty years, on a forest the open market said was worth at least $220 million and a national expert says is worth a billion-plus. That is not a research forest sustaining itself by managed harvest. That is a research forest that requires subsidy in perpetuity, drawn from somewhere outside the trust, against an asset the trust no longer holds.

Jerry Franklin — the senior figure in Pacific Northwest old-growth forestry, the man more responsible than anyone living for what we know about how these forests work — was asked to peer-review OSU's experimental design. His response, in Appendix 13 of the proposal itself, was unsparing: "I do not find that the design meets the high standards that are required for a statistically valid and, perhaps more important, a socially convincing outcome ... you don't have any true controls!" And: "the whole notion that you are doing a meaningful test of the TRIAD concept is nonsense." And, plainly: "It is your proposal. I do not think that it does credit to the institution or yourselves; you can do much better than this." The State is converting a billion-dollar trust asset into a research vehicle that the leading expert in the field, reviewing the State's own proposal, calls scientifically incoherent.

## The smoking gun

For three years we tried to address all of this in the channels designed for it — letters to the Land Board, testimony in public meetings, public-records requests, two lawsuits, an amicus filing from the national ASTL. The State responded with administrative process and silence. Then, on October 24, 2025, the State filed its formal Answer in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, Coos County Circuit Court case 24CV38372, and used the following words, on the record: the State "does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."

Read that sentence twice.

The plaintiffs in 24CV38372 are schoolchildren, school districts, and the constitutional advocacy organization formed to enforce the trust on their behalf. The duties alleged are the duties of a trustee to a beneficiary, under Article VIII, Section 8 of the Oregon Constitution and the 1859 federal Admissions Act. Asked, on the record, in a court of law, whether it owes the duties of a trustee to the schoolchildren for whom the Common School Fund was established in 1859, the State of Oregon answered no.

That answer is the smoking gun, and the State signed it.

## What changed on January 28, 2026

For most of the period 1859 to 2024, the doctrinal question of who in Oregon has standing to enforce the school trust against the State as trustee was unsettled. The State's litigating position, across decades, was that only the Attorney General could sue — which meant, in practice, that the State sued the State, never, and the trust went unenforced.

On January 28, 2026, the Oregon Court of Appeals, in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, reversed the trial court's dismissal of our case and held that schoolchildren, school districts, and OASTL had constitutional standing under Article VIII, Section 8 to bring claims for breach of the trust. The hearing had been held at Corvallis High School in front of real students; we believed at the time, and continue to believe, that the courtroom architecture mattered. The judges saw who the beneficiaries are. A hundred and sixty-seven years after Oregon promised, in writing, to hold these lands in trust for the schoolchildren, an organized beneficiary constituency walked into a courtroom and was told, for the first time, that they have the right to be heard.

The State's "does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged" filing came three months before that ruling. As of January 28, 2026, the beneficiaries the State told the court it owed no duties to are, by the appellate court's holding, the people the State must answer to.

## What this book is

This book is the documentary case. Twelve chapters; named actors; dated decisions; dollar figures; the source on every claim. Foreword by Margaret Bird, who has spent forty years inside the architecture of school-trust law and who, in October 2016, observed for the public record that in the entire 235-year history of school trust lands in the United States, no state had ever spent more than it earned on its trust assets — until Oregon. Body chapters by me, Dave Sullivan: lead plaintiff in 24CV38372, president of the Advocates for School Trust Lands, retired professor of business at OSU, the institution that, through its College of Forestry, is the willing partner to the conversion this book documents. The legal sections were drafted with Daniel Zene Crowe, our General Counsel and lead trial attorney in the active case.

The reader who reads only Chapter 1 has the indictment. The chapters that follow document it.

Chapter 2 takes us back to what Oregon promised in 1859 — the constitutional architecture of the Common School Fund, the trust character of the grant, the binding language of Article VIII, and the federal Admissions Act that put the duty into law. That architecture is doctrinally settled. The breach is what comes after.

The trust was set up by people who knew exactly what they were doing. Let me show you what they built.
