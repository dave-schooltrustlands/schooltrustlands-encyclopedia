# Chapter 10 — OASTL Forms; the Suit Filed; the Standing Victory

I want to ask you to hold two dates in your head as you read this chapter. The first is February 14, 1859 — the day President Buchanan signed the Oregon Admissions Act and the school-trust architecture this book has been walking through came into legal effect. The second is January 28, 2026 — the day the Oregon Court of Appeals issued its opinion in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* and held that the children of Oregon's schools have legal standing to enforce that trust against the State that holds it for them.

One hundred and sixty-seven years sit between those two dates. For all of that time, the trust existed. The doctrine existed. The corpus existed. The State held the legal title and stood in the position the 1859 Act and Article VIII assigned it — trustee, on behalf of beneficiaries the Constitution named as the schoolchildren of Oregon. What did not exist, for any of those one hundred and sixty-seven years, was an organized beneficiary constituency that could walk into a courtroom in the State of Oregon and assert the trust against the trustee in the trustee's name.

On January 28, 2026, after 167 years, that constituency finally appeared inside the courthouse and asserted the trust against the state as trustee — and won the standing question.

This chapter is about how we got there.

## I. The thing that had been missing

I want to spend a paragraph on what I mean by *the constituency.*

The 1859 architecture, as Chapter 2 walked through, was as clean an instrument as the American public-land system produced. The federal grant. Article VIII. The State Land Board at the top of the management structure under doctrines the Oregon courts had affirmed since *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden* in 1931. The 1992 Attorney General opinion calling the trust relationship "a trust of the highest nature." The 2000 amendment adding Article VIII Section 8, making the school-funding duty enforceable at law.

What the architecture did not produce was the operational defense the architecture had always required. A trust without a beneficiary constituency that knows it is a beneficiary, that knows what its rights are, and that knows how to enforce those rights against a misbehaving trustee, is a trust the trustee can manage in whatever direction the trustee's institutional incentives point. That is what the Elliott story across Chapters 4 through 9 documents. The Department of State Lands and the State Land Board did not move against an empty room — they moved against a room in which the legal beneficiaries had no organized presence at all. The OEA did not show up. The OSBA did not show up. Individual school districts did not show up. Parents did not show up. The closest thing to an organized beneficiary voice between 1990 and 2024 was a handful of retired foresters, a tree farmer or two, an economist in Salt Lake City who had built the equivalent system in another state, and the occasional environmental advocate who got the framing right but had institutional interests of their own.

The work of organizing a beneficiary constituency, the work of putting that constituency into a posture from which it could appear inside the courthouse and assert the trust against the trustee, was not done. The architecture sat there waiting for it to be done. From 1859 through the Lone Rock vote in 2017, through the $100 million bond substitution in 2019, through the $99.6 million "investment value" vote of December 13, 2022, through the secret meetings and the records-request stonewall, the constituency was missing.

## II. OASTL — what it is and why it exists

The Oregon Advocates for School Trust Lands was incorporated in the State of Oregon in 2024 as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit corporation. The organization is the Oregon affiliate of the national Advocates for School Trust Lands, which Margaret Bird and the Utah cohort had founded in 1999 to do nationally what OASTL is now doing in Oregon.

I want to describe OASTL plainly, because some readers of this book will have heard the organization described in adversarial terms by people who have an interest in misdescribing it.

OASTL is a registered Oregon nonprofit. Its board members serve without compensation. I am the President. Barb Sullivan is the Treasurer. Laura Cooper sits on the Board as a member-attorney. Bob Zybach serves as Secretary. Margaret Bird, although she lives in Utah and serves as President of the national ASTL, is on the Oregon affiliate's Board as well. David Gould, who served as the organization's first President in 2023 and who was a great-grandson of the Elkhorn Ranch pioneers whose land sits inside today's Elliott State Forest, passed away in 2024 and is named in memoriam on the Board page. None of us is being paid.

OASTL exists for one purpose: to function as the organized beneficiary constituency that, for the 165 years from 1859 to 2024, did not exist inside the State of Oregon to enforce the school-trust against the State. OASTL exists to bring beneficiary-side cases against the State of Oregon for breach of fiduciary duty. The school-trust corpus is the trust. The schoolchildren of Oregon, and through them their school districts and their parents, are the beneficiaries. The State is the trustee. OASTL is the organized voice of the beneficiaries.

That is all the organization is, and that is all the organization needs to be.

Daniel and OASTL signed a Joint Engagement and General Counsel Employment Agreement on December 19, 2025.

**Natalie Scott** of The Scott Law Group is the lead appellate counsel who secured the January 28, 2026 Court of Appeals win in the 2023 case (Coos County 23CV39056; Court of Appeals A184055; petition pending before the Oregon Supreme Court at S072734). Natalie graduated summa cum laude from the University of Oregon School of Law and clerked for Judge David Brewer on the Oregon Court of Appeals. Behind them sits the bench: **Margaret Bird**, who carries the trust-doctrine substance from her 40 years inside the architecture; **Laura Cooper** (OSB #863589), admitted to the U.S. Supreme Court and the 9th Circuit; and the research bridge that Laura coordinates with on case-law work as the case proceeds.

That is the cast. That is the room. That is the constituency that walked into the courthouse on August 9, 2024, and filed the case 24CV38372 is the docket number of.

## IV. The 2024 case — *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*

The case was filed in the Circuit Court of the State of Oregon for the County of Coos on **August 9, 2024**, as case number **24CV38372**. The named plaintiffs are **Siuslaw School District 97J**, **Powers School District 31**, **Oakland School District**, **Advocates for School Trust Lands** (the national ASTL), and **OASTL** (the Oregon affiliate). Subsequent amendments added individual minor-schoolchildren plaintiffs through their guardians ad litem — **April Diviney** as guardian of Bradley Reiter; **Robin Ramsey** as guardian of Connor Ramsey. The defendants are the State of Oregon, the State Land Board, Governor **Tina Kotek**, then-Secretary of State **LaVonne Griffin-Valade** (later **Tobias Read** in that office, with **Elizabeth Steiner** added as State Treasurer), and the Treasurer in office at the relevant time.

The complaint asserts the architecture this book has been walking through. The Oregon Admissions Act and Article VIII establish a school-trust the State holds for the benefit of Oregon's schoolchildren. The State's management of the Common School Fund and the trust lands that fund it, during the years up to and including 2024, has breached the fiduciary duties any trustee owes a beneficiary — the duty of prudence, the duty of loyalty, the duty to account, the duty not to engage in self-dealing transactions. The remedy the plaintiffs seek is the remedy any beneficiary can seek against a misbehaving trustee — disclosure of records, an accounting, restitution where the breach has caused loss, and equitable relief against the State to enforce the trust going forward.

The Complaint reaches the Elliott directly. The 2017 Lone Rock sale-and-reverse, the 2019 bond substitution, the 2022 OSU plan and the "Children's Forest" rebranding, the December 13, 2022 SLB vote to "decouple" the Elliott at $99.6 million, the systematic refusal to provide trust-accounting data to representatives of the beneficiaries — all of it is on the face of the Complaint. The First Amended Complaint, filed April 22, 2025, sharpened the pleading. The harm — to a Common School Fund that is supposed to be growing and is instead operating at a net loss, with Oregon as the only state in the 235-year history of school trust lands to have lost money on its trust — is documented in the State's own reporting.

The State's response, when the Complaint was filed, was the response the State has been giving for thirty-five years to anyone who asked the questions this Complaint asks. The State moved to dismiss.

I will let Chapter 11 take up what the State said in its Motion to Dismiss and in its October 24, 2025 Answer — the language is so striking, on a freestanding basis, that the next chapter is built around it. What matters for this chapter is what the trial court and the Court of Appeals did with the State's positions.

## V. The trial court rulings — Judge Combs

Judge **Combs** of the Coos County Circuit Court is the trial judge. The case has been before him from filing to the present.

On **March 6, 2025**, Judge Combs issued a Letter Opinion granting standing to the plaintiffs and denying the State's Motion to Dismiss on the 2024-breach claims. The Letter Opinion held that the school districts, the schoolchildren plaintiffs, and the parents had standing to assert the trust under the Oregon Admissions Act and Article VIII. The 2024-breach claims — the claims for breaches committed by the State in the calendar year preceding the filing of the Complaint, within whatever statute-of-limitations window the doctrine allows — survived. The case proceeded.

On **September 5, 2025**, Judge Combs issued a second Letter Opinion denying the State's Motion to Dismiss on what the State had styled as immunity grounds. The State had argued that only the Attorney General could bring a claim of this kind on behalf of the State, that sovereign immunity barred the action, and that the Land Board's decisions were "discretionary" in a sense that immunized them from review. Judge Combs rejected all three. The school-trust doctrine has, across multiple states for a century and a half, recognized beneficiary standing as a matter of course. The trust doctrine runs as a substantive duty of the State independent of common-law sovereign-immunity doctrine. A trustee's fiduciary duties are not "discretionary" in the sense the State was invoking.

The two Letter Opinions, taken together, are the trial court's foundation. The 2024 case had standing. The breach theory was viable. The State's immunity defenses had been rejected. The case was moving toward discovery on the merits.

## VI. The Court of Appeals — January 28, 2026

While 24CV38372 was moving through the Coos County Circuit Court, the parallel 2023 case — *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, Coos County 23CV39056 — was on appeal to the Oregon Court of Appeals as A184055.

The 2023 case was the predecessor matter. Filed September 25, 2023 by Natalie Scott, the case had asserted similar Article VIII fiduciary-breach claims on behalf of schoolchildren plaintiffs. The trial court had dismissed the case in February 2024 on standing grounds — finding that the schoolchildren-plaintiffs lacked the legal standing to assert the trust against the State. Natalie filed the appeal. Her Opening Brief, filed August 2, 2024, argued that Article VIII Section 8 — the 2000 amendment making the legislature's duty to fund schools enforceable at law — gives schoolchildren-plaintiffs the standing the trial court had denied.

The Court of Appeals heard oral argument in 2025. The oral argument was held, in a procedural arrangement Natalie negotiated and that I want to call out specifically, at **Corvallis High School**, with real Oregon schoolchildren — the named beneficiaries of the trust — present in the audience while the State of Oregon argued that those schoolchildren had no legal interest in the management of the trust their constitutional rights named them as the beneficiaries of. I attended. The contrast between the State's brief and the room the brief was being read in did the case more rhetorical work than any of us had planned for.

On **January 28, 2026**, the Court of Appeals issued its opinion. Natalie won. The Court reversed the trial court's dismissal on standing grounds. The Court held, in the operative language the OASTL litigation has been quoting at every subsequent procedural step:

> *A court's decision on the law governing the management of the common school fund will have practical effects on the amount that the common school fund contributes to the schoolchildren plaintiffs' education.*

The legal logic of that sentence is the legal logic the OASTL constituency had been waiting one hundred and sixty-seven years to hear an Oregon appellate court write. The school-trust's management is not a "political question" from which the beneficiaries can be excluded. The State's management decisions have *practical effects* on the funding the beneficiaries receive. The beneficiaries have standing to challenge management decisions that breach the trust. The constitutional architecture of Article VIII is enforceable at law — by the beneficiaries the Constitution named.

The opinion was unanimous. The reasoning in the body was clean. The case was remanded to the trial court for further proceedings on the merits.

The doctrinal posture, after January 28, 2026, is the posture this book has been describing all along, finally rendered into Oregon case law. The trust is real. The duties are real. The beneficiaries can sue. The State must answer.

## VII. The discovery posture and what comes next

After the January 28, 2026 Court of Appeals win, the OASTL team — Daniel as lead counsel in the 2024 case, Natalie as appellate counsel in the 2023 case, with the Supreme Court petition in 23CV39056 now pending — moved to put the doctrinal win to work.

In the 2024 case, that work began with the documents. On **January 28, 2026** — the same day the Court of Appeals opinion came down, by deliberate timing — Daniel served the State with **Plaintiffs' First Request for Production of Documents**. The RFPs asked for what any beneficiary asks a trustee for: the records of the trust's management.

The State of Oregon, on **February 27, 2026**, served its Response.

The Response is the subject of Chapter 11. What I will say here, by way of foreshadowing, is that the Response was not what a trustee's counsel files when the trustee believes it owes the duties the litigation has alleged.

For one hundred and sixty-seven years the architecture had held a seat open at the table for a constituency that did not exist. In 2024 the constituency formed. In 2025 the trial court ruled that the constituency had standing and the case could proceed. On January 28, 2026, the Court of Appeals confirmed that the constitutional architecture means what it has said it means since 1859. And on February 27, 2026, the State of Oregon answered the first discovery request by refusing to turn over the records.

The standing victory opened the case. What happens when the State of Oregon is finally asked, on the public record of a court of competent jurisdiction, to account for what it has done with the school-trust — that is the next chapter.
