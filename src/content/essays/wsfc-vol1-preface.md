# Preface

## Why I Wrote This Book

Before you read any of the chapters that follow, I want to tell you what I am asking you to read this book *as*. It is not a memoir or a polemic. It is an indictment — assembled from the documentary record, dated, named, footnoted, offered as a citizen's brief on what the State of Oregon is doing to its schoolchildren and to the forest that was supposed to fund them. I have written it the way the case will be tried: in dates, in dollars, in named actors, in the actors' own words. Where I have an opinion, I will tell you it is an opinion. Where I have a fact, I will give you the document.

I started this work in 1996, although I did not know it at the time. I had recently joined the OSU College of Business as an assistant professor, after fifteen years at Tektronix in Portland watching a great American company lose its grip on what it had invented. I bought a tree farm in the Kings Valley west of Corvallis, joined the Oregon Small Woodlands Association, and began to pay attention to the Elliott State Forest, about a hundred and twenty miles south of my land, which the Oregon Department of Forestry was, in those years, managing well.

The Elliott was producing about fifty million board feet of timber annually for the schools. Jerry Phillips, the District Manager who ran the Elliott from 1968 to 2001, said that his thirty-three years there had produced "$790 million dollars of revenue in Oregon's Irreducible Common School Fund and supported about 400 jobs for the local economy." That was the baseline. That is the world this book is, in part, trying to explain the loss of.

By 2017 the baseline was gone. The harvest had dropped to zero. The State Land Board, sitting as trustee, had decided that the Elliott was "a net drain on the fund" and had set in motion a series of decisions — the Lone Rock sale, the Lone Rock reversal, the OSU "research forest" plan, the $100 million bond issue, the $99.6 million appraisal, the December 2022 vote, the formal denial of fiduciary duty in 2025 — that, taken together, amount to the conversion of a billion-dollar trust asset to a research forest paid for by general-fund bonds.

I want to tell you the moment the case crystallized for me.

It was October 24, 2025. I was sitting at my desk in Kings Valley reading the State's formal Answer in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*. In paragraph after paragraph the State, through its Department of Justice, denied the substantive allegations in our complaint. That part I expected. What I did not expect was the language the State used to deny the duty itself. The State of Oregon, sitting as trustee of an Article VIII trust the Oregon Constitution had established in 1859 and the Oregon Supreme Court had recognized in *Eagle Point Irrigation District v. Cowden* in 1931 as a "trust of the highest nature," filed a pleading saying it "does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."

I read the sentence twice. Then I read it again. I sat at the desk for a while and did not move.

What I had been arguing for two decades — that the State was breaching its fiduciary duty, that the trust was being drained, that the Elliott was being taken from the schoolchildren — had been, until that moment, an allegation I had to prove. The State's preferred posture had been to argue jurisdiction, standing, ripeness, the technicalities. With its October 24, 2025 Answer the State did something different. It told the court, in writing, that it owed no duties to my clients at all. It denied the trust.

That was the moment. *They have given me, in their own pleading, the indictment.*

The book you are about to read is the indictment that filing made writable. Every chapter is some version of: *here is what the State has done, here is the document showing it, here is the date.* I am not asking you to take my word. I am asking you to read what the State itself put in writing and draw the conclusion the documents draw.

I owe you, before we begin, the source of the title.

In May 2024, the journalist Jim Petersen, who has covered Pacific Northwest forestry for sixty years and who publishes *Evergreen Magazine* out of Bigfork, Montana, published an article on the Elliott decoupling. He titled it *Who Steals from Children?* He sent me a transmittal note on May 5, 2024 with the final approved versions, and on May 7 *Evergreen* posted it. Petersen's editor's note opened with a sentence that has stayed with me longer than any other writing on this case: *"In my 60 years as a working journalist, Who Steals from Children is easily one of the most frustrating and purposefully deceptive stories I've ever followed."* When the Library decided, in early 2026, to launch an anthology series documenting state-by-state attempts to diminish Common School Funds, Jim Petersen's title was the only one that fit. He has graciously allowed us to carry the phrase forward. This book is Volume 1 of the series; the title is his, with attribution.

The title is sharp enough that I owe you a clear statement of what I have not written.

I have not written a polemic. The State of Oregon is not run by villains. It is run, mostly, by people I have voted for, in some cases campaigned for, in a few cases known socially for decades. None of them, to my knowledge, set out to break the trust. What this book documents is the slower and more troubling pattern of how a trust can be broken by people of generally good intentions who, decision by decision, conclude that the next move is defensible. Each move can be defended. The cumulative pattern cannot. The indictment is not a moral one against any particular person. It is a fiduciary one against the institution as it has been functioning for a decade.

I have not written a partisan book. Oregon's drift has happened under Democratic governors and been criticized by Republicans — Secretary of State Dennis Richardson before his death, State Representative Court Boice in open letters to the current Governor and Treasurer. Richardson's verdict on the Lone Rock reversal was that the State had committed "a breach of our ethics." I give credit where it is owed, regardless of party.

I have not written an attack on environmentalism. I am a tree farmer. I love the Elliott. What I object to is not the *protection* of the forest but the *theft* of the forest from the trust — the use of "research" and "carbon" and "wildlife" as cover stories for the conversion of a billion-dollar asset out of the children's trust into a state agency the legislature can fund or defund as it pleases. The forest can be protected. The children can be served. The State has chosen to do neither while telling everyone it has done both.

I have not written a final word. The case is in active litigation. The Court of Appeals' January 28, 2026 standing ruling has opened the door; the trial phase is ahead of us. I will update what I can in future printings.

What I have written is the case as it stood on the day the State formally denied the duty: a fiduciary indictment in plain language, with the documents available for inspection, addressed to the parents and school administrators and journalists and legislators and trust-law specialists who can, between them, decide whether what is being taken will continue to be taken.

The forest belongs to the children. The trust says so. The Constitution says so. The State of Oregon says, in writing, that it does not.

This book is the record of the disagreement, and an argument that the children should win it.

— Dave Sullivan, Coos County, Oregon, 2026.
