# Chapter 8 — Enforcement when the Beneficiary Cannot Sue: The Captured-AG Architecture and the Eighth Anchor

*Parens patriae, the substitute bar, and the doctrinal work of the constituency*

---

A trust without an enforcer is a wish. A trust whose enforcer works for the trustee is something else again — the structure that produced *Schools of the Republic*'s subject for the better part of two centuries. The architecture chapters of this book describe what was built. This chapter describes how it gets policed when the office charged with policing it is on the trustee's payroll.

In classical charitable-trust doctrine, the natural plaintiff is not the beneficiary. The beneficiary class in a charitable trust is, by design, indefinite — a class of present and future schoolchildren, or of the poor, or of the public — and indefinite classes do not as a rule sue. The Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 94 carries forward the centuries-old chancery rule that a charitable trust is enforceable by the State Attorney General as *parens patriae*, with Restatement § 95 confirming the AG's role and Bogert §§ 411–417 mapping the doctrinal mechanics in the modern American state.[^ch8-1] The Uniform Trust Code § 405(c) installs the same rule by statute in the states that have adopted it. The architecture presupposes a state law officer institutionally capable of suing the trustee.

In the school-trust context, the trustee is the State. The State Attorney General is the trustee's lawyer. The presupposition that built the doctrine — independence of the *parens patriae* from the entity *parens patriae* enforces against — is structurally absent. The result is what this chapter calls captured-*parens patriae* enforcement, and the question the rest of the chapter takes up is how a school-trust beneficiary class actually obtains a hearing when the natural plaintiff is on the other side of the table.

The short answer is that enforcement happens through substitutes. The longer answer is that the substitute architecture has worked in a small but consistent set of cases. It has not worked in the larger set of cases where the substitute has been absent. The substitute is what the eighth anchor — the Library, the constituency, the standing community — is doctrinally for. This is the chapter's editorial argument. It is offered with the modal restraint the rest of the book applies. The historical record does the work the prose does not.

## I. The captured-AG architecture

The Restatement (Third) of Trusts § 94 begins from a premise that is almost embarrassingly simple. A charitable trust is "subject to enforcement at the suit of the Attorney General or other appropriate public officer."[^ch8-2] The premise is simple because the institutional separation it presupposes is, in the ordinary charitable trust, real. A private foundation's trustees are private parties. The state AG is a public officer. When the AG sues the trustees for self-dealing or for diversion of trust funds, two distinct entities meet in court. Bogert §§ 411–417 walks through the mechanics: the AG's standing, the AG's discretion, the AG's exclusivity in some jurisdictions and concurrence with named beneficiaries or "special interest" plaintiffs in others.[^ch8-3]

The doctrinal apparatus is centuries old. It traces, through Bogert and the Restatement, back to the Statute of Charitable Uses of 1601 and the chancellor's supervisory authority over charitable funds. The core of the apparatus is a single institutional fact: the AG and the trustee are different. They report to different principals. They face different incentives. They can litigate against each other without one of them firing the other.

That institutional fact does not survive the school-trust transition. When the State of Oregon is the trustee of the Common School Fund and the Oregon Department of Justice is the trustee's counsel, the doctrinal apparatus loses its mainspring. The AG cannot sue the trustee because the AG *is* the trustee's lawyer. The same office that, in the private-foundation case, would file suit against a self-dealing trustee, in the school-trust case files the trustee's defensive briefs. Bogert's "appropriate public officer" is captured by definition.

The literature on charitable-enforcement institutional capture has been explicit about this for a quarter-century. Evelyn Brody's work on the structural under-enforcement of charitable trusts identifies AG capacity, AG attention, and AG independence as the three operating variables. The school-trust subset is the limit case — the case in which capacity and attention are both present and independence is structurally foreclosed.[^ch8-4] Susan Gary's mapping of state AG charitable-trust offices makes the same point from the inventory side.[^ch8-5] The literature treats school-trust enforcement as the structural anomaly inside charitable-trust enforcement, not as the central case.

A handful of states have built workarounds. Utah, after the 1983 corpus collapse and the citizen-organizing campaign that followed, created a statutory office: the Utah School Children's Trust office under Title 53D. The office's director represents the school beneficiary class as a fiduciary advocate inside state government. The statute confers standing to bring claims against the trustee agencies the director's office watches.[^ch8-6] New Mexico's Land Grant Permanent Fund Council carries elements of the same model on the investment side, with statutory advisory authority and a public-meeting record that documents the trustee's decisions.[^ch8-7] Wyoming's bifurcated trust-counsel structure separates the lawyers who represent the trustee in trust matters from the lawyers who represent the State in non-trust matters, opening at least the possibility of within-government adversariness on trust questions.[^ch8-8]

The states that have built workarounds are the exception. Oregon has not. Most of the original section-sixteen states have not. Most of the early-template states have not. The default in the country is the structure described above: AG works for trustee, trustee is the State, beneficiaries are an indefinite class of present and future schoolchildren, and *parens patriae* enforcement is captured.

The doctrinal apparatus, in those states, runs into a wall it cannot climb. The Restatement and Bogert presuppose AG independence. The school-trust context strips that independence away. What follows in the next section is the architecture the country has built around the wall — three substitute paths, each with cases, none of them a clean replacement for the captured AG, all of them load-bearing where they have worked.

## II. The substitute-enforcement architecture

Three paths carry the enforcement work that the captured AG cannot. The cases are few. The cases that have worked share institutional features the cases that have not lacked. The map is not pretty. It is the map.

### Path 1: Federal-court Supremacy Clause posture

The lead case is *Branson School District RE-82 v. Romer*, 161 F.3d 619 (10th Cir. 1998). Branson is a school district, a political subdivision of Colorado, and an arguable direct beneficiary of the section-sixteen and section-thirty-six grants under the Colorado Enabling Act. Branson sued the State of Colorado in federal court alleging that recent state legislation had breached the federal trust embodied in the Enabling Act. The Tenth Circuit held that Branson had standing and that the state legislation was preempted to the extent it conflicted with the federal trust language.[^ch8-9]

*Branson* worked because three institutional features lined up. The breach was discrete: a piece of state legislation enacted on a date, with effects measurable on a date. The plaintiff was a direct beneficiary in form recognizable to the federal courts: a school district, a political subdivision, an entity with budget and staff. The plaintiff had counsel — Mountain States Legal Foundation, a public-interest non-profit with a roster of attorneys experienced in Western public-lands litigation — operating as the substitute for the absent specialist trust bar.[^ch8-10] Take any of the three features away and *Branson* does not produce the precedent the country has been citing for a quarter-century.

The drift-pattern cases struggle on the federal-court Supremacy Clause path because the discrete-breach feature is missing. Oregon's school-trust drift between roughly 1990 and 2017 is not a single piece of legislation. It is a quarter-century of administrative practice — lease pricing decisions, accounting choices, fund-management discretions, sale-and-exchange transactions — that aggregate, over time, into a pattern of under-administration the architecture chapters describe. There is no Branson-style legislative date the federal court can point at and call the breach. The plaintiff who attempts a *Branson* suit on a drift pattern faces a federal forum trained to look for discrete federal acts and finds, instead, an administrative gradient.

The contemporary Oregon litigation, *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, 24CV38372 — the Coos County trial-court matter that the Oregon Court of Appeals heard in 2025–26 — is, doctrinally read, the plaintiff team's solution to the drift-pattern challenge. The vehicle is not a Supremacy Clause challenge to a piece of state legislation. It is a discovery-misconduct posture: a claim that the trustee, in defending the underlying drift suit, has failed to produce records the trustee was obliged to produce as a matter of fiduciary disclosure. A discovery failure is a discrete event with a date. The Motion to Compel filed May 2, 2026 puts the discrete-event question to Judge Stone.[^ch8-11] Whether the vehicle holds is a question the Oregon record will answer. As a matter of doctrinal architecture, the move is the textbook plaintiff-side response to the federal-court drift problem: when the breach you actually want to litigate is an administrative gradient, you find a discrete event inside the gradient and litigate that.

### Path 2: State-court taxpayer or organizational standing

The lead case is *Kadish v. Arizona State Land Department*, 155 Ariz. 484, 747 P.2d 1183 (1987), affirmed by the Supreme Court of the United States as *ASARCO Inc. v. Kadish*, 490 U.S. 605 (1989).[^ch8-12] Kadish — a taxpayer and a member of the Arizona Education Association — sued in Arizona state court challenging mineral-lease practices on Arizona school-trust lands as a breach of the federal trust under the 1910 Enabling Act. Arizona state court permitted the suit. The Arizona Supreme Court ruled on the merits. The Supreme Court of the United States affirmed, in an opinion holding that state courts may adjudicate federal trust-law questions presented by plaintiffs who would lack Article III standing in federal court.

The doctrinal force of *ASARCO v. Kadish* is the unbundling of standing from forum. Article III's case-or-controversy requirement applies in federal court. Most state courts apply analogous but not identical standing rules, and a number of states — Arizona, Montana, New Mexico, and a handful of others — apply notably more permissive rules for taxpayer and public-interest standing. A taxpayer or a teacher or a parent who would be turned away at the federal courthouse door under *Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife*, 504 U.S. 555 (1992), can be heard in the state courthouse on the same federal trust question. The state court's holding on the federal trust question, once final, becomes the kind of state-court precedent the Supreme Court of the United States can review on a writ of certiorari without ever importing Article III into the state proceeding.[^ch8-13]

*Forest Guardians v. Wells*, 201 Ariz. 255, 34 P.3d 364 (2001) `[VERIFY]`, extended the *Kadish* posture to a non-profit organizational plaintiff suing under the Arizona Enabling Act on a non-mineral-lease theory, holding that a public-interest non-profit organized around the trust subject matter has standing under Arizona's permissive state-law rules.[^ch8-14] *Montanans for the Responsible Use of the School Trust v. State* `[VERIFY citation]` is the strongest non-profit-standing precedent in the Western public-lands cohort outside Arizona — the Montana state courts granted preliminary and permanent injunctive relief against eleven Montana school-trust statutes, with attorney's-fees recovery under Montana's private-attorney-general doctrine, and the underlying organizational plaintiff was a non-profit organized to bring the suit.[^ch8-15] The case has not, as of this writing, received the doctrinal attention it deserves in the secondary literature.

The doctrinal vehicles the modern non-profit plaintiff pleads in the state-court posture are not new. *Hunt v. Washington State Apple Advertising Commission*, 432 U.S. 333 (1977), articulated the associational-standing test by which an organization sues on behalf of affected members.[^ch8-16] *Havens Realty Corp. v. Coleman*, 455 U.S. 363 (1982), articulated the organizational-standing theory by which an organization sues on its own behalf, on a theory of mission-frustration plus resource-diversion.[^ch8-17] The two doctrines are distinct but pleadable in tandem, and the modern school-trust non-profit plaintiff typically does plead them in tandem. *Hunt* gives the organization standing to represent its members' interests in a trust to which the members are beneficiaries. *Havens Realty* gives the organization standing to assert its own interest where the trustee's breach has frustrated the organization's mission and forced it to divert resources. Together, the two doctrines support the kind of dual-track pleading that the active state-court suits in Arizona, Montana, and Oregon have used.

The state-court path has limits. *Papasan v. Allain*, 478 U.S. 265 (1986), holds that the Eleventh Amendment forecloses retrospective monetary relief from a state treasury for school-trust restoration, even where the substantive trust claim is sound.[^ch8-18] *Edelman v. Jordan*, 415 U.S. 651 (1974), and *Ex parte Young*, 209 U.S. 123 (1908), together fix the sovereign-immunity framework that determines what relief a federal court can grant against state officers — prospective injunctive relief is available; retrospective monetary relief from the treasury is not.[^ch8-19] These are doctrinal ceilings on the most ambitious remedy theories. They are not ceilings on the basic capacity of state courts to adjudicate the underlying trust questions. A plaintiff who wants prospective injunctive relief and a declaration that the trustee has breached the trust can get both in a permissive-standing state court. A plaintiff who wants the State to write a check restoring the corpus to its pre-breach value runs into the Eleventh Amendment.

The state-court path also depends on the standing rules of the particular state. The plaintiff who can sue in Arizona or Montana cannot necessarily sue in Oregon or Wisconsin. The *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* line worked, in part, because Oregon voters added Article VIII § 8 to the state constitution by ballot initiative in 2000, creating a textual standing provision for Common School Fund beneficiaries that does not exist in most peer states.[^ch8-20] The state-court map, like the federal-trust-language map the architecture chapters describe, is uneven by jurisdiction. The plaintiff strategy is forum-dependent.

### Path 3: Federal-AG enforcement under the 1894/1910 enabling-act language

The lead case is *Ervien v. United States*, 251 U.S. 41 (1919). The United States, by and through the Attorney General, sued New Mexico's Commissioner of Public Lands for spending three percent of school-trust-land income on advertising the State's resources. Justice McKenna's opinion held that the trust purposes "must be regarded; they must be performed; they admit of no substitution; they admit of no addition."[^ch8-21] The decision is the only modern federal-AG enforcement action against a state for school-trust breach in the doctrinal record this chapter has been able to verify.

The case caption names the trustee personally. The defendant was Robert P. Ervien — not New Mexico in the abstract, not the State Land Office as an institution, but the Commissioner who had authorized the advertising spend. Period coverage in the *Santa Fe New Mexican* and the *Albuquerque Morning Journal* treated the suit as state-political news during the early Larrazolo administration. `[VERIFY: Larrazolo's relationship to the *Ervien* defense as governor 1919–1921; period-press treatment of Ervien-as-named-trustee.]` The named-trustee posture is the period-color piece Chapter 6 picks up; the doctrinal point here is that the federal Attorney General sued an individual state officer in his fiduciary capacity, and the Supreme Court treated the personal naming as proper. Section V.A's third anchor — individual trustee accountability — is the modern restatement of the same move.[^ch8-21a]

*Ervien* was 1919. The federal authority *Ervien* invoked — the express federal AG enforcement clause in § 10 of the New Mexico–Arizona Enabling Act of June 20, 1910, and the parallel clause in § 12 of the Utah Enabling Act of 1894 — has been operative every year since.[^ch8-22] The U.S. Attorney General can bring suit, in the United States' name, against any state holding lands under the 1894 or 1910 enabling-act language for a breach of the federal trust. No further enabling action is needed. No statute of limitations is named in the text. No case has held the authority extinguished or dormant by disuse.

The dormancy is institutional, not doctrinal. The secondary literature surveyed for this chapter has not located a modern federal-AG enforcement action against a state under § 10 or § 12 in the century-plus since *Ervien*.[^ch8-23] `[VERIFY]` The reasons for the dormancy are not the subject of this chapter. The fact of the dormancy is. The federal executive, across administrations of both parties for a hundred and seven years, has not invoked the authority *Ervien* validated. The authority remains. The political demand to invoke it has not.

This is a finding about constituency, not about doctrine. The authority is operative; what is missing is the constituency capable of generating the political demand to activate it. The eighth anchor section of this chapter takes up the constituency question directly.

## III. The bar-economics asymmetry

The three paths the prior section maps share an institutional feature the secondary literature has been slow to name. The plaintiff's lawyers, in every successful school-trust enforcement case in the modern record, have been non-profit lawyers. Not contingent-fee tort lawyers. Not the structural-reform civil-rights bar in its Title VII or § 1983 form. The school-trust plaintiff bar, where it exists, is the public-interest non-profit bar — and where it exists, it exists because a particular non-profit organized to bring the suit and staffed the case from its own institutional resources.

The economics tell most of the story. Equity reform of multi-decade administrative practice — which is what school-trust enforcement is, in most states — does not generate the discrete monetary recoveries that fund contingent-fee practice. A *Branson*-style declaration that a piece of state legislation is preempted produces a remedy. The remedy is prospective. The trust gets administered differently going forward. The plaintiff's law firm gets a fee award if the statute provides one and the court finds the case fee-shiftable; otherwise the law firm gets the satisfaction of the precedent and not much else. A contingent-fee practice cannot survive on satisfaction. The contingent-fee bar's absence from school-trust enforcement is a feature of how the United States legal market is organized, not a moral failing of the contingent-fee bar.

The structural-reform civil-rights bar — the bar that does work the school-trust subject matter requires, in the form of multi-year injunctive practice supported by foundation grants and institutional retainers — has historically not owned public-lands trust law as part of its subject-matter portfolio. The reasons are several. The affected states are geographically dispersed. The pre-existing precedents are sparse. The doctrine layers trust law on top of public-lands law on top of state constitutional law. And no *Brown v. Board* or *Roe* style doctrinal anchor exists around which a national plaintiff strategy could organize. The structural-reform bar is in the country. It is not, in any sustained way, in the school-trust enforcement business.

The substitute is the issue-organized public-interest non-profit. The historical record is unanimous on this. Mountain States Legal Foundation organized the *Branson* litigation. The Tenth Circuit's 1998 Supremacy Clause precedent does not exist without the organization's plaintiff-side staffing.[^ch8-24] The Arizona Education Association supplied the institutional muscle behind *Kadish*, and the named plaintiff Kadish was a member of the AEA. The 1987 Arizona Supreme Court decision and the 1989 Supreme Court affirmance both rest on AEA staff, AEA counsel, and AEA institutional staying-power across the multi-year litigation arc.[^ch8-25] Forest Guardians was the named non-profit in *Forest Guardians v. Wells*; the 2001 Arizona Supreme Court decision is, in the case caption, the organization's name.[^ch8-26] Montanans for the Responsible Use of the School Trust was the named non-profit in the Montana injunctive-relief line. The organization itself was the substitute for the absent specialist trust bar.[^ch8-27] The Oregon Association of School Trust Lands and the Association for State Trust Lands are the named non-profits supplying the institutional substrate behind *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, 24CV38372. The case does not exist without the two organizations' staffing.[^ch8-28]

The historical record is unanimous. No state's school-trust enforcement architecture has ever functioned without a non-profit substitute for the absent specialist bar. There is no exception in the modern record. There is no state in which the State Attorney General, acting independently as *parens patriae*, has produced the enforcement output the doctrine assumes the AG would produce. There is no state in which the contingent-fee tort bar, acting on its own institutional incentives, has produced school-trust enforcement litigation. There is no state in which a structural-reform civil-rights bar has organized around school-trust enforcement as a subject-matter specialty independent of a non-profit plaintiff. Every successful suit, in every state where there has been a successful suit, has had a public-interest non-profit at the center of the plaintiff team.

The point is structural rather than blame-assigning. The bar-economics asymmetry is not a moral failing of any of the three bars whose absence the asymmetry describes. It is a feature of how the United States legal market organizes plaintiff-side litigation around revenue. School-trust enforcement does not generate the revenue that funds contingent-fee work. School-trust enforcement does not fit the doctrinal portfolio that funds the structural-reform bar. The work has to be funded another way. The non-profit substitute is how it gets funded. The doctrinal map and the operational map line up on this point in a way that few other points in the school-trust subject matter line up.

## IV. The eighth anchor as doctrinal remedy

Margaret Bird's reframe of the eighth anchor on May 5, 2026 made the doctrinal claim of this chapter directly. The Library — the institutional infrastructure of watchfulness, the constituency of co-librarians and named editors and standing readers — *is* the eighth anchor as it exists today.[^ch8-29] The earlier framing, which treated the eighth anchor as a parcel-level public ledger to be built, was a description of one possible deliverable. The reframe identifies the actual institutional fact: the Library, the constituency it convenes, and the standing watchfulness it produces are what enforcement requires, and what the seven structural anchors require to operate.

This is a doctrinal claim, not a moral supplement to the seven anchors. The seven structural anchors — the section-grant template, the in-trust language, the products-and-proceeds reach, the breach-of-trust naming, the null-and-void rule for non-conforming dispositions, the federal-AG enforcement clause where it exists, the constitutional state-trustee architecture — are necessary. The bar-economics asymmetry described in Section III shows that they are not sufficient. The doctrinal apparatus the architecture chapters describe presupposes an enforcer the school-trust context does not supply. The eighth anchor is the substitute enforcer.

More precisely: the eighth anchor is the institutional infrastructure that lets the substitute enforcer form. The non-profit plaintiff that staffs *Branson* or *Kadish* or *Forest Guardians* or *Advocates for School Trust Lands* does not appear from nothing. It forms because a constituency exists. The constituency is composed of citizens, educators, beneficiary-class members, and specialist scholars. Together they generate the institutional demand for the non-profit. They fund it, they staff it, and they supply the named plaintiffs the non-profit's caption requires. The Library, in the framing this chapter adopts, is the institutional infrastructure that lets the constituency form and operate. It is the standing place where the watchfulness happens, where the substrate gets maintained, where the named editors and co-librarians and readers do the slow work of keeping the trust legible to itself and to the public.

The chapter's argument has, to this point, named the eighth anchor as a doctrinal fact and described what the constituency does once it exists. It has not yet named the mechanism by which the constituency gets *built* — by which a watchful crew of thousands, capable of generating the institutional demand the non-profit substitute requires, comes into operation in a state where it did not exist the year before. The mechanism is not theoretical. Margaret Bird has stated it operationally, drawn from the Utah reform she helped run, and the chapter records her statement in her own voice because no abstraction summarizes the procedure as cleanly:

The mechanism is structural rather than exhortatory. Routing the trust's income directly to the building level — to the school where the parents, the teachers, and the principal are the people deciding how the money is implemented — converts the beneficiary class from an abstract category into a network of concrete decision-makers. The Utah figure is the operative scale: approximately 9,000 Utahns sit on the school community councils that govern the per-school distributions of the Utah School LAND Trust Program, and each of them, on Margaret's account, goes home and discusses at the Sunday dinner what is happening at the local school. The word spreads fast. The constituency follows the dollars to the table. What the chapter has been calling the substitute architecture — the non-profit, the named editor, the standing reader — assembles itself, in any state with the right operational design, around the rooms where the trust's income is being deliberated over by the people whose children are in the building. That is the mechanism by which the eighth anchor is populated.[^ch8-29b]

The Sacred Compact's V.H section names this directly. The counter-architecture the Compact describes is not a single act of legislation or litigation. It is the slow, patient, multi-decade work of holding the trust to its temporal frame — a forever gift, to forever schools, for a forever democracy.[^ch8-30] The Compact's VII section, on civic practice, walks through the operational requirements of the work: the Library as the institutional location, the co-librarians as the staffing, the named editors as the editorial discipline, the standing readership as the constituency in its public form. None of this is poetry. All of it is the institutional substrate that produces the non-profit plaintiff that staffs the suit that enforces the trust the architecture chapters describe.

The doctrinal claim, stated plainly: constituency-as-substitute-for-captured-AG-and-absent-specialist-bar. The Library is the institutional infrastructure that makes constituency-as-substitute possible. This is not the claim that the Library is the legal enforcer. The Library does not file briefs. The Library convenes the constituency that funds the non-profit that retains the lawyers that file the briefs. The chain is mediated. The doctrinal point is that the chain has to begin somewhere, and in the school-trust context the only beginning the historical record validates is the constituency. Architecture does not enforce itself. The captured AG does not enforce. The contingent-fee bar does not enforce. The structural-reform bar, on this subject matter, does not enforce. The non-profit does, when the constituency that creates the non-profit exists. The Library's job is to be the place where the constituency exists.

Margaret's framing, then, is the doctrinal capstone of the substitute-enforcement architecture. The eighth anchor is not a deliverable to be built. It is the institutional fact that makes the seven anchors enforceable. The book identifies it as such throughout — and this chapter, on the doctrinal record, names the identification doctrinal rather than rhetorical.

## V. Strategic landscape and what the next decade requires

The synthesis the rest of this chapter has worked from suggests four things about the next decade.

First, drift-pattern cases require transactional anchors. Multi-decade administrative drift is not litigable as a multi-decade administrative gradient. It has to be litigated through a discrete event inside the gradient — a particular lease, a particular accounting period, a particular discovery failure. *Branson* worked because the breach was a piece of legislation with a date. *24CV38372* works, if it works, because the breach is a discovery failure with a date. Future drift-pattern plaintiffs will face the same configuration, in Oregon and elsewhere, and the strategic challenge will be the construction of the discrete transactional anchor that converts the gradient into a justiciable event.

The historical floor of this point is Alabama. *State v. Schmidt* (Alabama 1913, aff'd by the U.S. Supreme Court in 1914) `[VERIFY full cite]` held that adverse-possession statutes could run against sixteenth-section school lands in Talladega County. Adverse possession is, in transactional-anchor terms, the slowest possible kind: the breach matures over the statutory period, with the last day of the period as the date the transactional anchor finally crystallizes. *Schmidt* is what the modern Counter-Architecture — the seven structural anchors of the *Sacred Compact* and the eighth-anchor framing this chapter develops — was built to address. A trust whose only enforcement anchor is the close of an adverse-possession period has no operational defense in the years between. Chapter 6 treats *Schmidt* as the doctrinal retreat that named the gap; this chapter's strategic point is that modern drift-pattern litigation needs faster anchors than *Schmidt* allows.[^ch8-29a]

Second, plaintiff-team configurations differ by state. Permissive-standing states — Arizona, Montana, New Mexico, and a handful of others — support state-court taxpayer and non-profit posture under the *Kadish* line. Restrictive-standing states require either the *Branson*-style federal Supremacy Clause posture, with its discrete-breach precondition, or the construction of a state constitutional standing provision of the kind Oregon voters added in 2000. The strategic map for school-trust enforcement is not single-template. It is jurisdiction by jurisdiction.

Third, the federal-AG enforcement option could be revived if the political demand existed. The 1894 Utah Enabling Act § 12 and the 1910 New Mexico–Arizona Enabling Act § 10 contain operative federal AG enforcement clauses. *Ervien* (1919) confirms that the clauses are usable. No further enabling action is required to bring a modern federal-AG suit against a state for school-trust breach under the 1894 or 1910 language. What is required is the political demand for the executive to invoke the authority — and the constituency capable of generating that demand. Building that demand is part of the constituency's work. The Library is, on the framing this chapter has adopted, the institutional infrastructure that makes building the demand possible.

Fourth, the constituency itself is the active variable. The architecture is built. The federal text is, in the 1894 and 1910 states, as strong as Congress ever wrote it. The state-side constitutional architecture exists, in varying forms, in most of the public-lands states. The doctrinal apparatus is laid in across Bogert, the Restatement (Third), and the Uniform Trust Code. What has been intermittent is the constituency that activates the apparatus. Where the constituency has been present — Utah after 1989, Colorado after Amendment 16 in 1996, Minnesota after the 2012 statutory reforms, Oregon since the founding of OASTL — the architecture has produced enforcement output. Where the constituency has been absent, the architecture has drifted.

The chapter's editorial frame, stated plainly: the trust is enforceable, but only when the substitute architecture exists. The substitute architecture is what the Library and the standing constituency are building. It is not yet built everywhere. It is being built in the places this book has documented. Whether it gets built in the rest of the country is the question the next decade will answer, and the work of that decade is the work the constituency does in its slow, patient, institutional form. The doctrine is settled. The architecture is settled. The active variable, after two centuries, is what it has always been — the constituency capable of standing in for the captured AG and the absent specialist bar, and the institutional infrastructure that lets the constituency form. That is the eighth anchor as Margaret has framed it. That is what the rest of this book takes up.
