# Chapter 2 — The First FOIA

> *"The Oregon form of government requires an informed public aware of the deliberations and decisions of governing bodies and the information upon which such decisions were made."*
>
> — ORS 192.620 (Oregon Public Meetings Law, statement of policy)

I want to walk you through what it is like to open an envelope from a state agency at your own kitchen table and read, on the agency's letterhead, a sentence about a place you have stood in that you know is not true.

This is going to sound, at first, like a small thing. It is. It happened in early 2020, at our kitchen table in Kings Valley, which sits at the end of a gravel road about twenty miles northwest of Corvallis. I was at that point a retired professor of business, sixty-something, two years into an effort to figure out what was happening to the Elliott State Forest. The envelope was thicker than I had expected. The contents were thinner.

It is also, on the inside, not a small thing. It is the moment I stopped being a curious citizen who occasionally went to Land Board meetings, and started being whatever I have been since.

---

I had spent the previous year asking around. I had read most of Jerry Phillips's *Caulked Boots and Cheese Sandwiches* — the Elliott's history written by the man who had managed the District for thirty-three years and brought $790 million to the Common School Fund off of those acres — and I had walked the Elliott in the company of people who knew the ground. I had talked to retired Department of Forestry foresters who had run timber sales on the Elliott in the 1990s. I had read the State Land Board minutes I could find. I had been to a public meeting at which the Department of State Lands explained, on a series of cheerful PowerPoint slides, that the Elliott was transitioning into a "research forest" and that the College of Forestry at Oregon State University was the institution that would run it. I had nodded along with everyone else in the room, the way one does at agency meetings, and gone home and made notes.

The notes were not adding up.

The slides talked about research. The slides talked about ecology. The slides did not say where the timber revenue had gone, what the trust corpus was worth, who had signed off on the change, or when. The slides did not name the legal mechanism by which a Common School Fund timber asset was supposed to be removed from its trust status without compensation to the trust. When I asked a polite version of those questions in the Q&A, I got a polite non-answer. When I asked the longer version of the same questions in an email afterward, I got no answer.

I knew, by then, the name of the statute that was supposed to handle this kind of moment. Oregon's Public Records Law lives in the Oregon Revised Statutes, sections 192.311 through 192.478. Its central rule is one a high-school civics class can state in a sentence: records held by a public body are presumed to be public, and the burden is on the agency to justify withholding them. I drafted a written request to the Department of State Lands, in plain language, on a single sheet of paper. I asked for five categories of documents. The financial statements of the Elliott — what was coming in, what was going out, what the trust was earning. The written management plans then in effect. The professional timber cruises that any commercial forester would have on file as a matter of routine. The schedules and locations of the Science Advisory Panel meetings. And the underlying data models that were supporting the College of Forestry's research-forest proposal. I signed it, I dated it, I mailed it.

I made a copy of the letter and stuck it on the corkboard above my desk so I would not lose track.

This was, looking back, the first formal public-records request I had ever made. I had been writing textbooks and teaching for forty years. I had been to court exactly once, as a witness in a property dispute. I had filed a tax return. I had never, in any other context, asked a state agency for documents under a statute. I assumed, the way most people would assume, that the agency would either produce the documents or, where they could not, tell me which statutory exemption they were relying on and why. That is what the law says they are supposed to do.

The envelope, when it came, was thicker than the letter I had sent. The first sheet inside was the agency's response.

---

I sat at the kitchen table and read it.

I read it twice.

I want to be precise about what I had expected and what I had not. I had expected the agency to push back on details. I had expected the agency to invoke an exemption or two and to redact some lines and to ask me to be patient on a few items. I had expected, on the financial statements, some hedging about which figures were preliminary and which were settled. I had expected, on the management plans, a comment that revisions were in progress. What I had not expected — what I had spent forty years of teaching never to expect from any institution, let alone a state trustee — was the agency to deny the basic fact.

The sheet on the table in front of me said, in essence, that no harvesting was occurring on the Elliott State Forest. The phrase I have carried around in my head ever since, the phrase that became the shorthand for the whole thing, is *no harvesting whatsoever*. Whether those are the agency's exact three words I am, at this date, no longer certain — the original paper went into a file box, and the file box has been moved more than once in five years — but the assertion in the response was clear, declarative, and on agency letterhead. There was no commercial harvesting underway on the Elliott. That was the State's answer.

I read it a third time, this time slowly, looking for the qualifier I must have missed. There was no qualifier.

I sat at the kitchen table for a while.

I want to give you the picture as honestly as I can. I was not angry, at first. I was confused. The Elliott had been a working timber forest for ninety years. It was the working timber asset of the Common School Fund — the federal land grant by which Oregon entered the Union in 1859, the foundation of the financial promise to Oregon's schoolchildren. Jerry Phillips had written a four-hundred-page book about the timber operations he had managed there. There were active sale records in the Department of Forestry's own files going back decades. The Department of State Lands itself had, on its own website, harvest-volume figures from the immediately preceding fiscal years. I had stood in the company of foresters at the edge of a cut block in the West Fork Millicoma drainage. The forest was being harvested. To say otherwise was not a fine distinction or a definitional argument. It was simply not the case.

When the confusion settled, what was left was the recognition that I had asked the wrong question of the situation. I had been asking, *What is the State doing to the Elliott?* I had assumed that the State would explain it, in agency English, on a sheet of paper. The agency was not going to explain it on a sheet of paper. The agency was going to say, on a sheet of paper, things that were not so, because the things that were so could not be defended in writing. That was the answer to the question I had asked. The answer was not the harvest figure or the management plan or the panel schedule. The answer was the sentence on the page.

I had spent fifteen years of my early career inside a company that did not believe the market was about to take its product line away, until the market did. I had spent the years since teaching graduate students to recognize the pattern. The pattern is institutional. It is not, usually, that anyone inside the institution lies on purpose. The pattern is that the institution has decided, somewhere upstream, what it is going to do, and the documents at the desk-level have to be made consistent with that decision after the fact. The documents start to drift away from the territory. The territory is what it is. The documents are what the institution can defend.

The sentence on my kitchen table was the kind of sentence an institution produces when the territory and the documents have separated.

---

What I knew about Oregon's Public Records Law in early 2020 was less than I knew six months later, when I had sat on the floor of my study with the statute book open and read the whole chapter twice. The short version is on the next page of this book, in a panel I have asked the publisher to box off so a reader who already knows this can skip it. What I knew at the kitchen table was the headline: the statute, in plain language, presumes records are public unless the agency carries the burden of showing they are not.

The agency in my case had not invoked an exemption. The agency had produced a sentence about the underlying fact. There is no exemption for that. There is, in the Public Records Law, a remedy for the case where an agency refuses to produce a record — you ask the Attorney General to order production, or you sue. There is no easy remedy in the Public Records Law for the case where the agency produces a sheet of paper and the sheet of paper contains an assertion the requester knows to be false. The Public Records Law presupposes that the agency is, in good faith, answering questions about its records. It does not presuppose that the agency is going to answer questions about the world.

I learned, sitting at that table, that the Public Records Law is a strong sword if the agency forgets to argue. It is a weaker sword if the agency simply states a different version of reality and waits for the citizen to give up.

This was, on the evidence, the State's working assumption.

I decided, on the evidence, not to give up.

---

I want to mark this moment because everything that came after — the lawsuits, the Cougar Pass crawl, the secret-meetings essay, the standing victory, the lead-plaintiff seat — flows from a small decision I made at the kitchen table in early 2020. The decision was to put the agency's own documents, alongside the world they referred to, on a page that anyone could read.

I went to my study after Barb went to bed. I bought a domain name. The name I bought was *ElliottSecrets.net*. I wrote a placeholder page that night and started, over the next several weeks, to build out what eventually became eight categories — Meetings, History, OSU, DSL, Lookouts, Fires, Roads, Financial. On each page I planned to put two columns. In the left column would be what the agency said, in the agency's own words, with the agency's letterhead, on the agency's response sheets. In the right column would be what I could document, from primary sources, was the case. The visitor to the site would be able to compare. The visitor would not have to take my word for any of it. The documents would be the witnesses.

The website went up in May 2020. The first published page was the home page, which I dated May 16. The DSL Secrets page, which is where the records-request material first appeared, went up about two weeks later. I dedicated the site, on the home page, to my father — J. Wesley Sullivan, who had edited the Salem *Statesman-Journal* in the years I was growing up and had served on the State Board of Forestry — because I knew what he would have done with the sentence on my kitchen table. He would have run it on his front page with the underlying facts in the same column.

I did not know, in May of 2020, that the website would still be archived on the Internet's Wayback Machine six years later as a primary source for a lawsuit in which I was the lead plaintiff. I did not know the lawsuit would happen. I did not know I would meet Margaret Bird. I did not know David Gould would die before the case was over. I did not know what Cougar Pass Lookout would look like from the ground after Bob Zybach and Barb and I crawled over the rotting roads to see what the State had let happen to it. I knew, only, that the agency had asked me to take its word against my own eyes, and I had decided that was a request I would not honor.

That is the small decision Chapter 2 is about. The rest of the book is what followed.

#### How Oregon's Public Records Law Works

*Skip if you know this.*

Oregon's Public Records Law lives in the Oregon Revised Statutes, sections 192.311 through 192.478. It is the law that makes Oregon's state agencies, in principle, accountable to the people who pay for them. The basic architecture is simple enough to fit on a postcard, and I am going to put it on a postcard for you.

First: in Oregon, the default is disclosure. A record held by a public body — the Department of State Lands, the State Land Board, an OSU dean's office acting as part of a state planning process — is presumed to be available to any person who asks for it. The agency does not have to be persuaded that the request is important, or that the requester is a journalist, or that the records will be used in a particular way. The records, by default, are public.

Second: the burden of withholding is on the agency. If a state body refuses to produce a record, the body must point to a specific statutory exemption — there is a list of them, in ORS 192.345 and 192.355 — and explain why the exemption applies. The agency cannot withhold a record because it would be embarrassing, because it would complicate a planning process, or because the agency would rather not. The agency must name the exemption and meet its burden.

Third: there is a remedy when the agency refuses. A requester whose request is denied can appeal to the Attorney General (for state-agency records) or to the District Attorney (for local-body records). If the appeal succeeds, the AG or DA orders production. If the agency still refuses, the requester can sue and recover attorney's fees.

Fourth: the records can be reviewed only on grounds the statute names. The statute does not authorize the agency to decide that the requester has the wrong motive or is asking for the wrong reasons. The agency's only legitimate question is whether the record is exempt.

In plain English: under Oregon's Public Records Law, when you ask the State for documents, the State has to give them to you unless the State can show, under a specific provision, that it does not. The State does not have a generalized right to keep its work product to itself. The public has a presumptive right to read what the public has paid the State to produce.

That is the law. Whether the law is followed is, as the next several chapters illustrate, a different question.

</aside>
