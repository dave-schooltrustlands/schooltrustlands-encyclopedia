# Chapter 7 — The 2022 OSU Plan and the "Children's Forest"

*Working draft v1, 2026-05-10. ~3,200 words target. Dave-first-person body, named actors, dated events, dollar figures. The most evidence-heavy chapter of the book — the chapter where the documentary record is most explicit about what the parties knew, when they knew it, and what they did anyway.*

---

I want to take this chapter slowly because the documentary record on it is the strongest record in the book. If you read no other chapter, read this one. The story I am about to walk you through is not a story about people who failed to understand what they were doing. It is a story about people who understood exactly what they were doing, who put their understanding in writing, who continued anyway, and who marketed the move under a name — *Children's Forest* — that used the beneficiaries of the trust as the cover story for the conversion that was taking the asset away from them.

The chapter has eight sections. I am going to walk through them in the order in which the events unfolded, because the order itself is part of the case.

## I. The setup

Chapter 6 ended with the 2019 bond substitution incomplete. The State had issued $100 million in general-obligation bonds and deposited the proceeds into the Common School Fund as a partial replacement for the Elliott's reduced revenue. The Elliott itself, as a parcel of trust-owned land, was still on the State Land Board's books. What was missing, from the Department of State Lands' point of view, was the formal step that would move the Elliott out of its existing legal status — Common School Fund grant land managed for trust revenue — and into a new legal status that would honor the management posture the agencies had been operating under for thirty years.

That step required legislation. The trust character of the Elliott is set by the Oregon Constitution, Article VIII, Sections 2, 5, and 8, and by the underlying federal grant in the 1859 Admissions Act. The Land Board cannot, by its own action, convert a CSF land parcel into a non-CSF land parcel. The Legislature cannot, by a simple statute, override a constitutional trust. What the Legislature can do is *create a vehicle* — a new entity, a new statutory designation, a new legal structure — that takes title to the land while the State Land Board takes its name off the books, with the formal transfer accomplished through a transaction that the Legislature characterizes as a sale or a transfer with compensation to the trust.

That is what happened in 2022.

## II. House Bill 3119

The Oregon Legislature passed House Bill 3119 in the 2021–2022 session. The bill created a new public corporation — the Elliott State Research Forest Authority — and authorized the State Land Board to transfer the Elliott to the Authority for management as a research forest by Oregon State University. The bill provided for the issuance of additional bonds to compensate the Common School Fund for the transferred asset. The bill set out the governance structure for the Authority: a board appointed by the Governor, with seats reserved for OSU, for the Department of State Lands, for the Confederated Tribes, for the Coos County local community, and for environmental and timber industry interests. The bill set the legal mechanism through which the Elliott would cease to be CSF grant land and become research forest land in a new legal category.

I want to pause and name what HB 3119 did in plain English, because the legalistic surface of the bill makes it easy to read past the underlying move. The bill took the asset out of the trust. It transferred the legal title from the State Land Board, which holds CSF lands on behalf of the schoolchildren, to a new entity that does not hold the land on behalf of the schoolchildren. The bill compensated the trust for the transfer with bond proceeds and future appropriations. The bill renamed the asset's purpose from *generating timber revenue for the Common School Fund* to *serving as a research forest for Oregon State University.*

The compensation was structurally identical to the 2019 substitution, and substantially larger. The 2019 bonds had been $100 million. The 2022 plan contemplated additional bond issuances on the order of $121 million, plus future appropriations, plus federal grants the Authority would pursue, plus revenue the research forest would generate from limited timber operations. The total package was presented to the Legislature as making the Common School Fund whole.

You should hold the structure in mind. *The trust gives up the asset. The State gives the trust bonds that the State will pay back from general revenue. The trust receives, in exchange for a working timber forest worth somewhere between one billion and one and three-tenths billion dollars, a series of bond proceeds and future appropriations whose total value over their term is some fraction of the asset's market value, secured by no asset other than the State's promise to honor the appropriations.*

The bill passed. Governor Tina Kotek's predecessor, Governor Kate Brown, had championed the framework before leaving office. Governor Kotek inherited it and continued it. The State Treasurer, Tobias Read, supported it. The Secretary of State did not block it. The bill became law.

## III. The "Children's Forest" rebrand

Now I want to take up the marketing of the bill, because the marketing is itself part of the documentary case.

Beginning in 2021 and accelerating through the 2022 legislative session, the Department of State Lands, the OSU College of Forestry, and the supporting coalition of conservation groups began referring to the Elliott as *the Children's Forest*. The phrase appeared in press releases, in public meeting materials, on websites, in legislative testimony, and in school-district outreach. The framing was that the Elliott, in becoming a research forest, would also become a forest dedicated to the children of Oregon — accessible for school field trips, for outdoor education programs, for the kind of environmental learning that would serve the children of the state in their educational lives.

I want you to sit with the marketing move for a moment.

The Elliott had been the Common School Fund's working asset since the federal grant in 1859. Its purpose, in trust law and in the Oregon Constitution, had been to produce revenue for the schoolchildren of Oregon. The schoolchildren were the beneficiaries. The forest was held in trust *for* them. The trust's name — *Common School Fund* — included the word *school* because the school children were who the trust existed to serve.

The conversion the 2022 plan accomplished took the asset away from those schoolchildren. It removed the asset from the trust that named them as beneficiaries. It replaced the asset's revenue stream with bond proceeds whose value was a small fraction of the asset's market value. It assigned management of the asset to a state university whose mission was not the schoolchildren's educational funding.

And the conversion was marketed, in the press materials and the public-facing communications and the testimony to the Legislature, under the name *Children's Forest*.

David Gould, who would become a co-plaintiff in the OASTL litigation that this book is about, wrote to the North Bend and Powers school boards on February 11, 2022, asking for their formal approval of his legal action and characterizing the move as what it was: *"The Elliott State Research Forest is a TAKE of promised Common School Funds for the Elliott State Children's Forest established in 1930."* Gould had grown up in Coos County. His family had homesteaded the Elkhorn Ranch in the 1880s. He understood what the Elliott had been since 1930 — the year the federal land transfer that established the Elliott in its modern form was completed — and what the 2022 plan was doing to it. The school boards, both of them, voted to support the action.

On March 3, 2022, after speaking to the Buster Club at the North Bend High School ball-team introduction, Gould wrote what for my money is the cleanest articulation of what the marketing accomplished: *"As I expected the students did not know they own 43,000 acres in their Elliott State Children's Forest."* The students did not know. Not because they were uninterested, but because the institutional architecture in which the Elliott was their asset had been operated, for decades, without their participation, and the rebrand of the asset they did not know they owned into a Children's Forest they were told would serve them was the marketing that capped the operation.

## IV. The OSU $2.1 million admission

I want to walk you, slowly and carefully, through the single most damning document in the 2022 record.

On April 14, 2021, the Oregon State University College of Forestry submitted its Elliott State Research Forest Proposal — the *ESRF Proposal* — to the Department of State Lands. The document was the formal OSU pitch for taking the Elliott as a research forest under what would become HB 3119. The Proposal ran to several hundred pages. The Proposal had been prepared over a period of approximately eighteen months under the leadership of OSU College of Forestry Interim Dean Anthony S. Davis, with OSU President F. King Alexander's institutional sponsorship and, after Alexander's resignation in early 2021, with President Becky Johnson's caretaker support pending the arrival of incoming President Jayathi Murthy in fall 2022.

The Proposal, in its financial section, contained a passage that I will quote because the passage is the documentary heart of this chapter. The OSU College of Forestry, evaluating its own plan for the Elliott State Research Forest under the financial models it had developed, concluded that the plan would produce *a $2.1 million deficit on an average annual basis for the first fifty years.*

Read that one more time. *A $2.1 million deficit on an average annual basis for the first fifty years.* On the average. For fifty years. In OSU's own modeling.

What it means, in concrete terms, is that OSU itself, in the document it submitted to the State Land Board to make the case for OSU taking the Elliott as a research forest, told the State Land Board that the operation would lose money every year on average for half a century. Not occasionally. Not as a one-time startup cost. As an average annual condition of the operation, over the entire fifty-year planning horizon.

The figure was disclosed in December 2021 in OSU's own documentation. The legislature voted on HB 3119 in early 2022 with the figure in the record. The State Land Board endorsed the transfer with the figure in the record. The Governor's office endorsed the transfer with the figure in the record. None of the parties involved was operating in the dark. They knew. They made the plan anyway.

I want to stop and explain why this matters, in trust-doctrine terms, because the moral content of the move and the legal content are the same.

A trustee may not transfer trust property to a manager whom the trustee knows will lose money on the property. That is not a sophisticated proposition. It is the simplest application of the duty of prudence. A reasonable trustee, with $1.3 billion in fair-market value of trust property under management, does not transfer the property to a manager whose own financial modeling projects that the manager will lose $2.1 million a year on the property for fifty years. The 2022 transfer did not comply with the duty of prudence on its face. The non-compliance was disclosed in writing, in OSU's own document, before the transfer.

The trustee — the State Land Board — endorsed the transfer anyway. The Legislature authorized the transfer anyway. The bonds were structured to pay the projected deficit, year after year, out of general revenue.

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember this. *Oregon State University, asked to take the Elliott from the schoolchildren, agreed in 2022 — and Oregon State University's own documentation said the plan would lose $2.1 million a year for fifty years. They knew, and they agreed anyway.*

## V. Jerry Franklin's peer review

The financial documentation was not the only document in the 2022 record that condemned the OSU plan. The scientific documentation did the same work.

Jerry Franklin is the dean of Pacific Northwest forest ecology. His career, beginning at the OSU College of Forestry in the 1960s and continuing through his long tenure at the University of Washington, defines the field of how Pacific Northwest mature-forest ecology is studied and what counts as rigorous experimental design in it. When the OSU ESRF Proposal was put out for technical peer review, Franklin was the obvious external reviewer to consult. He wrote what became Appendix 13 of the ESRF Proposal record.

I will not reproduce the entire Appendix 13 review here, because the technical detail matters more than the rhetorical force, but I will summarize what Franklin concluded. He concluded that the experimental design in the OSU ESRF Proposal did not meet the standards of forest research. The design's structure — the way the experimental treatments were assigned to forest stands, the way the control treatments were defined, the way the statistical power was justified — did not, in Franklin's judgment, support the inferences the OSU plan claimed it would support. The plan was not, by the standards of the field's most authoritative figure, a research forest plan. It was, in Franklin's reading, a management plan dressed in research vocabulary.

I want to be careful here, because I am not a forest ecologist and I am not a peer reviewer in the field. What I am reporting is what the field's most authoritative figure put in writing, in a formal peer review attached to the proposal that was before the Legislature. The plan that HB 3119 authorized was a plan that the dean of the field did not regard as scientifically valid. The Legislature voted on it anyway.

Franklin's review is in the documentary record under `L0_Primary_Sources/Research_Reports/ESRF/`, attached as Appendix 13 to the April 14, 2021 ESRF Proposal. The review is short and direct. It is worth reading in full if you want to see what the documentary record actually said about the experimental design the plan claimed to support.

The pattern, by mid-2022, was visible to anyone willing to look. The financial documentation said the plan would lose money for fifty years. The scientific documentation said the research design did not meet field standards. The trust-doctrine reading said the transfer breached the duty of prudence. The State Land Board, the Legislature, and the Governor's office endorsed the plan anyway.

## VI. The Huntington memo

On October 13, 2023, an internal Governor's-office memo from Geoff Huntington, a senior policy advisor to Governor Kotek, made the political character of the plan explicit. The memo conceded — in the kind of plain language internal memos sometimes carry that public-facing testimony does not — that the Elliott State Research Forest plan had been designed to meet political objectives, not fiduciary obligations to the schools.

I want to be careful about how I report this, because the Huntington memo is one of the most important documents in the OASTL litigation's documentary record and I do not want to overstate it. The memo is summarized on the OASTL.org legal-resources page, which characterizes the document as *"Policy memo acknowledging that the Elliott State Research Forest plan was designed to meet political objectives, not fiduciary obligations to the schools."* The full text of the memo is in the litigation record. The memo is itself an internal Governor's-office document; it was not designed for public release; it surfaced through the kinds of records-process pressure that public-records advocacy and litigation discovery produce.

What the memo does, when you read it against the public-facing testimony from the same period, is to confirm what the financial and scientific documentation had already implied. The plan was political. The plan was designed to resolve the political problem the Elliott had become for the Land Board after 2017, not to maximize the trust's financial contribution for the beneficiaries. The plan met the political objective. The plan did not meet the fiduciary obligation. The Huntington memo, in October 2023, said so in the kind of language internal memos use when they are not being written for the public.

That memo is the kind of document that, in trust-doctrine terms, settles the duty-of-loyalty question. A trustee who designs a trust transaction to meet political objectives rather than fiduciary obligations to the beneficiaries has not administered the trust. The trustee has used the trust. The Huntington memo is the internal record of the using.

## VII. The Murthy withdrawal letter that did not withdraw

One month after Geoff Huntington's October 13, 2023 memo, on November 13, 2023, Oregon State University President Jayathi Murthy wrote a letter to the Department of State Lands withdrawing OSU from the Elliott State Research Forest project. The letter is in the documentary record under `L0_Primary_Sources/Correspondence/OSU_Murthy_ESRF_2023-11-13.pdf`. The text of the letter is brief and decisive. OSU was withdrawing as the management entity for the ESRF.

The Confederated Tribes of the Coos, Lower Umpqua, and Siuslaw Indians issued a press release on November 15, 2023 calling the withdrawal a positive development and reiterating their long-standing opposition to the plan. The press coverage of the withdrawal was, for a brief window in November 2023, framed as a victory for the opponents of the conversion. The Elliott had been saved, this framing said. OSU had walked away. The Children's Forest was off the table.

That framing was incorrect.

The structural plan continued to advance through different channels. The Elliott State Research Forest Authority continued to exist as a legal entity authorized under HB 3119. The Authority continued to develop operational planning documents. The ESRF Operations Plan Public Review Draft of 2025 — currently in the L0 substrate under `L0_Primary_Sources/Research_Reports/ESRF/ESRF_OpsPlan_PublicReviewDraft_2025.pdf` — shows the planning continuing along the same structural lines after OSU's nominal withdrawal. The State Land Board's working theory had not changed. The Department of State Lands' working theory had not changed. The Legislature's authorization had not been repealed. What had happened, in November 2023, was that one of the institutional participants had withdrawn its name from the operation. The operation continued.

I want to name the pattern explicitly because it is one of the things this book is trying to teach the reader to see. *When one path closes for a state actor pursuing an off-purpose conversion, the state actor pivots to a different path with the same destination.* The 2017 sale was reversed under public pressure. The 2019 bond substitution accomplished a partial conversion through legislative budgeting. The 2022 OSU plan attempted a full conversion through legislation. When OSU withdrew in November 2023, the structural plan continued through the Authority. The destination has been continuous. The mechanisms have varied.

## VIII. The 2022 resistance

I do not want to close this chapter without naming the resistance that the 2022 plan produced, because the resistance is part of the case and the participants are part of the cast.

The Joint Ways and Means subcommittee of the Oregon Legislature heard testimony on SB 1546A — a related bill from the 2022 session that, alongside HB 3119, would have completed the legal architecture for the OSU transfer — and the testimony from the Coos County and Coast school districts was substantial. The school boards of **North Bend**, **Powers**, and **Coos Bay** passed resolutions opposing the transfer. Their reasoning was the reasoning the OASTL litigation eventually built on: the Elliott had been held in trust for their districts' children since 1859, the conversion was taking the asset from those children, and the substitute compensation was inadequate.

The Lars Larson Show, the syndicated Oregon-based conservative talk radio program, picked up the story in February 2022. Bob Zybach introduced me to Larson's audience as a former OSU professor and President of the Oregon Websites and Watersheds Project who had been working full-time on the Elliott for five-plus years. The interview ran. The audience the radio reached was the rural-Oregon timber-country audience that had not been engaged in the 2017 sale controversy, and which the 2022 plan was directly affecting.

A sign campaign ran on the South Coast. The slogan was *Save the Children's Forest* — a slogan the campaign deliberately chose because it inverted the rebrand the plan was using. The plan said *Children's Forest* and meant *research forest under OSU management whose funding stream comes from bond appropriations rather than trust beneficiary distributions.* The campaign said *Save the Children's Forest* and meant *return the asset to its trust status as a working forest for the Common School Fund beneficiaries who own it.* The two phrases used the same words to mean opposite things, which is what is supposed to happen when the framing of a public issue becomes the issue.

The most important single piece of resistance correspondence from the 2024 follow-on period was Representative **Court Boice's open letter** of April 8, 2024, addressed by name to Governor Tina Kotek, State Treasurer Tobias Read, and Secretary of State LaVonne Griffin-Valade — all three members of the State Land Board. Boice, a Republican from Curry County on the southern Oregon coast, called out the Land Board members individually. The letter was the legislative-side witness statement that the structural plan was visible to anyone willing to look, and that legislators on the side of the trust's beneficiaries were prepared to put their names to that observation in writing. The letter is in the L0 substrate under `L0_Primary_Sources/Official_Correspondence/Rep_Boice_Letter_to_Land_Board_2024-04-08.pdf`. It is short and direct. Read it.

The 2022 resistance did not stop the plan. The plan moved forward. The OSU withdrawal in November 2023 looked like a victory for the resistance but turned out to be a withdrawal of a name, not a withdrawal of the operation. The Elliott State Research Forest Authority continued. The planning documents continued to be drafted. The structural conversion the 2019 bond substitution had prepared the ground for, and the 2022 OSU plan had attempted to complete, was not undone by the OSU withdrawal.

What stood, after November 2023, was the documentary record. The OSU $2.1 million admission in OSU's own December 2021 documentation. Franklin's Appendix 13 peer review. Huntington's October 13, 2023 internal memo on the political-not-fiduciary character of the plan. Murthy's November 13, 2023 withdrawal letter. Boice's April 8, 2024 open letter to the three Land Board members by name. The documentary record was what it was. Anyone willing to read it could see what had happened.

What was not yet visible, in the public record, was the documentary record of what the agencies had been doing *during* the public process — the records-process refusals, the secret advisory-committee meetings, the OSU College of Forestry's $660,000 in DSL funding spent during a period of fully secret meetings, the May 27, 2020 Anthony Davis email that asked, with apparent unawareness of how the request would read, whether the Science Advisory Panel could meet in secret to discuss how to go about being public. That documentary record is what Chapter 8 takes up.

The 2022 plan was not stopped by the resistance. The 2022 plan was paused by OSU's nominal withdrawal in November 2023. The plan's structural advance has continued through the Authority since. And the documentary trail of how the plan was developed in the years before its public unveiling — the trail the OASTL litigation is now compelling the State to produce — is the next chapter.
