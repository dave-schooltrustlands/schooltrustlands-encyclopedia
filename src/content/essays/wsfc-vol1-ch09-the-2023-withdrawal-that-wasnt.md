# Chapter 9 — The 2023 Withdrawal That Wasn't

*Who Steals from Children: Volume 1 — How Oregon Is Trying to Steal the Elliott State Forest from the Common School Fund*

---

There are two ways a public agency can tell you it has lost an argument. One is to say so. The other is to walk away from the position it has been holding and to keep, as it walks, the project the position was protecting.

In the fall of 2023, both happened to the Elliott on the same calendar page. On October 13, Geoff Huntington, writing on behalf of the Governor's Office, put in a memo what no senior state lawyer had previously put in writing: that the Elliott State Research Forest had been designed for political, not fiduciary, ends. One month later, on November 13, OSU President Jayathi Murthy sent a letter to the Department of State Lands withdrawing OSU from the project. Together, those two documents looked like the end. They were not the end. The conversion of the Elliott out of the Common School Fund did not pause on November 13, 2023. It found another vehicle.

This chapter is about how that pattern works.

## The Huntington memo

The October 13, 2023 memo from Geoff Huntington at the Governor's Office is, in my reading, the single most consequential internal document of the entire Elliott decoupling era. I am going to describe it carefully because the description matters.

Huntington was, at the time, working on behalf of Governor Tina Kotek's office on the Elliott file. He had been involved in the negotiations between DSL, OSU, and the Governor's Office over the structure of the Elliott State Research Forest. He was, in other words, an insider. The memo he produced is not an outsider's critique. It is an insider's diagnosis.

The memo conceded, in writing, that the design of the Elliott State Research Forest was driven by political accommodation rather than by fiduciary duty to the Common School Fund. It conceded that the financial structure — OSU's own December 2021 estimate of "a $2.1 million deficit on an average annual basis for the first 50 years" — could not be defended on trust grounds. It conceded that the choice to keep the asset off the Common School Fund's productive ledger was a choice made for reasons that had nothing to do with the schoolchildren who are the trust's beneficiaries.

I want to be plain about why this matters. For five years, from 2018 through 2023, every public communication from DSL, the Land Board, and OSU had described the ESRF as a creative solution to the trust's challenges — a way to honor the Common School Fund's interests while pursuing research and conservation values. The Huntington memo is the first internal document in which a senior state lawyer concedes that the public framing was the public framing, and the actual driver was political. The political driver was not, in the memo's frame, illegitimate — political accommodation is what governors do. The problem was that political accommodation cannot be the driver when the asset being accommodated belongs in trust.

A trustee who acts on political grounds for the convenience of the trustee, rather than on fiduciary grounds for the benefit of the beneficiaries, has breached the duty of loyalty. The Restatement (Third) of Trusts states the duty in one sentence: a trustee must administer the trust solely in the interest of the beneficiaries. The Huntington memo, read alongside the financial structure it described, is not a description of the duty of loyalty being honored. It is a description of the duty of loyalty being suspended.

The memo never became public in the sense of being posted to a state website. It surfaced through the slow grind of public-records work and litigation discovery. But it exists. It is in the documentary record. And it stands as the State's own acknowledgement, in the voice of its own Governor's Office lawyer, that the conversion of the Elliott was not what the public was being told it was.

## The Murthy withdrawal letter

One month later, on November 13, 2023, OSU President Jayathi Murthy sent a letter to DSL announcing that OSU was withdrawing from the Elliott State Research Forest project.

Murthy's letter was brief, formal, and unambiguous. It did not cite the Huntington memo — university presidents do not, as a rule, cite the Governor's Office's internal memoranda in their formal correspondence. But the timing leaves little room for coincidence. One month after a senior Governor's Office lawyer puts in writing that the ESRF was designed for political not fiduciary ends, OSU's president walks away from the deal.

I have spent thirty-three years inside OSU as a faculty member and administrator. I know how the university makes decisions of this scale. A withdrawal letter from a sitting president on a project of this size, with this much institutional investment — OSU had been paid $660,000 by DSL to develop the original ESRF proposal — is not signed lightly. It is signed when the president's legal counsel and the president's external relations team have advised that the institutional risk of staying in the project exceeds the institutional cost of leaving. The Huntington memo, circulating at exactly the moment Murthy's office would have been weighing whether to continue, was the kind of document that converts a manageable risk into an unmanageable one. OSU did not want to be the public face of a project that the Governor's own office was conceding had been built on the wrong fiduciary foundation.

The Confederated Tribes of Coos, Lower Umpqua, and Siuslaw Indians issued a press release two days later, on November 15, opposing the ESRF on independent grounds. Coastal-county Republican legislators issued their own statements. The political coalition that had been wavering against the ESRF began to consolidate. Margaret Bird's December 2022 letter to the Land Board — her "decoupling is a euphemism for selling" warning — had been on the record for nearly a year. Bill Lansing's $1-billion-plus fair-market estimate had been published. Bob Zybach's declaration on the underlying management failures had been filed in my 2021 case.

The ESRF, as a public project, was over.

But the ESRF was never the point. The ESRF was a vehicle. The point was the conversion of the Elliott out of the Common School Fund.

## What changed; what didn't

On November 13, 2023, what changed was the surface vehicle. OSU was out. The "research forest" framing, as a freestanding public-facing project, no longer had a university partner. The Land Board could no longer announce, in the language of academic stewardship, that the Elliott would be transferred to a research mission.

What did not change was the underlying institutional commitment.

The State Land Board, the Department of State Lands, the Treasurer's office, and the Governor's office had spent six years building toward the conclusion that the Common School Fund's claim on the Elliott was an inconvenience to be dissolved. The institutional commitment was not a position any individual official had taken. It was a posture the institution had developed. It was reflected in the 2017 sale attempt that was reversed under public pressure; in the 2019 bond substitution that turned the trust's claim on a working asset into a claim on a budget line item; in the 2020-and-after secret meetings; in the redacted Advisory Committee; in the State's silence on records requests; and in the ESRF rollout itself. Murthy's withdrawal removed the public face. It did not remove the institutional commitment underneath.

When an institutional commitment loses one vehicle, an institution committed to that commitment finds another. That is what happened next.

## SB 147

On June 25, 2025 — nineteen months after Murthy's withdrawal — the Oregon legislature passed Senate Bill 147. SB 147 decoupled the Elliott from the Common School Fund through a statutory mechanism that did not depend on OSU, did not require a research-forest framing, and did not produce the kind of public-coalition opposition that had killed the ESRF.

The mechanism was technical, which is part of why it worked. SB 147 repealed ORS 530.450 — the statutory provision that had governed the Elliott's status as a Common School Fund asset since the 1930s. The repeal, in combination with the bond structure of 2019 and the administrative posture DSL had taken since 2020, completed in statute what the ESRF had been intended to complete in practice. The Common School Fund's claim on the Elliott as a particular asset was severed; the Common School Fund's right to revenue from the Elliott was converted into something else; the trustee's discretion to manage the Elliott as something other than a school-trust timber asset was confirmed.

I wrote to Natalie Scott about SB 147 a few days after the bill passed. My note to her, on June 25, 2025, said this:

> *I particularly like Section 1 about Senate Bill 147. I believe a strong case can be made that the sale didn't occur until the Governor/legislature repealed ORS 530.450.*

That sentence is, in retrospect, the most important sentence I have written in this case. It is the sentence that names what SB 147 was. SB 147 was the legal completion of a sale that had been engineered, in administrative form, by every step since 2017. The State has, throughout this litigation, taken the position that no "sale" of the Elliott ever occurred — that the Elliott remains, technically, State property, and that the trust's beneficiaries therefore have no standing to complain about a transaction that did not happen. That position depends on the reader of statutes ignoring what statutes do. A sale, in trust-law terms, does not require the exchange of a deed. A sale requires the conversion of a beneficiary's interest in a particular asset into something less, in exchange for value flowing somewhere else. SB 147 did exactly that.

The strategic effect of SB 147, from the State's perspective, was to do what the ESRF would have done — sever the Elliott from the Common School Fund — without the public-facing structure that had killed the ESRF. There was no Anthony Davis email. There was no redacted Advisory Committee. There was no $660,000 DSL-funded research proposal. There was a bill. The bill was passed through the normal legislative process. The bill was signed. The bill became law. And the Common School Fund's claim on the Elliott, which the ESRF would have severed administratively, was severed statutorily instead.

## The state-actor pivot

What I want the reader to understand about this chapter is a pattern that I think is general — a pattern that applies not only to the Elliott but to every long-running attempt by a State to convert a trust asset to a non-trust purpose. I will call it the state-actor pivot.

A state agency or a state coalition that has decided, internally, to advance a particular conversion does not stake the conversion on a single vehicle. It develops a portfolio. The administrative vehicle (the ESRF) and the financial vehicle (the 2019 bond substitution) and the legislative vehicle (SB 147) are not three separate projects. They are three implementations of one institutional decision. When public pressure kills the administrative vehicle — as it did in November 2023 — the financial vehicle is already in place and the legislative vehicle is already being drafted. The institution is committed at a level deeper than any individual vehicle, and the institution will pivot.

This is why reputational pressure on individual officials, while sometimes necessary, is not sufficient. Murthy's withdrawal was a reputational victory. It was a real victory; OSU's name was off the project; the public-facing framing was disabled. But the conversion did not reverse. The institutional commitment behind the conversion did not reverse. The State Land Board did not, in November 2023, decide that the Common School Fund's claim on the Elliott was a fiduciary commitment to be honored. The State Land Board decided that one of its vehicles had failed and another would be needed.

Eighteen months later, the other vehicle arrived.

The diagnostic test for whether you are dealing with an individual-official problem or an institutional-commitment problem is what I just described. If you remove the individual official and the project disappears, you were dealing with an individual-official problem. If you remove the individual official and the project reappears in a different form, you were dealing with an institutional-commitment problem.

The Elliott decoupling is an institutional-commitment problem.

## Why this matters for the litigation

OASTL's 2024 case in Coos County is the litigation that survives because it understands what the 2023 withdrawal was not. Daniel Crowe and the team did not treat Murthy's withdrawal as the end of the conversion threat. They treated it as a public-facing event whose underlying institutional commitment remained. They built the 2024 case — *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon*, case number 24CV38372 — to address the institutional commitment, not the surface vehicle.

The complaint in 24CV38372 names the State of Oregon as the defendant. It does not name OSU. It does not name the ESRF as a freestanding project. It names the trustee. It alleges breach of trust against the trustee. It seeks an accounting from the trustee. The complaint is built to be true regardless of which particular vehicle the State chooses to deploy, because the breach is in the State's posture toward the trust, not in any single transaction.

This is what made the 2026 standing victory at the Court of Appeals so important. The court's January 28, 2026 opinion granted Article VIII Section 8 standing to the schoolchildren and to OASTL on behalf of them. Standing is not a one-transaction concept. Standing is the legal authority to require the trustee to account, period. Once the schoolchildren-plaintiffs have standing, they can ask the trustee about the 2017 sale attempt, about the 2019 bond substitution, about the 2020 secret meetings, about the 2022 ESRF rollout, about the 2023 Huntington memo, about the 2025 SB 147 statutory completion of the conversion, and about whatever vehicle the State deploys next. The standing victory is not a victory about one bad transaction. It is a victory about the trustee's continuing obligation to deliberate, on the record, with the beneficiaries' interests as the only legitimate consideration.

OSU walked away in November 2023. The conversion did not.

That is the lesson of this chapter and the one I want the reader to carry forward. Reform of an institutionally-committed conversion does not arrive through the resignation of any single official. It arrives through the establishment of a continuing oversight structure that an institutional commitment cannot circumvent. In the trust-law tradition, that continuing oversight structure has a name: it is called a court. The 2024 case is the schoolchildren-plaintiffs' first organized appearance, in 167 years, inside the institutional architecture that the Admissions Act of 1859 and Article VIII of the Oregon Constitution had quietly assumed would always exist.

How they got there — how an organized beneficiary constituency finally appeared in the courtroom and asserted the trust against the State as trustee — is the subject of the next chapter.
