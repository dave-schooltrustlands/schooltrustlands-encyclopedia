# Chapter 4 — The 1990s Capture

*Working draft v1, 2026-05-10. ~3,000 words target. Dave-first-person body, named actors, dated events, dollar figures. The chapter where the book names *capture* in Margaret Bird's working vocabulary.*

---

I want to walk you through something you have to see before the rest of the book makes sense.

Between roughly 1990 and 2017, the people running the Elliott State Forest stopped seeing it as the Common School Fund's working asset and started seeing it as a problem to be solved. Nothing in the constitution changed. The 1859 Admissions Act did not change. Article VIII, Sections 2, 5, and 8 of Oregon's constitution did not change. The trustees still took the same oath. The forest still had the same trees. And yet, decade by decade and decision by decision, the Elliott was reframed inside the State Land Board and the Department of State Lands from a long-term beneficiary asset into a net drain on agency continuity — until by the mid-2010s the Board's working theory was that the trust would be better served by selling the forest and reinvesting the proceeds in financial assets the agency itself was easier to administer.

That reframe is what Margaret Bird calls *capture*. And Oregon between 1990 and 2017 is the cleanest documented case of capture in the country.

I am going to use the word *capture* deliberately and I want to be careful about what I mean by it. I do not mean that the Land Board members of the 1990s and 2000s sat down in a room and decided to betray Oregon's schoolchildren. I do not mean that the DSL staff who drafted the post-1990 management plans went home from work thinking they had stolen anything. I am claiming something subtler than that, and harder to fight. I am claiming that the room itself drifted — that the institution's understanding of who the Elliott was *for* moved, one defensible decision at a time, until the trust's beneficiaries were no longer the people in the room and the people in the room had institutional interests other than maximizing common-school funding.

Drift-by-capture is the hardest form of trust failure to oppose, because no individual decision in the chain looks like betrayal — and Oregon between 1990 and 2017 is the cleanest documented case of capture in the country.

Here is how it happened.

## I. The architectural starting point, 1990

In 1990, the Elliott State Forest was the workhorse asset of Oregon's Common School Fund. Ninety-three thousand acres of Coast Range Douglas-fir, managed by the Oregon Department of Forestry under contract with the Department of State Lands, generating reliable annual timber revenue for the irreducible trust corpus that Article VIII, Section 2 of the Oregon Constitution declared "separate and irreducible." The forest had carried the trust through the New Deal and through the 1962 Columbus Day blow-down. Jerry Phillips, who managed the Coos Bay District for the Oregon Department of Forestry from 1956 to 1989 and wrote the canonical Elliott history *Caulked Boots and Cheese Sandwiches*, lived through the period when the architecture mostly held. That period ended on his watch.

The 1977 Attorney General Opinion 7450 — which I want to put on the table at the start of this chapter because it disposes of a common defense before the defense is offered — ruled on whether income-producing Common School Grant Land could be designated as a Natural Area Preserve under ORS 273.562. The AG answered yes, but only *"when such designation would adversely affect the financial contribution of such land to the Common School Trust Fund"* — meaning, in operational terms, no. Off-purpose use of the Elliott was prohibited where it adversely affected the trust's revenue. That doctrine was the 1977 floor. It has never been amended. It was simply bypassed.

Hold the 1977 floor in mind. We are going to come back to it.

## II. The ESA decade, 1990–2000

The first thing that happened was the spotted owl.

The northern spotted owl was listed as a threatened species under the federal Endangered Species Act on June 22, 1990. The marbled murrelet was listed in October 1992. Both species are obligate users of the kind of mature Douglas-fir habitat the Elliott had been growing for sixty years. The federal listings carried with them substantial restrictions on harvest activity in occupied habitat.

The Department of State Lands' fiduciary obligation, under both the constitution and the 1977 AG opinion, was to manage the Elliott for the benefit of the Common School Fund. The federal listings did not change that obligation. They changed the operational landscape inside which the obligation had to be discharged. A trustee whose corpus is constrained by external regulation does not get to walk away from the corpus; the trustee is supposed to manage *around* the constraint as competently as possible.

What actually happened was different.

Between 1992 and the mid-2000s, the Department of State Lands and the Department of Forestry undertook a series of habitat conservation planning efforts intended to put the Elliott on a federally approved no-incidental-take footing. The first HCP, finalized in 1995, set aside large blocks of the forest as conservation reserves. Harvestable acreage went down. Annual revenue went down. And critically — and this is where the institutional reframe begins — the agencies' internal vocabulary for the Elliott began to shift. The forest stopped being described inside DSL as "the Common School Fund's working timber asset." It started being described as "the Elliott," as if the forest itself had become a noun whose purpose was a subject of debate rather than a constitutional pre-commitment.

A small thing. But the language matters. When the people in the room stop calling an asset by what it is *for*, they have stopped treating the *for* as a constraint that binds them.

## III. The second decade, 2000–2010

By the mid-2000s, the operational story had a new feature. The HCP's reserve set-asides plus litigation pressure from environmental groups had reduced the Elliott's harvestable acreage to a fraction of its former working area. Annual revenue to the Common School Fund had fallen accordingly. And the political costs of the timber harvest that did remain — protests, lawsuits, press attention — had begun to outrun the school revenue inside the agencies' own accounting of what the Elliott was costing them in attention and political capital.

I am going to give you a number that almost no one in Oregon has heard. Bob Zybach, who earned his OSU PhD on Coast Range fire history in 2003 and has been the Elliott's most patient scholarly observer, put the number on paper in his 2018 *Daily Caller* op-ed, weeks before the Camp Fire destroyed Paradise, California:

> *"You take away logging, grazing and maintenance, and you get firebombs."*

Read that as a fire-ecology statement and it is a warning about the 2018 Camp Fire and about the 2020 Labor Day fires that swept Oregon two years later. Read it as a fiduciary statement and it is a description of what the post-1990 Elliott was becoming on the Department of State Lands' watch. The forest the Common School Fund actually owned in 2010 was not the same forest it had owned in 1990. Less of it was being actively managed. More of it was accumulating ground fuels and standing dead. The asset, in the trustee's hands, was being permitted to deteriorate.

Zybach said this directly in his July 11, 2021 sworn declaration in Sullivan v. Oregon (21CV15024). He named, with the specificity of a forester who had walked the ground for thirty years, what the agencies were not doing — the maintenance roads they were not maintaining, the lookout towers they were letting rot, the pre-commercial thinning they were not undertaking, the fire breaks they were not refreshing. He named the comparison: from 1952 until 1987, only one forest fire in all of western Oregon was greater than ten thousand acres. Since 1987, more than thirty such fires. Several over a hundred thousand acres. The pattern was recent. It was caused. And the Elliott was on the trajectory the pattern described.

A fiduciary watching the corpus deteriorate has a duty to act. The 1990–2010 record shows the Department of State Lands not acting — and, more troublingly, beginning to construct the institutional case for why action was no longer worth attempting.

## IV. The institutional reframe

This is the hinge of the chapter. Stay with me through the reasoning, because what I am about to describe is what *capture* means in operational practice and why it is so hard to oppose.

By approximately 2010 to 2012, the working position inside the Department of State Lands and on the State Land Board could be summarized roughly as follows. The Elliott has become more expensive to operate as a timber asset than it generates in revenue. The endangered-species constraints are not going away. The political costs of harvest have continued to rise. The agency's accounting shows the Elliott as a net drain on the Common School Fund — meaning the trust is *losing money* by continuing to hold the asset. Therefore, the trust would be better served by selling the forest and reinvesting the proceeds in financial assets that produce stable returns without the operational and political overhead.

Read on its own terms, that argument is defensible. It is the kind of reasoning that any administrator under pressure would arrive at. It looks, inside the agency frame, like fiduciary diligence. The Land Board members of that period were not fools and were not, individually, acting in bad faith. They were looking at the numbers their staff put in front of them and reaching the conclusion the numbers seemed to point toward.

But step outside the room and the picture is different. Three things change.

*First.* The "net drain" calculation depended on accepting as fixed a set of operational constraints — reduced harvest, increased reserves, higher administrative costs — that were partly the product of the agencies' own choices in the 1990s and 2000s. The Department of State Lands did not actively challenge environmental litigation in the way a private timber owner with a billion-dollar working asset would have. It did not pursue alternative management approaches Zybach and others had repeatedly proposed. It did not pursue the active forest management that would have reduced both the political costs and the fire risk. The "drain" was, to a substantial extent, a drain the trustees themselves had permitted to develop. Citing the drain as the reason to dispose of the asset is the move of a shopper who breaks merchandise on the floor and then takes it to the checkout counter and asks for a discount — language I used on the original ElliottSecrets website in 2020, and language I am using again here because the analogy is exact.

*Second.* The 1977 AG Opinion 7450 said off-purpose use of the Elliott was prohibited where it adversely affected the financial contribution to the trust. The 2010s reframe did not amend the 1977 doctrine. It bypassed the doctrine by reclassifying the trust's relationship to the asset. If the forest is a "drain," the argument went, then disposing of it *helps* the financial contribution rather than harming it. The doctrine is technically respected; the substance is reversed. This is the move that Oregon's own Attorney General had ruled out in 1977 — not because the language tracks, but because the structural effect is the same. The trust's working asset was being prepared for conversion under the cover of a defensible operational logic.

*Third.* The room in which the conversion was being prepared had filled, over twenty years, with people whose institutional interests were not the trust's interests. The State Land Board itself — Governor, Secretary of State, Treasurer — sits as trustees because the Oregon Constitution puts them there, but the trustees rarely if ever do the analytic work themselves; they take the staff recommendations the Department of State Lands brings them. The DSL staff are state employees whose institutional incentives include reducing political controversy, simplifying their own administrative load, and retaining good relations with the environmental and forestry communities that produce the litigation pressure. The Department of Forestry, which manages the Elliott under contract, has its own incentives that do not perfectly align with maximizing CSF revenue. The OSU College of Forestry, whose involvement deepens through the 2010s, has academic interests in the kind of research-forest framing that is incompatible with active sustained-yield harvest. Environmental advocates, with whom the agencies negotiate continuously, have preferences that pull toward larger reserves. Legislators, whose authority is implicated when the trust's revenue falls short and the general fund has to make up the difference, have a continuing interest in stabilizing the forest's drain on the budget. Each of these constituencies has a defensible reason to participate in the Elliott's management. None of them is the trust's beneficiary. None of them is a schoolchild. None of them, in 2010 or 2015, was represented by an organized advocate inside the room.

Margaret Bird's term for what is in the room when this configuration obtains is *the trustee's coalition*. Her vocabulary for what is not in the room is *the beneficiary's seat*. Capture happens when the trustee's coalition fills out and the beneficiary's seat is empty.

## V. The 1977 doctrine, again

I want to come back to the 1977 AG Opinion 7450 because its bypassing is the technical heart of what makes the 1990–2017 period a capture rather than a policy disagreement.

The 1977 doctrine settled, with reasoning that has not been overturned, that off-purpose use of Common School Grant Land was prohibited where the off-purpose use adversely affected the financial contribution of the land to the trust fund. The doctrine had teeth. It said that designating a piece of CSF land as a Natural Area Preserve — or, by parallel reasoning, as a research forest, or as a habitat reserve — could not be done if the designation reduced the land's revenue to the schools.

What the 1977 doctrine assumed, and did not name, was that the agencies enforcing it would treat the financial contribution as something to be maximized within reasonable operational constraints. The doctrine did not anticipate a future in which the agencies would themselves *redefine the financial contribution* by accepting reduced harvest as a fixed input, then point to the resulting low contribution as a justification for off-purpose use.

That is what happened. By the time the State Land Board began voting in the 2010s on Elliott decoupling, alternative-management proposals, and eventually the 2017 sale, the working theory inside the agencies was that the Elliott's contribution had fallen so low that any disposition that produced even modest revenue was a fiduciary improvement. The 1977 doctrine, which would have required the agencies to *first* defend the contribution and *only then* consider off-purpose use, was inverted. Off-purpose use was treated as the rescue, not as the breach.

The doctrine was not amended. It was never put before the legislature. It was never argued in front of a court. The agencies simply moved past it.

This is the operational signature of capture. The legal architecture sits unchanged on the books while the institutional behavior on the ground has rotated by ninety degrees. A reader who walks into the Department of State Lands in 2015 and asks about the 1977 AG opinion gets a respectful answer about how the doctrine still applies. A reader who watches the actual decisions — the management plans, the harvest reductions, the sale exploration, the OSU partnership — sees a different agency than the doctrine assumes.

## VI. The aggregate effect, viewed from outside

Aggregate twenty-five years of institutional reframing and you get the structural picture.

An asset reserved in 1859 for common schools — for the benefit of "the children of the people of this State and their descendants forever," in the trust language Article VIII, Section 2 of Oregon's constitution carries — became, between 1990 and the mid-2010s, an asset whose use was decided by a coalition of state agencies, university administrators, environmental advocates, and legislators. None of whom were the trust's beneficiaries. Most of whom had institutional interests other than maximizing common-school funding. Some of whom were, by 2017, openly advocating for converting the Elliott into something other than a CSF working asset.

The Common School Fund, in this period, had no advocate. The Oregon State Board of Education was not in the room. The school districts were not in the room. The parents were not in the room. The schoolchildren — the named beneficiaries of the trust — had no constitutional standing the agencies were treating as enforceable. (That standing would not be granted by an Oregon court until the January 28, 2026 Court of Appeals opinion this book takes up in Chapter 10. For the entire 1990–2017 period, the agencies operated as if the beneficiary class had no judicial recourse — and, as a matter of black-letter law, the agencies were correct.)

The forest still belonged to the trust. The architecture still said so. The room in which decisions about the forest were made had simply ceased to be a room in which the trust was represented.

## VII. What the reframe was not

I want to take one common defense off the table before we close this chapter.

The Land Board members of the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s — Governors Roberts, Kitzhaber, Kulongoski, Brown, and Kotek; Secretaries of State Keisling, Bradbury, Brown, Atkins, Richardson, Clarno, Fagan, and Read; Treasurers Hill, Edwards, Wheeler, and Read — were not, individually, acting in bad faith. I do not know any of them well enough to make personal claims about their motives, and I do not need to. The capture argument does not require bad faith. It requires only that the institutional process by which decisions reach the Board has been structured so that the trust's beneficiaries are not represented in it. The Board members are voting on staff packages. The staff packages have been built by people whose interests are not the trust's interests. The Board approves what looks like a defensible package and the trust drifts.

That is the structural diagnosis. Whether any individual trustee, in any individual moment, would have voted differently if she had been better informed is a question I do not need to answer. What I need you to see is that the *room* — the institutional configuration in which the Land Board was making decisions about the Elliott from approximately 1990 to 2017 — had drifted out from under the trust. The trust was being administered by a coalition that did not include its beneficiaries.

## VIII. Toward 2017

The 2017 sale-and-reverse, which is the subject of the next chapter, is the moment when the capture broke into open view. The State Land Board, on February 14, 2017, voted to sell the Elliott to a Lone Rock-led consortium for $220.8 million. Three months later, under sustained public pressure, the Board reversed. The reversal is, in conventional Oregon political memory, remembered as a "win" — schoolchildren's land saved from a private buyer.

What I want you to be ready to see in Chapter 5 is that the reversal did not undo the capture. The reasoning inside the room — that the Elliott had become a net drain, that the trust would be better served by conversion, that the 1977 doctrine no longer constrained operational decisions — was not retracted in May 2017. The Board did not say *we were wrong to think the trust is better served by selling*. The Board said *the political pressure makes a sale infeasible at this time*. Different sentence. Different posture. Same underlying reframe.

The 2017 sale was reversed. The 2017 *reasoning* behind the sale was not.

That is the seam through which the 2019 bond substitution, the 2022 OSU plan, the 2023 secret meetings, and every subsequent move could continue forward without contradicting the May 2017 reversal. The capture had been exposed. The capture had not been remedied.

I want you to keep that distinction in mind as we go to the next chapter. The Elliott was not saved in May 2017. The sale of the Elliott was deferred. There is a difference. The room had not changed. The beneficiary's seat was still empty. And the institutional commitment to convert the trust's working asset into something the trustee's coalition was easier with had not been reversed. It had only been told, by a public that for one moment was paying attention, that *that particular conversion mechanism* would not be tolerated.

The next mechanism was already being prepared.
