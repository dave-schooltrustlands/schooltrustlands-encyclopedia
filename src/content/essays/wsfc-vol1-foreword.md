# Foreword

I have spent the last twenty-three years inside the architecture of America's school trust lands. I came to this work as an economist, by way of state-government fiscal analysis, and I stayed in it because the architecture itself is one of the most extraordinary fiduciary experiments any country has ever attempted — a forever gift, from the founding generation to the unborn, of a piece of land in every township dedicated to the maintenance of the public schools. I have read the admission acts. I have read the trust accountings. I have sat in trustee meetings in nearly every public-land state in the country. I am, by training and by long habit, slow to call something a theft. I want the reader to register that before I say what I am about to say.

In 2016 the Oregon School Boards Association asked me, for a press release, to compare Oregon's school-trust record to the broader national record. I gave them the sentence I want this foreword to rest on. I said: *In the 235-year history of school trust lands that I have been studying for the last 23 years, no state has spent more than they earned like Oregon has. It never has happened.*

I meant the sentence literally. I had not been making rhetorical points; I had been doing arithmetic. A school trust is supposed to grow. The corpus is supposed to be preserved. The income is supposed to flow to the schools. Across the public-land states there is enormous variation in how well trustees have done this — some states have built their permanent funds into the billions; others have watched their corpus erode through inattention or inflation. But before Oregon, in two and a third centuries of the experiment running, no state had managed to *spend more than it had earned* on its trust lands. That is what Oregon did. It was, in the literal accounting sense, unprecedented. Ten years later, I am more certain than I was then.

Dave Sullivan asked me to write this foreword because he and I have been collaborators for years and because my name on the front of the book signals to the school-trust community that what he documents in the body is consistent with what the rest of us, working inside this architecture across the country, have been watching Oregon do. I am giving that signal deliberately. I have read his manuscript, his cases, and the State's filings. I have watched the people he names make the decisions he describes them making. The indictment Dave lays out in this book is, in my judgment, correct. It is conservatively stated. It is supported by the documentary record. And it describes, in the particulars, a pattern I have seen in outline in other states and have never seen anyone else describe as openly or as completely.

I want to tell the reader, briefly, what makes Oregon's case clarifying for the school-trust field.

First, what *decoupling* means. For the last decade, the State of Oregon has used the word *decoupling* to describe what it has been doing to the Elliott — separating the forest from the Common School Fund's beneficial interest, transferring it to a research-forest arrangement, compensating the fund with bonds. The word is bureaucratic, polite, abstract.

In a letter I wrote to the State Land Board on December 11, 2022, the day before the Board's vote on the so-called *investment value* sale, I told the Board what the word meant. I wrote: *decoupling is a euphemism for selling*. A trustee that takes the trust's primary working asset, transfers it to an outside arrangement at a price the trustee itself has chosen, and substitutes a stream of general-fund bonds for the asset's earnings has *sold the asset*. The bookkeeping is more elaborate than a normal sale; the doctrine is the same. The receivables that were substituted for the asset are an obligation of the state's general fund, not of the trust corpus. That is the difference between owning a piece of property and owning a promise from a politician.

The Board voted anyway. Twelve months later the architect of the receiving arrangement, OSU, withdrew. The structural plan kept moving through different channels. The pattern Dave documents in the chapters that follow is what happens when a fiduciary process keeps advancing after the architecture justifying it has collapsed — when the *transfer* is the object, and the rationale is a story the trustee tells itself.

The second thing to register is how unusual the *combination* of failures in Oregon is. I have seen trustees make poor investment decisions, miscalibrate harvest rotations, over-rely on a single asset class, fail to account properly to their beneficiaries. Each of those things is, separately, recoverable. The trust survives the mistake; the next generation of trustees does better; the corpus is rebuilt. What Oregon has done is different. Oregon has stacked the failures. The asset was managed down. The losses were treated as a justification for *removing the asset from the trust altogether*. The removal was priced by an appraisal the trustee selected. The compensation was substituted from a source — general-fund bonds — that the trustee itself controls and can stop paying at any future legislative session. The trustee's own counsel, when sued by schoolchildren, filed a formal pleading saying the trustee *"does not owe Plaintiffs the duties alleged."* I have not, in twenty-three years, seen a state make that denial in a court of law. It is the sentence that, more than any other, told me Dave was right that this had to be written down now, while the record was still open.

The third thing to register is what is at stake doctrinally — not just for Oregon's schoolchildren, who lose what is being taken — but for the school-trust architecture itself across the country. The trust mechanism the Confederation Congress wrote into the Northwest Ordinance in 1787 and that every public-land state inherited at admission has, in most places, held across two centuries. It has held because the doctrine has held: the trustee owes duties; the duties are enforceable; the beneficiary class — children — has, in principle, standing to assert them. Oregon's defense in the lawsuit Dave's organization filed is, in plain language, that none of those propositions is true. If Oregon's defense succeeds, the doctrine that has held the school-trust architecture together since 1787 is broken not just in Oregon but everywhere it is treated as persuasive authority. If Oregon's defense fails — as I expect it will, and as the Court of Appeals' January 28, 2026 standing ruling already suggests it will — the doctrine is strengthened by the very fact of its having been tested.

That is why this book matters beyond Oregon. Other states are watching. What Oregon's State Land Board has tried to do is something that, if it were allowed to stand, would be tried elsewhere within a decade.

I do not put my name on books lightly, and the reader has a right to know what I am vouching for. I am not vouching for every adjective. Dave writes the way he talks — plainly, sometimes sharply, with the confidence of a man who has been in this fight long enough to know what he is looking at. I have edited his text where I thought it overstated. He has accepted those edits. Where he has not, it is because the documentary record was stronger than I had remembered. I am vouching for the body of the case — the dates, the dollar figures, the named actors, and the fact that the decisions described in this book were made by those actors at the times the book says they made them. I have read the underlying documents. They support the text.

I am also vouching, more importantly, for the conclusion. In the 235-year history of school trust lands, no state has done what Oregon is doing. The Elliott is, by the State's own appraisal, worth $99.6 million; by every independent assessment I have seen, worth ten times that. The schoolchildren of Oregon are the beneficiaries of a trust that, on paper, still exists. In practice, the trustee has been removing the asset from the trust for a decade and arguing in court that it has no duty to do otherwise. That is the indictment. Dave has documented it. I have checked it. The reader can judge.

Before turning the page to Dave's preface, the framing I think most clarifies what you are about to read.

A school trust is a forever gift to forever schools for a forever democracy. The trustees of that gift are the officials in whose hands the corpus rests at any moment. They did not write the trust; they inherited it. They will hand it forward. Their fiduciary duty runs to children they will never meet, in classrooms not yet built. The only question that matters across the generations is whether each generation of trustees handed the corpus forward at least as intact as they received it.

Oregon's current trustees have not. That is the finding of this book. I concur in it.

The work of the next generation — of which Dave Sullivan is one, his lead counsel Daniel Zene Crowe another, and the schoolchildren named as plaintiffs in *Advocates for School Trust Lands v. State of Oregon* the rest — is to stop the loss and restore what can be restored. The work after that is to make sure what has happened in Oregon does not happen in the next state, and the next.

This book is the field's working document for both tasks.

— Margaret Bird, Salt Lake City, 2026.
